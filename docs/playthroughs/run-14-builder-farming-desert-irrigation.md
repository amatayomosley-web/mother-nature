# Run 14 — Builder, Desert, Late-Winter-to-Spring Irrigation Exploration

Date of run: 2026-05-10
Status: Exploratory scoping run. **Farming is NOT currently committed to Mother Nature scope.** This run is a design exercise to surface what desert farming would need, what it would break, and what alternative-design options exist before any commitment.
Run author: William + Claude (design exercise)

> **Exploratory caveats** — Treat magnitudes (cistern volumes, channel grade tolerances, crop calorie yields, dig-rates, harvest windows) as proposals for surfacing the *shape* of the problem, not as committed numbers. Several systems referenced (irrigation infrastructure, soil amendment, plant-growth modeling, flash-flood modeling) are not in any locked design section. The run's purpose is to ask the right questions, not answer them.

## Phase 1 — Days 1-6, the water survey and the storage decision

### Beat 01 — Day 1, the saliency hit and the math problem

Vasco arrives at the dry-wash mouth with the sun already climbing. He spends the first hour standing still and reading the land. **§14.13 saliency** at Mason-Earthworks-Expert plus Survivalist-desert-Practiced produces a layered read no single-node character would surface:

- The **dry-wash bed itself** reads as a gravity-fed channel pre-cut by water — Earthworks-Expert sees grade, sees the meander cuts, sees where the wash has scoured deeper through soft sand and where it sheets out across compacted gravel. The wash *is* his channel; he is not digging it from scratch, he is *modifying* it.
- **Caliche layer** outcrops in two places along the wash, ~30cm below the sandy surface — Mason-Expert sees a free supply of cement-hard mineral that can be quarried with the pickaxe, broken into rubble, and used for mortar bed and cistern lining.
- **Clay streak** in the wash bank ~150m up-canyon — Earthworks-Expert sees this as cistern-lining material. Clay puddled and tamped is the standard pre-industrial pond-liner.
- A **shade pocket** on the east bank where a low overhang of caliche gives a 2m cool zone for ~4 hours a day — Survivalist-desert reads this as the camp site, not the build site.
- **Three xeriscape signals** within the first 200m: a stand of mesquite (Forager: pods in ~late spring, root system probably 10m deep, can drink at depths he can't reach), a prickly pear cluster (water reservoir in pads, edible fruit later), an agave (slow-growing, multi-year, mature plant is a calorie + water store).
- The **spring location** he was told about — he hikes the 600m up-canyon. **The spring is at his feet within ~10 minutes** — Earthworks-saliency reads the sub-surface flow: a damp patch in the wash, sedge-equivalent green where everything else is brown, a slight darkening of the sand. He digs with the trowel. **Water seeps in at ~50cm depth.** Estimated flow: dribble — perhaps 5-15 liters/hour, and that's assuming the recent rain hasn't drained out yet.

**The first day's central finding**: the spring is real but the flow is *much* less than his project plan assumed. **His 30m pipe + 600m of wash is a 600m problem with a 30m solution.** He sits with this for an hour.

The **water math** writes itself:

- Vasco's daily water need at desert ambient: ~4L drinking + ~1L cooking/cleaning = ~5L. (His 10L bag is 2 days survival reserve.)
- Spring output (estimate, unverified): 50-200L/day if it flows steadily; perhaps zero in a dry week.
- Crop water need (estimate): tepary bean is *drought champion* and needs ~15-25mm rain equivalent per week during establishment; a 2m × 4m plot at 20mm/week = ~160L/week = ~23L/day. For one plot.
- Four plots = ~90L/day during establishment.
- Plus his own ~5L.
- = ~95L/day, against a spring output of 50-200L/day if the spring holds.

**The margin is razor-thin and weather-dependent.** A two-week dry stretch with no spring inflow would kill the plots and threaten his survival reserve simultaneously.

**Gaps surfaced:**

- N65 — **Intermittent spring as a stochastic water source** — current weather-grid (§3.3) models pressure systems, but desert spring flow depends on aquifer recharge from prior rain weeks ago; needs a separate sub-surface water model
- N66 — **Crop water demand spec** — Mother Nature has no agronomy model; without it, "did the crop get enough water" is undefined
- N67 — **Storage capacity vs construction time vs lining quality** as a Builder/Earthworks design problem; analogous to Run 06's Day-4 trade-off matrix but on water rather than walls

### Beat 02 — Days 2-4, the cistern decision

Vasco picks his storage location: at the dry-wash bottom near his camp, where the wash widens and the caliche layer is thick. Plan: pit dug into the clay-streak band ~3m × 3m × 1.5m deep, lined with caliche-mortar (his own recipe: crushed caliche + clay slurry + water, troweled onto a tamped subgrade), covered with a sun-cloth + brush mat to reduce evaporation.

**Estimated capacity**: ~13,500L if filled, but realistically he'll fill it ~30% from the spring at the slow pace, and evaporation losses in desert summer can run 5-10mm/day off open water. Covered, maybe 2-3mm/day — still significant on a 9m² surface (~25L/day evaporation alone, covered).

**The construction effort** — at his skill profile:

- **Digging**: sand for the first 30cm (fast), then clay-streak band (slow, sticky, ~1 day per 30cm depth at his rate), then caliche bottom (pickaxe work, ~2 days for the final 30cm and the bottom flat). **Total: ~6-8 days of digging.**
- **Lining**: caliche-mortar mixed, troweled, smoothed, allowed to set ~24 hours per coat, two coats minimum. **~3-4 days lining.**
- **Cover frame**: sun-cloth + brush mat over a willow-frame, ~1 day.
- **Total cistern build: ~10-13 days.** Mason-Expert + Earthworks-Expert means he's at the upper end of his potential pace; a Carpenter-flavored Builder would be slower.

**The decision he is actually facing**: build the cistern first (Days 1-12) and *then* plant + *then* dig the channel? Plant first and accept that the spring + 10L bag has to carry him? Build channel first because it's a 30-day project?

**He commits to a parallel-track plan, the Builder's instinct**:

1. Days 1-4: temporary water capture (small pool at the spring, covered with sun-cloth) + cistern excavation
2. Days 5-12: cistern lining + first irrigation channel segment (top 200m, easiest grade)
3. Days 13-20: complete channel to the build site + plant micro-plot 1 (tepary bean, fastest-maturing)
4. Days 21-35: channel maintenance + plot 2-3 (amaranth, gourd) + start drip system on plot 1
5. Days 36-60: tending + parallel-survival foraging + Hunter-baseline opportunistic small-game

**This is a 60-day project that assumes the spring holds, the weather cooperates, and no cascade event occurs.** All three assumptions are fragile.

**Gaps surfaced:**

- N68 — **Caliche-mortar recipe** (crushed caliche + clay slurry + water, troweled in 2 coats with cure time) — a Mason-desert sub-specialty technique. Analog to Run 06's float-foundation: Mason-Expert in temperate doesn't auto-pass; needs procedural access.
- N69 — **Evaporation loss as a per-hour modifier on open water surfaces** — analogous to Run 06's mosquito productivity tax, but for water budgets. Desert biome blocker.
- N70 — **Cistern capacity × construction-time × lining-quality** trade-off matrix — Builder-Earthworks design space

### Beat 03 — Days 5-6, the channel grade question

Vasco walks the 600m route with a length of cordage stretched between two stakes, eyeballing grade. The wash has natural grade — that's why water flows through it — but the natural grade is too steep in places (water runs and doesn't soak; carries away the channel surface) and too shallow in others (water stagnates and evaporates).

**Earthworks-Expert saliency** here fires hard. He reads the wash's natural grade against the *ideal* grade for a gravity-fed channel (~0.5-1% drop, slow enough to retain, fast enough to flow). He marks ~12 spots where the channel needs cut-down (steep spots — sediment-bench check-dams to slow flow) and ~6 spots where it needs build-up (shallow spots — clay-tamped berms to maintain head).

**Channel construction estimate at his rate**:

- 600m of channel × ~5min/m for digging + grading at Earthworks-Expert pace = ~50 hours of work = ~7-9 working days
- Plus ~18 check-dam / berm structures @ ~1 hour each = ~18 hours = ~3 days
- Plus channel lining where flow-rate would scour (~30% of length) — caliche-mortar bed = ~3-4 days
- **Total channel: ~13-16 days** of focused work, weather-dependent

**At Master grade (signature ability "graded earthwork")** — Vasco doesn't have this signature; his channel would be grade B (the channel flows, but loses ~20-30% to seepage and minor erosion at non-lined sections). A Master Earthworks Builder would produce grade A (lined throughout, optimal grade, <5% loss). **The mastery delta lands on water-loss percentage, not on whether the channel "works."**

**Gap surfaced:**

- N71 — **Channel grade quality grading per §14.3** — Master vs Expert vs Practiced produces different water-loss percentages, not different binary "works/doesn't"
- N72 — **Channel lining as a sub-task within the larger irrigation project** — caliche-mortar applied selectively where scour risk is high

## Phase 2 — Days 7-20, the cistern, the channel, and the first plant

### Beat 04 — Days 7-12, cistern and channel parallel work

Vasco's daily rhythm settles into a desert-Survivalist pattern: **work the cool morning (06:00-11:00) and the cool evening (17:00-19:30), shelter mid-day (11:00-17:00) under the shade pocket on the east bank.** Mid-day temperature peaks at ~28°C ambient in his late-winter window — survivable in shade but lethal under direct sun while doing pickaxe work.

The work-window inversion is the **opposite** of Run 06's summer-tundra inversion (which pushed work to the night side). **§14.13 saliency** under shade pocket: he reads the wash for distant motion, watches the sky for dust haze, watches the prickly pear pads for water-stress signals (the pads flatten slightly when the plant pulls from reserves).

**Days 7-12 accomplishments**:

- **Cistern**: dug + lined two coats. Mason-Expert + Earthworks-Expert combined produces grade-A masonry (caliche-mortar walls smooth, level). Cover frame on, sun-cloth over.
- **Channel top 200m**: dug + 4 check-dams + initial caliche-mortar lining on the two scour-prone bends. Grade B (he's working solo and the channel is unmortared except at scour spots).
- **Spring catchment**: small ~50L collection basin dug at the spring head, sun-cloth covered, pipe connected. **Pipe runs through the steepest section** (the upper 30m of the channel from spring downward, where natural grade would overflow without it).

**The cistern fills slowly.** By Day 12 evening: ~400L in storage. Spring is flowing at ~30-50L/day during this week (mid-rain-season aquifer recharge). His 10L bag is topped from the spring directly; cistern is buffer.

**Body state end of Day 12**:
- Warmth: 90% (desert daytime, fine)
- Hunger: 60% (eating into rations faster than planned due to heavy labor)
- Thirst: 70% (drinking from spring + cistern at need)
- Tiredness: 55% (cumulative)
- Salt: 50% — **first finding**: the salt block is heavier use than he projected. Sweat losses on pickaxe work are significant.

### Beat 05 — Days 13-17, the channel pushes downstream

Continues channel work, top-down. By Day 17 the channel reaches the planned micro-plot site at Day 17 evening — ~500m of channel dug + graded + selective-mortar-lined. Grade B overall; one stretch (~80m of soft-sand wash bottom where he couldn't bind the surface) is grade C and will lose water badly.

Test flow on Day 17 evening: opens the cistern outflow + watches a small trickle make its way ~480m down. It takes **~90 minutes** for water to reach the plot site at his flow rate. **Loss to seepage and evaporation across the channel: estimated 60%** — he started with 80L from the cistern and ~30L arrived. Worse than he hoped. The grade-C section drinks badly.

**Gaps surfaced:**

- N73 — **Channel transit-time and per-meter water-loss as a real spec** — the channel is not a binary "carries water/doesn't"; it's a percentage retention with grade-quality input
- N74 — **Distributed water network as a Builder/Earthworks project** — analog to wiring, plumbing in modern construction. Where does this skill live in the §14 tree?

### Beat 06 — Days 18-20, plot 1 prep + plant + drip distributor

Vasco prepares plot 1 (2m × 4m, ~8m²) at the channel's downstream end. **Soil amendment** is the question — desert soil is sand-dominant with clay streaks, low in organic matter. He has:

- **No compost** (desert decomposition is slow, his rations don't yield much, no animal manure available)
- **Mesquite leaf litter** — gathered slowly from under the mesquite stand over Days 8-17 as a side-task while working. Small amount.
- **Burnt brush ash** from his cooking fires — small amount, alkaline (a concern in alkaline desert soil but limited quantity won't tip pH significantly)
- **Cactus pad pulp** as a moisture-retainer + minor nutrient (Forager-xeriscape technique he has at Practiced — slice pads, bury slices in the plot soil)

He digs the plot 30cm deep, mixes in his thin amendments, levels. Sets up a **drip distributor** from the irrigation pipe — drills 8 small holes spaced ~50cm apart along a 4m run of pipe, with small plugs he can adjust per-hole to balance flow. **This is the precision tool work** — hand drill + careful hole-spacing + plug-management for differential flow across the run (downstream holes naturally want less water because head pressure drops along the run; he compensates by drilling smaller upstream holes).

**Plant**: tepary bean, 24 seeds in 4 rows of 6. He chose tepary first because (a) ~70-day cycle which still **exceeds the 60-day run window** by ~10 days — meaning even his fastest crop won't fully mature in his stay; (b) drought-tolerant, will survive if water cuts; (c) protein-rich, the highest-value yield if he gets pods.

**Day 20 evening: plot 1 planted, drip system tested, channel carrying water from cistern at a measured loss rate.** First crop in the ground.

**Body state end of Day 20**:
- Warmth: 85%, Hunger: 55% (rations down to ~5 days), Thirst: 80% (water now plentiful from cistern), Tiredness: 60%, Salt: 35% — **declining**

## Phase 3 — Days 21-40, the tending grind and the cascade pressures

### Beat 07 — Days 21-28, plots 2-3, the flash flood scare

Vasco plants plot 2 (amaranth) and plot 3 (gourd creeping over plot 1 as shade) across Days 21-25. **Plot 2 amaranth is the high-risk plant** — 80-100 day cycle, *will not* mature in the run window, won't even fully flower. He plants it anyway as a multi-season scoping question: does the soil hold it through summer dormancy and into next winter's wet window? In a real run, he'd never know.

**Day 26 — the flash flood scare.** A storm front parks over the upland 20km away — Vasco can see the dark cloud structure on the horizon. He doesn't know how much rain is falling or whether the dry-wash drainage extends back to the storm's catchment. **The dry-wash he camped in is the *exact* flood corridor he should not be standing in.**

**§14.13 saliency at Earthworks-Expert** reads the wash's geometry as flood-channel — he knows this — but his Novice weather-reading doesn't tell him how much water is in the upland storm. **The mastery delta hits him here**: a Survivalist-desert-Expert (which he is not) would read the dust-load and barometric signal and have a lead-time estimate. He has minutes-to-hours of uncertainty.

He moves the camp gear to the east-bank caliche overhang (above the wash floor). Cistern is in the wash bed — too heavy to move, has to take the risk. Channel is in the wash — same. Plots are at the wash mouth — same.

**At 14:30, water arrives in the wash.** Not a wall, just a brown push — maybe 20cm deep, moving at walking pace, building. By 15:00 the wash is running ~40cm deep. By 16:00 it's dropping.

**Damage assessment Day 27 morning**:
- **Cistern**: covered, partially buried in the wash bed under the new bank — the flood actually *added* sediment around the cistern, partially burying it but not breaching the lining. **Net: cistern intact, partially silted, fresh inflow of murky water that needs settling.**
- **Channel**: the top 200m is fine (caliche-mortar lined, held). The middle 300m grade-B section is significantly degraded — check-dams partially washed out, ~3 of 18 berms gone. The bottom 100m (grade C, sandy section) is essentially *erased* — back to wild wash bottom.
- **Plot 1**: tepary bean seedlings just emerging. **Plot 1 is destroyed.** Sediment buried the seedlings under ~15cm of fresh wash silt. They will not recover.
- **Plot 2 (amaranth)**: same fate.
- **Plot 3 (gourd)**: same fate.
- **Drip distributor pipe**: still mostly in place but full of silt; will need flushing.

**Vasco lost ~75% of the plot work** in a 90-minute flood event he could not have prevented at his weather-reading tier.

**Gaps surfaced:**

- N75 — **Flash-flood modeling tied to upland storm catchment** — currently Mother Nature's weather (§3.3) is a coarse-grid model; flash floods would need a hydrology layer on top
- N76 — **Plot site selection vs flood corridor** as a Builder/Earthworks design problem — the dry-wash is the irrigation supply AND the flood path; these conflict
- N77 — **Skill-delta for Survivalist-desert vs Builder cross-domain weather-reading** — Vasco at Novice weather couldn't read storm catchment volume; Survivalist-desert-Expert would have
- N78 — **Cascade event interrupting an infrastructure project** — analog to Run 03's axe-slip injury and Run 06's bear-at-the-river-bend; here, it's the biome's signature event eating the work

### Beat 08 — Days 28-35, decision point and re-plant

Vasco sits with this for a full day. The options:

- **A. Abandon plot work, treat the remaining 25 days as pure survival arc** — he has rations, the cistern (now silted but salvageable), shade, salt. He'd survive easily and leave.
- **B. Re-plant plots in a flood-safer location** — but moving the plots off the dry-wash means moving away from the channel, means re-running the channel to a different terminus, means re-doing two weeks of work.
- **C. Replant the same location, accept the same flood risk, but raise the plot beds** — terraced raised beds with stone retaining walls (Mason-Expert) that would shed sediment.
- **D. Replant on the *east bank* above the wash floor**, accept that the channel doesn't reach them and use hand-carry irrigation from cistern (Vasco walks water 2× daily) — the labor is brutal but the flood risk is gone

He picks **C + partial D**: rebuilds plot 1 as a raised stone-retained terrace ~30cm above wash floor, replants tepary, uses partial-drip + partial-hand-carry. Plots 2 and 3 he relocates onto the east bank above flood line.

The re-plant happens Day 30. **The tepary 70-day cycle now extends past his 60-day run window by 40 days.** He explicitly will not see harvest. He's planting for the question, not the food.

### Beat 09 — Days 36-40, foraging shifts, predator pressure

By Day 36 his rations are down to ~2 days. **He shifts to live foraging** — Forager-xeriscape Practiced fires regularly:

- **Mesquite pods** are not yet ripe (late spring is their window) — he gathers small amount of green-pod which is edible-but-low-yield, marks the stand for later
- **Prickly pear pads** (de-spined, sliced, boiled or roasted) — ~150 cal per pad, available reliably
- **Prickly pear fruit** — only the over-wintered fruit from last summer (rare, dried), not this year's (not yet)
- **Agave heart** — high-value (~3000 cal in a mature plant) but requires roasting in a pit for 24+ hours, kills the plant; he marks one mature plant as a "if I'm starving on Day 55, this is the calorie reserve"

**Day 38 evening — packrats at the plots.** A packrat (small rodent, ~200g, opportunistic) is in plot 1, eating the tepary seedlings he replanted Day 30 — they're 8 days old, just past germination, the most vulnerable stage. He chases it; it leaves. He estimates ~3 of his 24 seedlings have been bitten through. They'll die.

**Day 39** — bird strike on plot 2 (amaranth). A small flock of ground-feeders has discovered the bed; the freshly-tossed soil holds visible seed cues. ~15% of plot 2 disturbed before he covers it with a brush mat.

**Day 40** — fresh **rabbit tracks** at plot 3 (gourd), an open meadow of soil that looks delicious to a rabbit. No damage yet.

**Vasco's defensive options** (no rifle, no bow Expert):
- **Cactus-needle fencing** — Forager-xeriscape technique he has at Practiced. He spends Days 39-40 building a low fence of cholla branches (jointed cactus with hooked spines) around each plot. Local + effective + free + signature-desert. Rabbits and packrats will not push through this; ground-feeding birds are deterred but not stopped.
- **Brush mat over the bed** to discourage bird-seeding behavior — works for established plants, can't cover seedlings

**Gaps surfaced:**

- N79 — **Wildlife pressure on agricultural plots** — packrats, rabbits, ground-feeding birds, locusts (if applicable). Each has a different defensive response. Currently §5 wildlife doesn't model crop-raid utility AI.
- N80 — **Cactus-needle fencing as Builder/Forager cross-domain recipe** — the desert-signature plot defense

### Beat 10 — Days 41-50, water budget tightens, spring drops

**Day 42 — the spring's flow drops.** Vasco measures: ~5L/day, down from ~30-40L during the wet window. The aquifer recharge is past. The cistern holds ~350L (post-flood salvage, post-tending use). At his current use (5L drinking + 5L plot maintenance for the raised + east-bank plots = ~10L/day), the cistern is a ~35-day reserve. **He has 18-20 days left in the run. Margin is real but not generous.**

He cuts plot 3 (gourd) entirely — it's the highest water-need, lowest immediate value. Pulls the seedlings. Two plots remain: tepary + amaranth.

**Day 45 — the cascade begins to compound.**
- Salt block down to ~20%
- Rations gone Day 43; on full foraging
- Mid-day temperatures climbing — ~32°C peak, signaling approach of the hot/dry season
- A coyote scent at the cistern Day 46 morning (drawn by his food cache + activity)

**Day 47-50** — Vasco focuses on the agave-heart roast as protein-and-water insurance. Days 47-48 prep, Day 49 pit-roast (24-hour low fire under sand-covered pit). Day 50 he eats roasted agave heart — ~2500 cal of dense, syrupy starch. Buys him 5 days of caloric runway.

**Body state end of Day 50:**
- Warmth: 80% (mid-day shade discipline holding)
- Hunger: 60% (agave windfall just landed)
- Thirst: 75% (water rationed)
- Tiredness: 65% — chronic
- Salt: 15% — **critical** — sweat losses now > salt block intake

## Phase 4 — Days 51-60, the close

### Beat 11 — Days 51-58, plot status and the survival arc

The tepary plot is the *one thing* of his project that's working. Plants are ~6 weeks old, lush green compared to the desert around them, flowering by Day 54, beginning to set pods Day 57. **The pods will mature ~Day 70 — ten days after Vasco leaves.** He will not see his harvest.

The amaranth plot is taller — Vasco's height by Day 55 — but nowhere near flowering. It will need full summer to seed-out, and the spring will likely dry before then. **Amaranth was never going to harvest in the run window.** The data point is that *it survived.* Whether it survives to summer-end is a question this run cannot answer.

His daily rhythm Days 51-58: water plots morning + evening, forage prickly pear + green mesquite, sit in shade mid-day, observe and log. **§14.13 saliency** continues firing on wash and channel and plot status. He repairs check-dams that have slumped. He pulls weeds (desert weeds — small, scarce, but real). He watches the sky every morning for storm signs.

**No second flash flood. No bear. No major cascade.** The run from Day 30 onward is the *quiet long middle* of a survival arc — exactly the texture §1 No Dominant Strategy + §2 Mechanical Behavior promised: most days are not dramatic, and they don't need to be.

### Beat 12 — Days 59-60, the close-out

Day 59-60 Vasco prepares departure. He:
- **Caches** the spring-catchment pipe + drip distributor under the caliche overhang for a possible return next winter
- **Logs** detailed observations of the plot states — pod-set on tepary, height on amaranth, soil moisture readings by hand
- **Walks the channel** one last time, notes where flood damage was permanent vs salvageable
- **Marks** the agave stand he didn't eat (he ate the mature one; two younger plants are 3-5 years from maturity)
- **Departs** Day 60 morning with his cached water reserve in the bag, leaving the cistern + channel + plots in place for either a return run or a future character

**He survived. The infrastructure exists. The crops are not harvested. The question is whether the infrastructure was worth the survival cost.**

## What ended Vasco — n/a, run alive

Vasco did not die in this scenario. He came close to a salt-deficiency cascade (Day 50 salt at 15%); the agave-heart windfall stabilized him. He came close to losing the run to the flash flood (Day 26); the replant at raised + east-bank locations recovered the project. He came close to a coyote cascade (Day 46 scent + Day 47-50 cache exposure during agave roast) but no encounter materialized.

A counterfactual: if Vasco had been **Forager-primary instead of Builder-primary**, the irrigation infrastructure project would have been impossible (no Mason/Earthworks saliency, no caliche-mortar skill, no channel-grade read), and he would have been pushed entirely to foraging + scavenging — a viable run but a *fundamentally different* one. **§1 No Dominant Strategy is preserved by the fact that desert farming is gated behind a Builder/Earthworks sub-specialty that not every character has.**

Another counterfactual: if the run had been **summer instead of late-winter-to-spring**, mid-day temperatures would have been ~42°C+ and the heat-tolerance debuff stack would have killed him on the pickaxe work in Week 1. The seasonal window is doing real work.

## Architecture Stress-Test results

- **§1 No Dominant Strategy** — HELD strongly. Desert farming is high-risk-high-reward, requires Builder/Earthworks Expert specifically, has a flash-flood failure mode, has a water-budget failure mode, has a wildlife-pressure failure mode, has a seasonal-window constraint, and produces a harvest *outside the 60-day window* meaning even success is partial. **The mechanic is not dominant — it is barely viable.** This is exactly the design test passing.
- **§2 Mechanical Behavior** — HELD. The flash flood emerged from upland storm + dry-wash geometry, not from a Storyteller arrangement. The spring flow dropped because aquifer recharge ran out, not because the design wanted drama. Plots failed to packrats because packrats live there.
- **§14 debuff-removal + mastery delta** — HELD. Vasco's Builder/Mason/Earthworks-Expert fired fluently on cistern + channel + grade-reading. His Forager-Practiced fired on agave + prickly pear + mesquite. His Glimpsed agronomy + Novice weather-reading produced exactly the gap that the flood exploited. **Same character, different skill tiers, observably different output across sub-tasks.**
- **§14.13 saliency** — HELD and EXTENDED. Earthworks-saliency surfaced sub-surface water (the spring), grade, drainage, clay streaks, caliche outcrops, scour-prone bends — a desert-specific layered read. **This run extends Run 06's "sub-specialty-specific saliency" finding to Earthworks as a distinct sub-node alongside Mason and Carpenter.**
- **§3 Desert biome material list** — significantly extended. Caliche-as-mortar, clay-streak-as-lining, mesquite-pod / prickly-pear / agave / cholla as Forager loop, sun-cloth as required gear, salt-block as continuous consumable. Cactus-needle fencing as Builder/Forager cross-domain.
- **Farming is NOT supported by current locked systems.** N66 (crop water demand) + N79 (wildlife crop raid) + N75 (flash-flood model) + agronomy at Glimpsed only = the run **simulated** farming because the design exercise allowed it, but Mother Nature does not yet have the systems to model it natively.

## Design Inventory

### Structures built (proposed)

- **Caliche-mortar lined cistern**: ~3 × 3 × 1.5m pit, two-coat caliche-mortar lining, sun-cloth + brush mat cover, ~13,500L theoretical capacity, ~30% fill realistic. Mason-Earthworks-Expert grade A construction; partially silted by flash flood, salvaged.
- **Gravity-fed irrigation channel**: 600m from spring catchment to plot site, ~12 check-dams + 6 berms, selective caliche-mortar lining at scour points, ~60% water-retention rate at grade B. Top 200m survived flood; bottom 400m partially destroyed and partially re-built.
- **Spring catchment**: ~50L collection basin at the spring head, sun-cloth covered, HDPE pipe outflow to the channel.
- **Drip distributor**: 4m HDPE pipe with 8 drilled holes + adjustable plugs, distributes head-pressure-aware flow across plot row.
- **Raised stone-retained terrace plot** (post-flood rebuild): ~30cm above wash floor, Mason-Expert dry-stack retaining walls, ~8m² growing surface.
- **East-bank above-flood plots**: 2 × ~6m² each, no channel access, hand-carry irrigation.
- **Cactus-needle fencing**: cholla-branch fence around each plot, ~50cm high, packrat/rabbit deterrent.
- **Agave roasting pit**: stone-lined pit, sand-covered, 24hr low-fire roast.

### Recipes / techniques surfaced (proposed)

- **Caliche-mortar**: crushed caliche + clay slurry + water, troweled in 2 coats with 24-hour cure between. Mason-desert sub-specialty.
- **Cistern lining + cover** for evaporation reduction.
- **Channel grade-reading + check-dam + berm design**: Earthworks-Expert procedural — cut grade where steep, build grade where shallow, line where scour.
- **Drip distributor pipe**: differential hole-sizing for head-pressure compensation along run.
- **Cactus-pad pulp as soil amendment + moisture-retainer**: Forager-xeriscape Practiced.
- **Agave-heart pit-roast**: 24hr low-fire under sand, ~2500 cal per mature plant, kills plant.

### Material constraints documented

- **Caliche**: abundant in dry-wash sub-surface at 30-50cm depth, pickaxe-accessible, free; pre-cementitious when crushed + slurried.
- **Clay streaks**: scattered in wash banks; cistern-lining material; tamping required.
- **Sand**: dominant surface material; useless for foundation, useful for thermal-mass insulation on roasting pits.
- **HDPE pipe (salvaged)**: 30m, irreplaceable mid-run, critical bottleneck for the drip system.
- **Water (intermittent spring)**: 5-50L/day depending on aquifer state; stochastic; cannot be relied upon.
- **Mesquite pods**: late-spring ripening; not available in run window.
- **Prickly pear pads + fruit**: pads always available; fruit only late summer + dried-over-winter remnants.
- **Agave**: multi-year resource; one mature plant = ~2500 cal, kills plant.
- **Cholla branches**: free fencing material; spine hazard during construction.
- **Salt**: consumable, depletes; no local salt source in run scenario (some desert biomes have salt flats — would change the run).

## EXPLORATORY DESIGN QUESTIONS

The run was set up to test six questions explicitly. Each is answered as a *design proposal*, not a commitment.

### Q1 — Does desert farming require an even-narrower skill tree?

**Yes.** This run surfaced at least two new sub-nodes:

- **Earthworks** as a Builder sub-node distinct from Mason and Carpenter — handles grade, drainage, terracing, channel design. Vasco read the wash because he had Earthworks-Expert; a Mason-only Builder would not.
- **Hydrology** as a sub-node (potentially Survivalist or Builder) — handles aquifer-state reading, flash-flood lead-time, channel transit-time. Vasco's Novice hydrology produced the flash-flood vulnerability.

A third sub-node (**Agronomy**) for the crop side is required if farming is ever committed. Vasco at Glimpsed agronomy was working from book-knowledge, not felt knowledge — analogous to Joren's float-foundation Practiced grade in Run 06.

**Proposal**: if farming enters scope, Earthworks + Hydrology join Builder; Agronomy joins Forager (or becomes a new Botanist→Farmer sub-tree).

### Q2 — Is desert farming so hard + risky that it preserves §1 No Dominant Strategy naturally?

**Yes, strongly.** The 60-day window of this run did not produce a harvest. The flash flood ate 75% of the first plot work. The spring's flow halved at mid-run. Wildlife pressure required active defense. Salt depletion nearly cascaded. The plant cycles exceed the safe season window.

**No reasonable player would pick desert farming as their *primary* survival strategy.** It is at best a *supplemental project* layered on top of foraging + scavenging + opportunistic hunting. **This is exactly the shape §1 demands**: a viable lane with specific costs that make it equally-good-not-better than the alternatives.

### Q3 — Does irrigation infrastructure cross into Builder/Mason territory, or need its own system?

**It crosses cleanly into Builder/Earthworks** with **two new techniques** (caliche-mortar, channel grade-reading) and one new gear category (irrigation pipe + drip distributor). The cistern is straight Mason-Expert work. The channel is straight Earthworks-Expert work. **No new system needed for the infrastructure side** — Builder + Earthworks sub-node covers it.

**The *crop* side is where the gap is**, not the infrastructure side.

### Q4 — How does farming integrate with weather?

**Tightly, and weather currently lacks the needed layer.** Two specific weather features are required:

- **Flash-flood modeling** from upland storm catchment to dry-wash channel — currently the §3.3 coarse-grid weather model produces precipitation but does not propagate it through terrain hydrology
- **Aquifer-state model** for intermittent springs — current weather model handles rainfall but not multi-week sub-surface recharge / discharge

Both are non-trivial additions. They are also valuable beyond farming — flash floods are a desert-biome hazard in their own right (§3.1 desert threats already names them); aquifer state matters for any character relying on a spring.

### Q5 — If channel + crop = 100 days total, does desert farming require seasonal planning?

**Yes. Strongly. This is the run's most important finding.**

Vasco's project did not complete in 60 days. The channel + cistern took ~20 days. The crops needed 70-120 days. **Total project window is at least 100 days; comfortably 120-180.** This means:

- **Farming as a play option implies long-term character continuity** — staying alive on one character across multiple seasons
- **Seasonal planning** becomes a real game layer — winter prep, spring plant, summer tend, summer-or-fall harvest
- **Multi-season character commitments** push toward the §4.9 "Year as Meta-Arc" being not just a milestone but a *gameplay loop* — farming characters explicitly play for the year
- **Permadeath** (§6) gets sharper teeth — a character who dies in summer loses a 100-day investment in the spring planting

**Implication**: committing to farming reshapes the play-arc length. It is a *long-game* option in a game currently designed around 1-30 day runs. This is not a small commitment.

### Q6 — Multiplayer: cooperative irrigation pays off, solo may never. Is this only viable in coop?

**Coop strongly amplifies viability.** Two-player work would have:
- Built the cistern + channel in ~half the days (15 vs 25-30)
- Distributed the water-carry labor on the east-bank plots
- Had one player on weather-watch / flash-flood lead time while the other worked
- Doubled the calorie burn but more than doubled the work output (specialization gains)

**Solo desert farming is viable but narrow.** Coop desert farming is **substantially more viable** but still risky. **Three-player coop with a Survivalist-desert-Expert weather-reader, a Builder-Earthworks for the infrastructure, and a Forager-xeriscape for the crops + parallel food** would be the "optimal farming team" composition — and even then, only marginally above survival-without-farming.

**This is the right shape for §1**. No Dominant Strategy + Coop multiplies-but-doesn't-eliminate-risk = the design intent.

## Scope-creep risk assessment

**If desert farming is committed to scope, the additional systems required are**:

1. **Crop water-demand model** — per-species, per-growth-stage, per-temperature water need. Non-trivial.
2. **Crop growth + maturation timeline** — per-species cycle length, per-temperature growth-rate modifier, per-water-stress yield-reduction curve.
3. **Soil amendment model** — desert soil organic content, the effect of compost/ash/cactus-pulp on yield, baseline poverty of desert substrate.
4. **Wildlife crop-raid utility AI** — packrat, rabbit, ground-feeding bird, locust (if applicable). Each a separate behavioral profile.
5. **Flash-flood propagation model** — upland storm to wash transit + volume + arrival-time + scour pattern.
6. **Aquifer-state model** — multi-week sub-surface water; spring flow as stochastic process.
7. **Agronomy as a sub-node** — Forager / Botanist sub-specialty for crop knowledge.
8. **Earthworks as a sub-node within Builder** — distinct from Mason and Carpenter.
9. **Hydrology as a sub-node** — channel grade, transit, loss; weather lead-time for floods.
10. **Long-arc character continuity systems** — if a character invests 100+ days in farming, the §6 permadeath economics get heavier; this might require a save/checkpoint or "winter-over" mechanic.

**This is 10 new systems. Mother Nature is currently scoped at 17 sections of design, ~6 of which are locked. Adding 10 farming-specific systems is a major scope expansion.**

**Alternative design options surfaced**:

- **Foraging-only path**: existing Forager node + xeriscape compendium expansion covers desert calorie needs without farming. Lower complexity, lower payoff, no infrastructure investment, no harvest milestone.
- **Wild-harvest cultivation**: instead of planting + tending crops, the player *manages* existing wild stands — pruning mesquite for better pod yield, protecting agave from over-harvesting by other players, marking prickly pear groves. This is **xeriscape stewardship**, not farming. Lower system cost (~3 new mechanics vs 10) and preserves the §2 Mechanical Behavior principle without scripting a "crop matures" event.
- **Water-only infrastructure**: irrigation + cistern + channel without any crop side. Player builds a water-supply network for survival (and for trading on MP); crops are explicitly *not* in scope. **This is Run 14 with the crops removed and likely the lowest-cost farming-adjacent option.** It uses Earthworks + Hydrology sub-nodes but does not require agronomy / crop / wildlife-raid systems.
- **Multi-season "homestead" expansion**: defer farming entirely to a post-launch content update, the way §5.8 defers domesticated animals. Big lever; build the foundations now (saliency for sub-nodes), commit content later.

## Big takeaways from Run 14

1. **Desert farming is feasible in scope but infeasible in 60 days solo.** The run produced infrastructure but no harvest. Either the run window extends to 120-180 days OR farming is committed as a multi-season project OR farming is deferred indefinitely.
2. **The infrastructure side (cistern + channel + drip) is a clean Builder/Earthworks extension** — no new system class needed, just two new techniques (caliche-mortar, channel grade-reading) and one sub-node (Earthworks).
3. **The crop side requires 10 new systems** — agronomy, crop water demand, soil amendment, wildlife crop-raid, flash-flood, aquifer model, plus three new sub-nodes (Earthworks, Hydrology, Agronomy). This is a major scope expansion.
4. **§1 No Dominant Strategy is preserved trivially** — desert farming is risky enough that no one will ever pick it as a primary strategy. It is at best a *signature project* for Builder-Earthworks-flavored characters, layered on top of standard foraging.
5. **Multiplayer cooperation is a real amplifier**, not a requirement. Solo is viable but narrow. Three-player team is substantially better. This aligns with the §1 server-mode rule that no mode dominates.
6. **The "water-only" alternative** (irrigation infrastructure without crops) is the **lowest-cost farming-adjacent commitment** and may be where the design lands — Earthworks + Hydrology sub-nodes earn their keep on water supply for survival + MP trade without requiring agronomy / crop / raid systems.
7. **The Year as Meta-Arc (§4.9) gains weight** — if farming enters scope, the year becomes a *gameplay loop* not a milestone. This is a real design pivot; worth deciding deliberately.
8. **Vasco's run shape — survived, did not harvest — is the honest answer.** The design exercise revealed feasibility-with-cost and the cost is large. A real player committing to this run with a real character would face the same trade-off, and most would not choose it. **That is §1 working as designed.**

The recommended next step is the **scope-decision conversation**: commit to farming (with 10 new systems), commit to *water-only* infrastructure (with 2-3 new sub-nodes), or defer farming indefinitely (with the saliency-for-sub-nodes foundation laid as preparation).
