# Run 06 — Builder, Tundra, Summer Construction Expedition

Date of run: 2026-05-10
Status: Day 78 in-game, alive, structure weather-tight, autumn arriving
Run author: William + Claude (design exercise)

## Setup

- **Character**: "Joren" (placeholder name) — Builder node, Mason sub-specialty above Carpenter
- **Biome**: Tundra, low-relief coastal plain ~3km from a slow river that dumps driftwood at its bend; rock outcrops on the north horizon, peat hummocks south
- **Season**: Midsummer onset → late summer → early autumn shoulder
- **Weather at start**: 11°C, overcast, light NW wind, midnight sun ~3 days past solstice (sun dips but does not set)
- **Time at start**: 02:00 "civic dusk" — the bright twilight that passes for night

### Starting kit (Builder, Mason-flipped, deliberate expedition)

- Builder standard variant: **mason hammer** (1.5kg, square face + cross peen), **hand drill + 6 masonry bits** (carbide-tipped, for stone work), hand axe, hatchet, knife
- Camp: ferro rod, paracord 20m, heavy tarp 3×4m, **heavy bedroll** (down-filled, rated -10°C), full cold-weather layers, **sun-cloth** (light reflective wrap for 24hr daylight glare), mosquito netting (head + sleep)
- Hauling: **pull-sled**, runner-shod, ~80kg cargo capacity, lash points
- Food: 10 days calorie-dense rations (pemmican, hardtack, fat — concentrated for low-foraging biome), water bag 4L
- No bow, no firearm. Builder-flavor only — spear backup not packed in favor of mass for stone-work tools

### Compendium grants at start

- Expert: **stonework** (cut, dress, dry-stack, mortar substitution), foundation, fortification, food-cache; tool-craft
- Practiced: **carpentry** (joints, framing — but Mason sub-specialty means timber-construction is a step down from Mason's stone fluency), cold-management (cross-domain Survivalist), sled-haul / load-bearing
- Novice: hunting, fire-craft, plant-ID, weather-reading, sub-arctic-specific construction
- Glimpsed: predator-reading, scent-reading

### Skill node

Builder — Mason sub-specialty primary; Carpenter secondary; Survivalist cross-domain Practiced.

### Goal

Build a winter-survivable shelter — durable enough that *someone* (Joren or another character) could overwinter in it. Sled hauled in via small bush plane drop on the river; pickup arranged for ~Day 90, with weather windows narrowing through autumn.

## Days 1-4 — landing, the site, the saliency hit

### Beat 01 — Day 1, the disembarkation

Joren steps off the gravel-bar runway. The plane lifts and is gone in under a minute. He is alone with a sled, ~280kg total mass between sled and pack, and a horizon that does not have a tree on it taller than his waist.

The first ten minutes are spent standing still. **§14.13 saliency** at Mason-Expert fires immediately and produces an inventory that a forest-builder's eye would not surface in this same landscape:

- A **frost-heave scar** ~40m off — a low ridge of upthrust gravel where the previous winter's freeze pushed stone toward the surface. To the novice, terrain texture. To the Mason, a free quarry.
- Three **drainage seeps** visible by the deeper green of sedge against the duller upland — saturated ground, water table <30cm down. The Mason's eye reads "do not foundation here" before any conscious thought.
- A **rock outcrop** on the rise ~120m north — granitic, weathered to fracture along bedding planes. **Building stone**, not just landscape.
- **Peat hummocks** on the south slope — mounded sphagnum 40-80cm tall, dome-shaped, indicating dry-enough surface that vegetation accumulates. Cut walls possible from these.
- The river bend ~3km west, with **driftwood** piled on the upstream gravel-bar by spring breakup — a finite reserve, no replenishment until next freshet.

**Joren is a Mason, in a non-native biome.** Mason-domain saliency fires fully — stone, drainage, frost-heave. **Tundra-specific saliency does not.** He cannot read the depth of the active layer (the thaw layer above permafrost) from surface vegetation cues alone — that's a Survivalist-tundra read he has only at Practiced cross-domain. The result: he sees the materials clearly and reads drainage well, but he doesn't yet know how deep he can dig before he hits ice.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N46 — Saliency layering by sub-specialty (Mason vs Carpenter vs generic Builder render different cue sets in same scene)
- N47 — Permafrost / active-layer depth reading (Builder-Mason sees stone clearly; Builder doesn't auto-read frost line)

### Beat 02 — Day 1-2, the mosquito-hour discovery

By 11:00 Day 1, the sun is fully up. Wind drops. Within twenty minutes the air around him is a moving cloud of mosquitoes. He pulls the head net.

**Productivity collapses.** Carrying logs, surveying, mason-hammer swings — all of it now happens through a head net's slight visual distortion, with the constant brush-and-slap reflex burning attention. He logs the first lesson without dignifying it: *summer tundra is mosquito tundra*.

Across the next 36 hours he learns the rhythm:
- **02:00 – 06:00** "civic night" — temperature drops to 5-7°C, mosquitoes mostly down. **Prime work window.**
- **06:00 – 11:00** — temperature rising, light wind often holds. Workable.
- **11:00 – 18:00** — mosquito peak. Heavy net work; coarse outdoor labor only.
- **18:00 – 02:00** — wind often drops at evening, mosquito second peak. Fire and smoke can suppress them at fixed camp.

He resets his clock. Sleep is from ~22:00 to 06:00 with the sleep-tent fully netted and a dark sun-cloth over the head end of the bedroll. Without the sun-cloth, the not-quite-dark of midnight sun keeps his circadian rhythm scrambled. **Sleep grade drops from B to D until the sun-cloth becomes part of the routine.**

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N48 — Mosquito pressure as a per-hour productivity modifier (not a discrete event)
- N49 — 24hr daylight effect on sleep recovery — sleep tent + blackout cloth as required, not optional, gear
- N50 — Hour-of-day shifted work cadence in summer arctic vs forest summer (work block inverted to night-side)

### Beat 03 — Day 3, the depth question

Joren begins foundation. The plan from his head: **rubble stone footing, dry-stacked, two-course deep.** This is the Mason's instinct — dig down to a frost-stable depth, lay a level course on tamped gravel, build up.

He starts to dig. **Spade hits ice at 38cm.** Not stone — true permafrost ice, hard, glass-cold, releasing meltwater immediately at the exposed face.

This is the active layer he could not read from surface cues. **The frost line he'd dig "below" in temperate ground is ABOVE the permafrost here — it's the seasonal thaw layer, and it goes back to ice in autumn.** Any foundation set within the active layer will heave each freeze-thaw cycle. Any foundation set onto the permafrost will, over years, thaw the ice it rests on, slump, and tilt the structure.

He sits with this for an hour. The Mason-Expert in him knows ten ways to handle marginal ground in temperate construction. None of those apply. **Tundra permafrost construction is a specialty he does not have.**

The cross-domain Survivalist Practiced tier surfaces a fragmentary memory: *float the foundation on a rock pad above the active layer, let the heave move the whole structure as one unit*. He's read this; he hasn't done it. **Grade-C confidence on the technique.**

He commits to the float approach. The foundation becomes: cleared pad of organic top-cover stripped off (peat, lichen, root mat), tamped gravel pad ~20cm thick, large flat-stone footing courses laid directly on the gravel. No digging below the active layer. The whole shelter will sit on top of the ground and ride the freeze-thaw together.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N51 — Permafrost construction (float-foundation pattern) — required for tundra Builder, absent from temperate Mason kit
- N52 — Active-layer-depth probe (a Survivalist-tundra check the Builder doesn't auto-pass)
- N53 — Mastery delta for in-specialty character operating in non-native sub-domain (Mason Expert → Mason-arctic Practiced; cross-domain knowledge fills gap incompletely)

### Beat 04 — Day 4, the material survey

He spends Day 4 not building. He surveys: walks all four cardinal directions out to ~1km, counts and ranks materials.

**Stone**: outcrop ~120m N, useful. Frost-heave scar ~40m E, abundant rubble of useful size — already broken, no quarrying needed. **Estimate: enough stone for ~3×4m walls at full height with no quarrying labor.**

**Driftwood**: river bend ~3km west. Hauling weight on the sled gives him ~80kg per round trip. A round trip burns ~5 hours at sled-load pace. Estimated reserve at the bend: enough for a roof frame + interior fittings + ~14 days of small fuel. **Driftwood economy**: not for walls. For roof framing and fire.

**Dwarf birch + willow**: scattered, max 2-3m, max 4cm diameter. Useless for structural lumber. Usable for: woven willow as roof underframe, brush bundles for insulation cavities, small-fuel kindling.

**Peat**: south slope hummocks. **Cut peat blocks** are a wall option. Time-cost of cutting + drying is real: a 40×20×15cm block of fresh peat is ~70% water by mass. Drying time in summer-tundra conditions (cool, often damp, intermittent rain): **two to four weeks** for full structural dryness, less for "stack and use as inner wall" damp-but-stable. He doesn't have weeks if walls block other work.

**Sod**: same as peat but shallower and tougher — surface mat 8-15cm thick, root-bound, tearable in slabs ~50×30cm. Sod is the **roof option**, not a wall option.

**Bone**: animal sign — caribou shed antlers (~3 found), one weathered moose skull at the river-bend, sparse. Frame use possible (drying-rack, small fittings), not load-bearing.

**Lichen, dried grass**: cavity insulation, available but slow to gather.

**The trade-off matrix forms.** Joren writes it out on the inside of his tarp with charcoal:

- **A. All-driftwood cabin**: ~25 sled-round-trips, ~125 hours of hauling alone before construction starts. Burns ~30% of his food window on transport. Familiar Mason-Carpenter construction once material is in. **Estimated build-complete: Day 50-55.**
- **B. Stone walls + driftwood roof frame + sod roof**: ~5 sled-round-trips for roof timber, no quarrying (use frost-heave rubble), dry-stack masonry at Mason-Expert speed but with float-foundation Practiced uncertainty. **Estimated build-complete: Day 35-40 if foundation holds, Day 60+ if foundation needs rework.**
- **C. Stone foundation + peat-block walls + sod roof**: minimal driftwood (door frame, ridge pole only). Peat-cut + dry-time is the path's constraint. **Estimated build-complete: Day 70+ unless peat is stacked damp.**

**§1 No Dominant Strategy in flagrant action.** No option is "best." A wins on builder-familiarity, loses on transport tax. B wins on speed if foundation works, loses on novel-technique risk. C wins on material-locality, loses on dry-time.

He picks B. **Mason's instinct + the rubble being already-broken at the heave scar tips it.** He logs the decision and the rationale — if it fails, he has the trade-off matrix to fall back on.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N54 — Driftwood as a finite per-river-bend resource with replenishment tied to spring freshet
- N55 — Peat-block dry time as a real per-week constraint, weather-dependent
- N56 — Sled-haul economy as material-cost multiplier in sparse-timber biomes

## Days 5-25 — the build, the rhythm, the bear

### Beat 05 — Days 5-12, foundation + first courses

Joren works the night-side block. **02:00-10:00 hard labor**, sometimes extending to 12:00 when wind holds and mosquitoes stay down. Afternoon is for material moves on the sled (driftwood haul: 3 round trips across the week, ~240kg of driftwood timber landed at the build site) and lighter tasks under the head net.

**Foundation**: Day 5-7. Strips a 4×5m pad (oversize by ~30cm in each axis for footing). Hauls ~60 sled-loads of gravel + rubble from the heave scar (short hauls, ~5 min each), tamps in layers, levels with the long edge of his hand axe as a straightedge. Sets first course of large flat stones as bearing footing.

**§14.13 saliency** while laying stone: Mason-Expert tier is fully active here. He reads the bedding plane of each stone, places the wider face down, picks the corner stones first (they set the line for the wall). What looks like "stacking rocks" to a novice is fast and confident — about half his temperate-construction pace because the stones are river-rolled rather than quarry-square, but still fluent.

**Days 8-12**: first three courses of wall. Dry-stack — no mortar (no cement, no clay he trusts, no firing kiln). Mortar is replaced by careful stone choice: every stone is fit so its weight wedges the stones on either side. Mason-Expert is exactly the skill this rewards.

**Quality grade A for the masonry itself.** The float foundation is the question mark — he won't know until next spring whether the heave moves the whole pad as one or shears it. The Mason-Expert grade applies to *cut-and-fit*. The float-foundation technique grades at C — he's done it by procedure, not by feel.

**Action time** (§14.3): foundation pad + 3 courses across 8 days = on track. The forest-builder estimate for a similar shell would be 5 days. **Tundra construction is ~1.6× the temperate time, even for an Expert Mason.** Most of the slowdown is environmental — head net interrupts, mosquito breaks, the work-block being half a normal day's length.

### Beat 06 — Day 13, the bear at the river-bend

Sled-haul day. Joren walks the 3km to the river bend with the empty sled. Wind from the south, river to his right.

**At ~700m from the bend**, the wind delivers him a complex of smells he cannot fully parse — not at his Glimpsed predator-reading tier. He notes "*something dead*" and continues, more carefully.

At the bend, on the gravel-bar where his driftwood reserve sits, **a brown bear is feeding on a beached carcass** — looks like a young caribou, possibly drowned during a river crossing days back. The bear is ~80m from the driftwood pile. Wind is in Joren's face; the bear has not seen or smelled him.

**§14.13 saliency for predator-reading** at Glimpsed tier produces almost no read. He sees "brown bear, large." He cannot read the bear's body state, hunger level, or aggression weight from posture. He doesn't know if this is a kill claim (defensive) or scavenge (less defensive). A Hunter would read this in seconds; the Builder cannot.

**Decision tree**:
- Wait — could be hours, could be days. Bears holding a kill (per §13.2 Carrion Chains) can claim a site for 2-5 days.
- Detour — circle wide around the bear, lose the driftwood reserve for the duration.
- Approach — bad. He has a mason hammer and a knife.
- Abandon today's haul — sled empty, walk back, lose the day.

He abandons the haul. Walks back the way he came, keeping the wind in his face for the first kilometer until he can break off. Logs the bear; logs the kill-site location; logs that the river-bend will be predator-claimed until the carcass is gone.

**§13.2 Carrion Chains directly impacts a non-combat Builder.** The bear is not after Joren. The bear's territory claim makes Joren's material reserve unreachable for an indeterminate window. The Mason's build slows for predator economics he can't directly counter.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N57 — Predator-claimed material reserves as a Builder-relevant denial mechanic (not just a Hunter problem)
- N58 — Builder defensive options when material extraction overlaps predator territory (no rifle, no bow Expert — what does Builder do?)

### Beat 07 — Days 14-21, walls + the rain week

The bear holds the river-bend for five days. Joren works what he can with on-site material: wall course 4, course 5, course 6. By Day 18 he is to chest height on three walls; the south wall lower because that's where the door + small window will sit.

Day 19 weather shifts. **A rain front parks over the coast for four days.** Intermittent drizzle, occasional heavier showers, ambient drops to 6°C. Mosquitoes recede; productivity in that axis *improves*. But:

- The stone pad and the laid courses are slick. Setting a heavy stone on wet stone — fingers cold, gloves wet — Joren has a near-miss on Day 20 when a 30kg corner stone shifts under his hand. He catches it with his thigh, bruises but no laceration. **§22 injury system narrowly avoided.** He logs the lesson: stop wall-work in active rain, work small fittings under the tarp instead.
- The peat hummocks he'd been eyeing as a backup wall material **saturate**. Cut peat blocks in this week would need 6+ weeks dry time. He's now committed to the stone-walls + sod-roof path; peat-as-wall-backup is gone.
- His driftwood already on-site (some 240kg of it stacked under tarp) stays usable. Wet wood would burn poorly later (§13.4) but as structural timber it's fine.

By Day 21 the rain clears. He returns to the river-bend on Day 22 with the sled. **The bear has finished the carcass + left.** Tracks lead off SE — he will not see this individual again on this trip, probably. He hauls a full sled-load.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N59 — Wet-rock construction safety (Builder-specific injury vector; analog to N40 axe slip)
- N60 — Per-material wet-degradation matrix (peat saturates differently from driftwood differently from sod-roof material)

### Beat 08 — Days 22-30, walls complete + roof framing

By Day 27, walls are at full height — 1.9m at the high point, 1.6m at the eaves where the roof will slope. Mason quality A throughout. Floor area 2.8 × 3.6m interior — small, deliberately, for heat retention.

He cuts driftwood ridge pole + rafters Day 28-30. Driftwood is irregular — not straight milled lumber — so each rafter is custom-fit. Carpentry-Practiced tier is firing here, not Mason-Expert. He's about 70% of an Expert-Carpenter's speed on this work. The rafters are good enough; they don't look *clean* the way a forest-builder's would. **Output grade B for the timber frame**, vs grade A for the masonry below it.

This is the **multi-axis quality grading from §14.3** in plain view — a shelter with a grade-A foundation, grade-A walls, grade-B roof frame, and (incoming) grade-B-or-C sod roof if the sod doesn't quite seat well. The structure will perform at the weakest grade, which is the roof. He plans accordingly.

### Beat 09 — Days 31-40, the sod roof

Sod cutting is the slowest task on the project. He cuts roughly 30 × 50 × 10cm slabs from the lower south slope — root-bound enough to hold together when carried, thick enough to insulate. ~8 slabs per work-day, ~140 slabs needed for the roof at his planned thickness.

He learns mid-cutting that sod cut from the seep-edges (deeper green) **collapses in the hand** — the substrate is too saturated. He has to walk further upslope to drier sod, which adds sled-haul distance. Days 33-36 are continuous sod transport with brief rests.

**§14.13 saliency at Mason tier is not helping much here.** This is a Carpenter / sub-arctic Survivalist task. He grades at Practiced, executes carefully but slowly.

Roof layered: driftwood rafters → woven willow brush undermat (resists the sod sagging through the rafters) → two layers of overlapping sod, grass-side down on the lower layer and grass-side up on the upper. He seals seams with handfuls of wet peat smeared by glove.

Day 40: roof on. The structure is weather-tight in a basic sense. **Light rain test on Day 41**: minor seep at one corner where two slabs didn't fully knit. He patches with more wet peat. By Day 43 the sod is settling, root systems re-knitting into the under-layer, and the seep stops.

### Beat 10 — Day 44, the hearth

Mason work returns. He builds a stone hearth in the SE corner of the cabin's interior, ~80cm × 60cm, with a stone chimney rising through a hand-cut hole in the roof. The chimney is the most technically demanding masonry on the trip — circular-stack with smoke draft, sealed with wet peat at the roof-pass-through. He spends two days on it.

**Fuel survey for the hearth**: driftwood (the primary fuel, finite reserve — at his current cutting + the remaining bend supply, ~40-60 days of moderate fire); willow brush (fast burn, small heat, abundant but kindling-only); peat (slow burn, dense smoke, abundant but smoke draft is the constraint); bone (none stored as fuel — too sparse). He logs that **peat-as-fuel** changes the chimney design — peat smokes far more than wood, the chimney needs more draft than he planned. He widens the throat slightly while the mortar-substitute is still workable.

**Smoke profile**: a peat-fed fire produces a thick, slow-rising plume visible at considerable distance. In a multiplayer context this is a signal as load-bearing as Run 02's smoke column at the river bend.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N61 — Peat-as-fuel burn profile (rate, heat output, smoke volume) distinct from wood
- N62 — Chimney-draft design depends on intended fuel mix (wood vs peat changes the spec)

## Days 45-78 — interior + the autumn race

### Beat 11 — Days 45-60, insulation + interior fittings

He spends ~15 days finishing the interior with materials that are now mostly cleanup-and-detail work. Wall cavity insulation: he had built the stone walls as single-course dry-stack with a ~6cm gap between inner and outer faces, intended for fill. He fills with:

- **Lichen** (dried, gathered slowly over the build window, ~3kg/day on slow-task days)
- **Dried grass** from the south slope, beaten free of seed
- **Brush of willow stems** as binder so the lichen + grass don't slump

Total cavity insulation across all four walls: he ends up with ~70% fill coverage. The remaining 30% (mostly upper courses) he closes off with a layer of sod stuffed in. **R-value estimate by feel: significantly better than uninsulated, far below temperate-zone code.**

Other interior: a sleeping ledge against the north wall (stone, 50cm above floor, raised against cold radiating off the floor). A small storage shelf above the hearth. A door frame in driftwood, with the actual door being his heavy tarp grommeted to a willow-stem frame — to be upgraded to a wood plank door if he gets back next summer.

By Day 60 the cabin is functionally a shelter someone could overwinter in. Marginal — but workable.

### Beat 12 — Day 61-72, the gear cache + outside work

He still has time. The plane pickup window is Day 87-90, weather permitting. He builds:

- An **outdoor fire pit** with a stone wind-shield wall, 4m from the cabin door (cooking outside in good weather, indoor only when needed)
- A **food cache** — small stone-corbel structure ~30m downwind, semi-buried into a low rise, with a slab roof. Bear-resistant by stone weight; nothing is bear-proof in absolute terms, but a 200kg slab is not casually lifted.
- A small **latrine pit** ~70m downwind of cabin and cache, with stone-edged trench

On Day 70 the weather signals shift. **First frost overnight, Day 71 dawn 0°C.** Sun lower in the sky. Days noticeably shorter — he's still in 18 hours of daylight, but the sun is no longer high enough to keep the surface warm late into the evening. Mosquitoes diminish. Work-window inverts back toward daytime.

By Day 72 the cabin reads correctly in the landscape — squat, low-profile, stone walls weathered already from rain + first frosts, sod roof greening over. From 200m it almost disappears into the slope.

### Beat 13 — Day 73, the second bear (different individual)

Pre-dawn, returning from a fuel-gathering walk, he hears digging at the food cache. Sprints the last 80m in just enough light to see a different bear — smaller, female, possibly a sub-adult — pawing at the cache structure. The slab roof has held; she has not got in. She has scattered some stones from the lower courses.

**Decision tree, Builder version**: no rifle, no bow, mason hammer + knife. He is at ~30m. The bear has not yet seen him.

He shouts. Slams the mason hammer flat against a flat rock — sharp percussive crack. The bear startles, runs ~50m off, stops, looks back. He repeats the noise. She moves further. By the time the sun is fully up she is 300m off and moving away.

**He survives the encounter by signal-display, not by combat.** The mason hammer was the right tool for the *signal* — a flat percussive crack carries well — not for fighting. **§15 No Dominant Strategy is honored in reverse here**: Builder has no apex-stopper in the kit, but the cache itself is the defense. Stone over slab + downwind placement bought him the time the bear needed to give up and try elsewhere.

He spends Day 73 reinforcing the cache: heavier slab on top, more weight on the lower courses. Logs the individual — a small female, possibly the same bear come back at any point. **Apex individual tracking (§13.2) at his Glimpsed predator-tier means he logs species + size + behavior** but not the identifying marks a Hunter would.

**Gaps surfaced:**
- N63 — Builder defensive toolkit — the cache structure as the active defense rather than the weapon
- N64 — Non-Hunter apex-individual logging fidelity (Glimpsed tier captures presence, not identity)

### Beat 14 — Days 74-78, batten down + the pickup window

Day 74-76: he batches small finishing tasks. Reinforces the door-tarp with double-layer at the bottom for wind. Builds a small woodshed lean-to against the south wall of the cabin for fuel storage (open-sided, sod-roofed). Caches the last of his fittings.

Day 77 he walks back to the river bend with the sled. Hauls one final load of driftwood — not because he needs it for himself, but because if anyone winters here next year, the fuel reserve is the difference between alive and dead. He stacks it inside the woodshed lean-to.

Day 78 evening: he stands outside the cabin in the long autumn dusk. The midnight-sun era is over; tonight will have a real two hours of dark. Wind is from the north, ~10 km/h, ambient 2°C. The structure he built — squat, stone, sod, weather-stained already — is what a Mason can do in tundra when given 90 days and 280kg of starting kit.

**It is not what a forest-builder would have built.** A forest-builder might have spent all 90 days hauling driftwood to attempt a wood cabin that would likely have remained unfinished. The trade-off matrix from Day 4 played out the way Mason-Expert read it — stone-walls + driftwood-roof + sod was the path that closed in the available window.

The plane is scheduled for Day 87-90. Joren is alive, sheltered, with 9 days of food remaining + 2 fresh hare snares set + the cabin's full stores already cached for whoever comes next.

**Run pauses here, ahead of pickup.**

## Architecture Stress-Test results (Run 06 Days 1-78)

- **§1 No Dominant Strategy** — fully exercised at Day 4 trade-off matrix. The three paths (all-driftwood / stone-driftwood-sod / stone-peat-sod) each carried specific costs: transport tax vs novel-technique risk vs dry-time. No path was strictly better. The Mason picked B on his strongest skill, not on raw optimality.
- **§2 Mechanical Behavior** — every consequence emerged from inputs: the rain-week was weather grid not a Storyteller "wet beat"; the river-bend bear was a Carrion Chain on a drowned caribou, not an encounter event; the second bear at the cache was scent + opportunism, not pacing.
- **§14 debuff-removal + mastery delta** — Mason-Expert fired fluently on stone work (grade A throughout), Carpenter-Practiced produced grade-B rafters, sub-arctic-Practiced produced grade-B sod work. **The mastery delta in non-native sub-domain held cleanly**: same character, observably different output quality across construction sub-tasks.
- **§14.13 saliency** — heavily tested. Mason-domain saliency surfaced the heave-scar, drainage seeps, rock outcrop, fracture planes; tundra-specific saliency (active-layer depth, frost-line read from vegetation) did NOT auto-fire — those required a procedural Survivalist-tundra check Joren only had at Practiced cross-domain. The principle that **saliency is sub-specialty-specific within a character node** held.
- **§13.2 Carrion Chains** — fired on a drowned caribou the player never saw alive. The chain denied Joren a material reserve for 5 days. The mechanic affected a non-combat node directly.
- **§15** — combat absent by design. Bear encounters resolved via signal-display and cache-as-defense. The "what does a Forager / Builder do" path (§15.6) implicitly extends to Builder here: no firearm, the structure itself is the defense.
- **Cold-onset race** — 90 days of viable construction window held as the design specified. By Day 70-72 the first frosts arrived; the active layer began to firm; mosquitoes diminished. The seasonal pressure was felt, not announced.

## Design Inventory

### Structures completed

- **Float-foundation stone cabin**: 2.8 × 3.6m interior, 1.9m peak / 1.6m eaves; dry-stack stone walls with cavity insulation; driftwood rafters; willow undermat; double-layer sod roof; stone hearth with stone chimney; tarp-and-willow door
- **Outdoor stone fire pit + windshield wall**
- **Stone-corbel food cache** with 200kg slab roof, semi-buried
- **Latrine pit** stone-edged
- **Woodshed lean-to** against south wall, sod-roofed, ~14 days of additional fuel stored

### Recipes / techniques surfaced

- **Float foundation on permafrost** — strip organics, lay tamped gravel pad, set first stone course directly on gravel. Whole pad rides freeze-thaw as one unit.
- **Cavity-fill insulation** — dry lichen + dried grass + willow-stem binder, filled into ~6cm wall cavity behind dry-stack inner face.
- **Sod-roof layering** — driftwood rafters → woven willow brush undermat → two layers overlapping sod (grass-down + grass-up). Wet peat for seam sealing.
- **Peat-cut block** spec started but not used at scale (rain-week saturated the source).
- **Bear-cache by stone-mass** — 200kg slab roof + corbel walls + downwind siting. Not bear-proof; bear-resistant.

### Material constraints documented

- **Driftwood**: finite per-river-bend, replenishes only on spring freshet. Sled-haul economy at ~5hrs/round-trip.
- **Stone (frost-heave rubble)**: abundant in scar zones; pre-broken; no quarrying labor needed.
- **Peat**: 70% water by mass when fresh; 2-4 week dry time in summer-tundra; saturates in rain-week.
- **Sod**: surface mat, slab-cuttable, root-bound; collapses in hand at seep-edges.

## Big takeaways from Run 06

1. **Tundra-summer construction is a different game from forest construction.** The mason's tools translate; the depth-of-dig instinct does not. Saliency for stone fires fully; saliency for permafrost does not. The mastery delta inside one character node is observable in real time.
2. **§14.13 saliency is sub-specialty-specific.** A Mason-flavored Builder sees stone, frost-heave, drainage, fracture planes. A Carpenter-flavored Builder in the same scene would see driftwood economics, joinery options, structural lumber. The character node is not monolithic.
3. **The bear at the river-bend is a Builder problem.** Carrion Chains denied a non-combat character a material reserve for five days. Predator pressure on Builders is denial, not direct attack — but it is real pressure.
4. **Mosquitoes are a productivity tax, not an event.** They modulate every hour of summer-tundra outdoor work. The work cadence inverts to a night-side block; sleep requires blackout. These are not anomalies; they are the biome's signature.
5. **The trade-off matrix at Day 4 is the Run-06 equivalent of Ren's "bow vs rifle" cost choice in Run 01.** Three paths, each with specific costs in the materials × time × novel-technique-risk axes. No path is dominant. The character chose B by reading his own skill profile, not by raw optimality.
6. **Cold-onset race held as the design's pressure model.** No Storyteller arc was needed. The seasons did the work — mosquito decline at Day 70, first frost at Day 71, sun lowering through Days 65-78. **§2 Mechanical Behavior in plain operation.**
