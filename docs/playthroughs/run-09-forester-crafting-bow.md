# Run 09 — Forester, Forest, Late Summer (Bow-Craft Arc)

Date of run: 2026-05-10
Status: Day 30 in-game, alive, signature-tier selfbow delivered. Two staves discarded en route.
Run author: William + Claude (design exercise)

## Setup

- **Character**: "Eira" (placeholder name) — Forester node, bowyer sub-node mastered
- **Biome**: Forest, late summer (mid-August equivalent)
- **Weather at start**: Warm/dry, 24°C, light westerly; sap finished spring rise — hardwoods at peak structural moisture-set
- **Scenario**: 30-day deliberate craft expedition. Established camp at the inside of a south-facing bend, mature mixed hardwood stand (yew pockets, black locust, hickory, ash, hazel, basswood)
- **Time at start**: Mid-morning Day 1

### Character background

Eira, late-20s, second-generation woodworker. Family workshop; her uncle was a master bowyer in the regional tradition. Her hands learned drawknife and rasp before pencil. She is here to make a bow she would stake her uncle's name on. This is not a survival expedition; it is a craft pilgrimage.

### Starting kit

Knife, hatchet, **drawknife**, awl (3 sizes), cabinet scraper, hand drill, files (small/medium/coarse), sandstone block, beeswax cake, dry hide-glue chunks, cast-iron glue pot, brush. **Folding tillering jig** (uprights, cradle, pulley — sets up in 10 min). 10m linen cordage, 5m raw sinew. Sharpening stone, leather strop. Tarp, bedroll, fire kit. 14 days food.

### Compendium grants at start

- **Expert**: woodwork (split/rive/drawknife/scrape/haft), tree-species ID, bow tillering, bark cordage, hide-glue rendering, finish chemistry
- **Practiced**: forager (local edibles, basic herbalism), animal-glue sourcing, trapper baseline
- **Novice**: tracking, predator-reading, bowhunting (she SHOOTS well — she tests every bow she builds — but has never stalked deer for meat)
- **Glimpsed**: weather signs, large-fauna behavior

### Skill node

Forester — woodwork primary, bowyer sub-node mastered; forager secondary; trapper baseline.

### Goal

A signature-tier selfbow. Supporting: cordage spool ~15m, bowstring + spare, bow case, 12 arrows, finishing applied. Return Day 30 or earlier.

## Days 1-3 — Stave selection

### Beat 01 — Day 1, the stand survey

Eira sets a low camp under a beech ~80m from a creek. Then walks. She is not hunting; she is **reading trees**.

**§14.13 saliency** fires hard. Same forest renders differently for her than for a Hunter:

- Bark patterns indicating past-storm spiral grain — her eye reads the spiral immediately
- A dark hollow in a beech ~3m up, irregular edges — **fungal entry**, decades of internal rot; novice sees a knothole and nothing else
- A yew leaning slightly off prevailing wind — **compression wood on the underside** weakens what would be the bow's belly. She walks past it
- A hazel stand — too thin for primary, but logs the spot for arrow shafts
- A basswood patch — marks two trees for cordage retting later

Finds nothing she wants on Day 1.

**Design observation**: bowyer-tier saliency is *negative* selection more than positive. The novice would fell the leaning yew, drag it home, then discover the compression flaw at rough-out. Eira rejects in seconds. Opposite-direction utility of the same §14.13 shader principle.

### Beat 02 — Day 2, the candidate

Walks 4km in a new arc. A **black locust** ~6m tall, single trunk for ~3m before first branch — straight, no twist, regular bark plates, no fungal sign. §14.13 Expert: clean ring spacing visible in bark cracks → slow, consistent growth. She circles three times. Pulls a small bark patch from a hidden side — wood creamy yellow, even, no microbial darkness. Sniffs. No sour smell. **Sound wood.**

She marks the tree but does not fell. **Felling window matters by species.** Late summer is, for locust, her judgment: sap set, moderate moisture, predictable cure — better than spring (too wet, prone to checking) or winter (hardest, slowest).

### Beat 03 — Day 3, the fell

Returns at first light. Controlled directional fell — notch on fall side, back-cut slightly above the hinge. Tree comes down clean into a gap she had pre-identified. **No shock fracture along the trunk.** Stump inspection: ~30 rings in 5cm, even — slow dense growth, ideal.

Splits the lower 2m of trunk lengthwise with hatchet + carved hazel wedges. Yields **four staves** roughly 5×5×180cm. **§14.13 reads each split face for grain runout** — three clean, one shows grain diving toward the surface near one end (would weaken the limb tip). That one becomes arrow stock.

Seals the ends of three primary staves with beeswax (slows end-checking during cure). Carries two to camp; cache-hangs the third in a tree near the fell site.

End of Day 3: three primary staves cut and sealed, retting sites marked, arrow stock identified. Body grade-A, food 12 of 14.

## Days 4-7 — The cure problem

### Beat 04 — Day 4, the master's accelerated cure

The unavoidable question: **green vs cured.** Freshly cut locust at full thickness cures over ~12 months. Eira has 27 days.

**§14 master signature ability**: rough out to near-dimension while green, seal the ends, then accelerate cure by reducing cross-section. A 5×5cm billet dries in ~12 months; a 4×3cm rough-out in ~3-4 weeks; a 3×2cm near-final shape in ~2 weeks with airflow. **The novice does not know to rough out before drying** — they cure the full billet, then find checks running halfway through at shape time.

~3 hours of drawknife + hatchet. Brings the stave from 5×5 to ~4×3. Re-seals ends. Hangs in dappled shade ~2m up, oriented across-wind.

**Needs surfaced**:
- N51 — Per-species felling window
- N52 — Cure time as f(cross-section, humidity, airflow, species). Naive "wait 30 days" doesn't reward the rough-out-before-drying decision
- N53 — Master accelerated-cure as signature. §14 caps (15-30%) are on *performance*; this signature compresses *process time*. Worth design conversation.

### Beat 05 — Days 5-7, parallel work

Day 5: rough-outs the second stave. Afternoon: strips inner bark from two marked basswoods (~4m × 30cm sheets per tree, long ribbons). Submerges in a creek pool weighted with rocks — **retting**, controlled bacterial breakdown of pectin. ~2 weeks summer water; checks Day 19.

Day 6 evening: cuts ~24 hazel arrow shafts (~85cm each). Bundles, hangs upside-down sheltered. Thin cross-section cures fast — ~1 week to workable.

Day 7: checks staves. One has a small end-side check beginning; caught early, she re-waxes. Forages chanterelles + wild garlic she IDs confidently.

**§14.13 saliency for foraging runs alongside the woodwork** — same shader system, different domain. The Hunter from Run 01 would have walked past these mushrooms.

## Days 8-14 — First tillering attempt + the crysal

### Beat 06 — Day 8, on the jig

First-roughed stave to the jig — ~3 weeks effective cure. Wood feels right, sounds right tapped.

Marks the **back** (faces away from archer, takes tension): outermost ring closest to true. Marks the **belly** (faces archer, takes compression): opposite face. Drawknife shapes limbs: taper from ~4cm at the riser to ~1.5cm at the tips. Symmetrical. **Leaves belly thicker than final — tillering reduces from belly side, never from back.**

Mounts on jig. First pull: ~10cm draw. **§14.13 saliency at bowyer-Expert tier**: she sees which inches of which limb are bending and which are stiff. Novice reads "the limbs are bending" and stops. Master reads the **distribution of bend along each limb's length**.

Right limb: **hinging** at mid-limb (one spot doing too much). Left limb: **stack** — bending near the grip, tips too stiff. Chalks both.

### Beat 07 — Days 9-10, scrape cycle and failure

Scrapes belly with cabinet scraper. Tiny shavings. Pulls string. Re-reads. Scrapes more. This is the loop. **A master spends days on tillering, not hours.**

Day 9 evening, ~6 hours in: right-limb hinge under control. Left more even.

Day 10 morning: draws to ~15cm and ladders. Closer. Then — **a small crackling from the right limb mid-limb.** She releases.

Tiny lifted splinters on the **belly** side of the right limb. **Crysal** — compression fracture. The hinge had been deeper than she read; the early hinging caused premature compression damage; now even at 15cm the belly is failing.

The stave is done. She sets it aside. Logs the lesson: **the hinge formed during rough-out, not during tillering** — too much wood at one spot with the drawknife on Day 8 before getting to the scraper.

**Need surfaced**:
- N54 — Crysal as discrete bow-failure mode (belly compression fracture), distinct from back-failure (tension split, often explosive), hinge (geometry, recoverable early), and knot blowout (point failure under draw)

### Beat 08 — Days 11-14, second stave + cordage

Day 11: pulls the second stave. **§14 mastery-novelty distinction**: post-mastery context, so the novelty multiplier does not fire. But her *attention* is sharper after the failure — she rough-outs more conservatively, leaving more wood on the belly than strictly needed. Rather scrape more later than crysal again.

Day 12: second stave on the jig at 10cm. Curve more even than the first's. Left limb slightly stiff at inner third; scrapes carefully.

Day 13: 20cm draw. Curve good. Lets the wood rest overnight. **Real bowyer's discipline** — wood "sets" between sessions; small inconsistencies surface after a rest that aren't visible during work.

Day 14 morning: 25cm draw. Right limb's outer third shows faint follow (slight set, doesn't return fully straight). Acceptable for this draw weight; means the limb is at mechanical limit. Final bow will draw to 26 inches, not 28.

**Cordage in parallel**: basswood retted to slipping-free. Removes, drains, scrapes loose outer layers with a wooden scraper. ~3 hours yields ~12m of clean fiber. **Twisting**: two strands twisted clockwise, plied counter-clockwise. Opposing twists lock the cord — non-negotiable. **§14 grade A cordage** holds ~25kg static before failure.

**Need surfaced**:
- N55 — Cordage multi-axis grade: tensile / wear / elasticity. Bowstring needs low elasticity; snares need wear endurance; camp lashing prioritizes tensile. One "cordage stat" is too thin.

## Days 15-22 — Bowstring, glue, long tiller, arrows

### Beat 09 — Day 15, the bowstring

Bowstring is not cordage. Mechanical demand: **inelastic** (energy to the arrow, not stretch), **wear-resistant** at serving and nocks, **dimensionally stable** through humidity.

Chooses **linen** for the body (kit-supplied — long even fiber, low stretch under hard twist) with **sinew serving** at loops and center. **Flemish twist**: 12 strands, two bundles of 6, each twisted then plied. Tapered locked loops. Sinew wraps at loops and center-serving. ~4 hours. Test-stretches against a beam to set the twist.

Output: **grade A+ bowstring**, rated to ~50# draw. **Spare**: simpler 10-strand reserve, less serving (~2 hours).

### Beat 10 — Days 16-18, long tiller + glue prep

Tillering progression:
- Day 16: 22cm / ~28#. Heat-bends the right limb tip with steam over the fire + a wooden form. **Master signature, second instance** — a novice would not attempt heat-bending; they'd accept the asymmetry or break the limb.
- Day 17: 24cm / ~36#. Curve holding. No new sets.
- Day 18: 26cm / ~42#. **Tillering complete.**

In parallel, **glue prep**: dry hide-glue chunks into the cast-iron pot with water. **Slow heat over coals, never boiling** — boiling destroys the protein. ~6 hours intermittent attention. Strains through linen scrap, cools to working gel. Grade-A.

Also makes a small batch of **pine pitch glue** (resin + charcoal + beeswax binder) as a field-repair backup — faster, more brittle in cold, complementary failure mode.

Day 18 evening: strings the bow for the first real time (not on the jig), draws to 26", holds. **The bow feels right.** Releases unloaded — no string slap, no limb torque. She breathes out.

### Beat 11 — Days 19-22, arrows + bow case

Day 19: Day-7 hazel shafts cured. Selects the 16 straightest, trims to ~74cm. **Straightens each by warm-pressure over a low flame at visible bends — master signature, third instance.** A novice cannot reliably eyeball a shaft and correct it. 12 pass final; 4 become spares.

Day 20: **bone arrow points.** Knaps from a deer femur brought as craft material — splits to slivers, grinds to point on sandstone, hafts into split shaft tips with sinew wrap + hide glue. Evening: snares a grouse, plucks wing feathers, splits along central rib, trims to three-fletch sections (~10cm × 12mm). Hafts with sinew + glue (sinew shrinks tight as it dries — locks the fletching). Three feathers per arrow, helical for spin.

Day 21: **nocks.** Awl + knife cuts a string-groove at the rear of each shaft — square to axis. Sinew wrap reinforces the nock area against splitting on hard release.

Day 22: **bow case.** Splits a small ash sapling, drawknife-shapes two thin half-shells into a clamshell around the unstrung bow. Wraps outside with water-soaked rawhide (cached deer-leg scrap) — rawhide shrinks as it dries, locks the case. Lined with smoke-cured rabbit fur from a Day 20 snare. Grade-A case.

## Days 23-30 — Finish, test, third discard, pack out

### Beat 12 — Days 23-25, finish

Final shaping: rounds the belly slightly, eases the back's edges (an edge concentrates stress on back fibers — risk of tension fracture). **Three coats of warm linseed oil**, deep-rubbed with linen scrap, dried overnight between coats. Final layer: **beeswax**, warmed, deep-rubbed, soft-cloth buffed.

Locust's creamy-yellow takes on faint amber under the wax. Grain visible, raised slightly. Signs the inside of the riser with her awl — three small marks. Her uncle's tradition.

### Beat 13 — Days 26-27, range testing

Sets a thick-pad straw target against a fallen log. Paces ranges.

- 20m: groups within ~10cm after 6 ranging arrows
- 30m: groups within ~25cm
- 40m: groups widen to ~40cm; arrow speed drops visibly

**Effective hunting range, her judgment**: 35m for clean vital-zone shots, 25m high-confidence. Matches §15 bow range (30-60m).

**§14 multi-axis grading, master vs novice bow**:

| Axis | Eira's bow (master) | Novice over-built |
|---|---|---|
| Draw weight | 42# at 26" | Often 50-60# (crude) or 25-30# (under-built) |
| Draw length | 26" stable | Variable, often inconsistent |
| Arrow speed (proxy) | ~165 fps (clean energy transfer) | 130-150 fps (energy lost to limb torque, string stretch, set) |
| Accuracy variance | ~10cm at 20m | ~30-50cm at 20m |
| Durability (shot count) | 5000+ before serious follow | 500-1500 before set deepens unusable |
| Aesthetic finish | grade A+ | grade C-D |

**The novice's crude over-built bow can win on raw draw weight.** Eira's does not. But on every other axis — energy efficiency, accuracy, durability, smoothness — the master's product wins. **§1 No Dominant Strategy intact within the craft chain**: more draw weight is not always better. A well-tillered 42# bow outshoots a crude 55# in every meaningful field application.

### Beat 14 — Day 28, the third stave attempt (and discard)

Retrieves the cached third stave Day 26. Rough-outs Days 27-28 as a spare bow for the trip back.

During Day 28 rough-out: finds **a deep insect tunnel ~40cm from one end**, hidden under apparently sound surface, running ~5cm into the wood across the planned belly thickness.

**She does not finish the stave.** Tries to convert to a shorter horse-bow geometry; the inclusion is too central. Sets aside as firewood. **A master tries and discards. This is the craft chain being honest.**

Final yield: 1 of 3 staves became the deliverable. **Honest ratio.**

### Beat 15 — Days 29-30, pack out

Day 29 inventory: selfbow in case; 12 hazel arrows in birchbark quiver; 15m basswood cordage on spool; primary string strung + spare in pack; ~8m unused sinew; jar of rendered hide-glue (beeswax-sealed); wax-paper packet of pine-pitch glue; tool roll.

Leaves the failed first stave (the crysal) propped against a tree near camp with a small linen tag — a marker for a future bowyer who might find it and learn from the scar. Her uncle's tradition.

Day 30: hikes out at dawn. ~4 hours to trailhead. Body grade-A. **She survives.**

## What did NOT end Eira — cascade-near-misses

A craft run is not zero-risk. Four triggers Eira navigated:

1. **Day 16 splinter** — deep drawknife splinter in right palm, pulled cleanly, washed + wild garlic poultice (Forager Practiced). Did not infect. **If ignored, this is the same cascade shape that killed Tova in Run 03.** Difference: Eira's Forager herbalism let her treat preemptively; Tova's Builder-novice plant-ID couldn't.
2. **Hide-glue fumes** — Days 16-17, working hot glue. Kept pot downwind. No respiratory issue. A character with known sensitivity (possible per-character trait) could have had a problem.
3. **Day 28 hatchet near-slip** — splitting the third billet, the hatchet glanced off a hidden knot. Blade passed her left thigh by ~10cm. **Same mechanic that killed Tova (Run 03 N40), reproduced here without injury** because Eira's bowyer-tier saliency reads grain patterns and her stance was deliberate.
4. **Food gap** — Days 16-18 she was so focused on tillering + glue she neglected foraging. By Day 18 the kit was down to 4 days. **Forager-Practiced let her rebuild Days 19-21** with mushroom + grouse + hare + foraged greens. **Without Forager secondary, she'd have hit food crisis by Day 22** mid-craft.

None killed her this time. **They could.**

## Architecture stress-test

- ✅ **§1 No Dominant Strategy within the craft chain.** Master's bow does not dominate on all axes (raw draw weight can favor crude over-built). Master wins on accuracy, energy efficiency, durability, finish. Tradeoffs preserved.
- ✅ **§1 at the weapon-class level.** Eira's 42# / 26" / ~165 fps bow hunts deer-class game at <40m. It cannot stop a charging bear or reach a moose at 100m. §15's rifle would, with the sound/ammo/weight costs. **Bow's gifts: stealth, ammo-from-arrows, craft-chain integration, MP neutrality.**
- ✅ **§2 Mechanical behavior throughout.** The crysal was not a hidden-table roll — it was the consequence of Day 8 rough-out leaving the right limb under-strong at one specific spot. Observable, debuggable post-hoc.
- ✅ **§14 multi-axis grading.** 6 quality axes; master wins on 5 and ties/slightly loses on 1. Output reads as observably better — finish, sound, balance, draw smoothness — not "a normal bow with hidden +stats."
- ❗ **§14 above-baseline cap (15-30%) needs clarification.** Eira's 20m accuracy variance (~10cm) vs novice (~30-50cm) is more than a 30% width delta. The cap likely applies per-input-axis (wood × tillering × arrow × fletching × shooter), stacking multiplicatively in final output. **Worth design conversation**: per-input or per-aggregate?
- ✅ **§14 signature abilities verified.** Three this run: (1) accelerated cure via pre-cure rough-out, (2) heat-bend limb correction, (3) eyeball shaft-straightness with warm-pressure. A novice cannot do these regardless of equipment.
- ✅ **§14.13 saliency in a non-combat domain.** Bowyer's eye rejected three trees in seconds (compression-wood lean, fungal entry, spiral grain). **Negative-selection mode** is novel — prior runs tested positive-recognition (cougar sharpens against brush, onion catches eye); this run shows saliency as rejection. Worth explicit acknowledgment in §14.13.
- ✅ **§15 production-end verified.** Eira's bow IS a §15 weapon. Properties (42# / 26" / ~165fps / 30-60m / 5000+ shots / arrow-recoverable / quiet) map cleanly. **The matrix is downstream of the craft chain.** Master-built and starter occupy the same matrix row but are mechanically distinct.
- ✅ **§14.12 Field Notes honored by absence.** No death-trigger fired. Framework respected by *not* generating an entry on a survival arc — Field Notes are death-only.

## Design inventory

### Structures surfaced

- **Folding tillering jig** (portable) — uprights, cradle, pulley + draw block. Bowyer-specific. Possibly a craftable.
- **Drying rack** (improvised) — staves hung in dappled shade, oriented across-wind. Could become a built structure with humidity-control variants (smoke-vented drying hut would let a master accelerate cure further).
- **Retting pool** — natural creek pool weighted with rocks. Not a structure — a *site selection*. Should the game model "retting-suitable water"?
- **Outdoor glue-rendering setup** — small cast-iron pot, low coals, wind-position discipline.

### Recipes surfaced

- **Selfbow** (locust/yew/hickory/black locust) — stave-selection → fell → rough-out → cure → tiller → finish. **The recipe is a process tree, not a single craft action.** Multi-day tillering loop.
- **Cordage (basswood inner bark)** — strip → ret (~14 days summer water) → scrape → twist-and-ply
- **Bowstring (Flemish twist)** — 12-strand linen laid, sinew-served at loops + center
- **Spare bowstring** — 10-strand simpler architecture
- **Arrows** — shaft select → cure → straighten → nock cut → fletch (sinew + glue) → point haft (bone, knapped)
- **Hide glue** — chunks → slow render → strain → cool to gel
- **Pine pitch glue** — resin gather → melt with charcoal + beeswax → cool to working block
- **Bow case** — split sapling shells → rawhide wrap → fur lining
- **Finish (oil + wax)** — three coats linseed → beeswax topcoat

### Materials

Black locust (primary stave). Yew (gold standard, identified, not selected this run). Basswood inner bark (cordage). Linen + sinew (bowstring). Hazel (arrow shafts). Bone (deer femur — arrow points). Grouse wing feathers (fletching). Hide glue, pine resin, beeswax, linseed oil. Rawhide scrap (case binding). Rabbit fur (case lining).

### Tool wear surfaced

Drawknife dulled after Day 14 — honed + stropped, 30 min. Hatchet chipped on Day 28 knot strike — re-ground on sandstone, 45 min. Cabinet scraper burr re-rolled twice during tillering — 5 min each.

**Need surfaced**:
- N56 — Tool wear use-type dependence. Drawknife dulls faster on dry hardwood than green. Hatchet survives splits better than chops on knot-heavy stock. Wear model should be use-dependent, not uniform decay.
