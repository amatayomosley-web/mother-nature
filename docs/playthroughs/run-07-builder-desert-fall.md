## Days 1–3 — site selection, soil testing, the arroyo question

### Beat 01 — Day 1, the arroyo wall as foundation

Selene drops in at mid-morning. The arroyo is broad here — ~12m floor-width, walls ~6m high, south face. She's selected the south wall partly for winter sun and summer shade, partly because it's the lee side against the prevailing west wind that funnels down the wash.

**§14.13 saliency** fires hard for Mason work, soft for everything else:

- The wall stone reads vividly to her — she can see at a glance where natural shelves and joint-planes will accept a built course; where the rock is friable (sandstone slabs) versus competent (basalt outcrops higher up). A novice would see "rock face." She sees a partial foundation she didn't have to lay.
- Soil at the wall base: she crouches, palms a handful, rolls it. The **ribbon-test fires automatically**. Soil ribbons cleanly to ~5cm before breaking — **medium-clay, sand-supported, draining**. Adobe-grade, but margin not generous. She'd want better clay for full structural adobe walls.
- Drainage signs: a faint debris line ~30cm up the south wall — old flood-mark. The wash has run here, not recently, but it has run. **§14.13 desert saliency fires on the debris line** because she's looking for it. A novice mason would not.
- Plants on the wall ledge above: a single ocotillo, mostly leafless this far past summer. Mesquite scrub thicker downstream toward where the wash widens. **Cottonwood-class trees: zero.** No structural lumber on site. Junipers ~400m upslope, dead-standing visible.

She paces the wall ~80m in each direction. The site she wants is a slight notch in the wall, ~3m wide, where the rock face is recessed and the floor is gravelly-flat. The notch gives her two anchor walls (the rock itself) and shelter from the funnel-wind.

**Decision tree on construction approach, surfaced explicitly:**

- **Cliff-face dugout** (excavate back into the wall) — free shelter, but the sandstone here is friable and the upper basalt may not span. Risky without test cores. Excavation cost: high.
- **Full adobe** (mud + straw bricks, sun-cured) — what she trained on. Cost: water-heavy, sun-cure slowing now that fall sun is weaker.
- **Dry-stone** (no mortar) — fast, no water input, but drafty and cold-loss high; weatherproofing problematic against any wind-driven rain.
- **Rammed earth / cob** (wet mix, formed in place, no drying-on-rack delay) — high water input, slower than adobe blocks in some respects but tolerant of bad clay.
- **Mortared stone** — requires mortar. Lime is the big question. Gypsum nodules in nearby strata might yield gypsum plaster. Lime would require limestone + a kiln burn (~3 days fuel for one batch). In a wood-scarce biome, lime is the single hardest input.
- **Hybrid** — stone footing courses (no water), adobe upper courses (water, but only what walls need above the footing). Roof: open question.

She doesn't commit yet. Day 1 ends with a rough camp under the tarp on a gravel rise out of the wash bottom (flood discipline — never sleep IN the channel), small fire of mesquite scrub, the wall mapped in her head, soil samples bagged for ribbon-testing properly tomorrow.

**Body state end of Day 1**: warmth fine, hunger 80% (light first-day load), water 75% (already 1.5L used — desert load is real), tiredness 60%.

### Beat 02 — Day 2, soil + water economy lockdown

Dawn. She works the cool hours hard. Walks the 800m up-wash to the spring before the heat. **The spring**: a seep in a fractured basalt joint at the base of a side-wall, with a small natural basin that holds maybe 20L when fresh. Flow: slow trickle, maybe 4L/hr. **§14.13 saliency** marks the basin's water quality — clear, no biofilm, mineral sediment in the bowl. Safe to drink direct per her Practiced water-reading.

She brings back 7L (filled bag — left 1L empty for carrying-ease on the return). Round trip: ~50 minutes including fill-wait. Calorie cost: real. **This is the desert constraint locking in**: every construction day will trade against a water-haul day.

Back at site, she lays out the soil-test grid. Five samples from five points along the build face. Ribbon-test each:

- Sample A (right at notch, where she'd build) — medium-clay, sand-supported. Decent.
- Sample B (10m east, lower in wash) — sandier, breaks at 2cm ribbon. **Would crumble in walls.** A novice picking this spot would be looking at a flash-flood failure point.
- Sample C (15m west, higher up bank) — clayey, ribbons to 7cm but shrinks visibly on drying (cracks). **Will crack adobe blocks.** Needs sand-tempering.
- Sample D (in the wash bottom) — silty, totally unsuited (high organic). Ignore.
- Sample E (upper bank above old flood line) — best of the five, ribbons to 8cm clean. **Adobe-grade.** Worth the haul up the bank.

She marks sample E as the source. Haul distance: ~30m + ~3m vertical. Manageable.

**Decision crystallizes**: hybrid construction. **Stone footing courses (dry-laid, mortared above ground level)**, **adobe-block upper walls (sample-E clay + sand-temper + straw)**, **roof TBD** — depends on whether she can pull enough mesquite/juniper for joists.

**Water budget for construction**: she does a back-of-envelope. Adobe needs roughly its own volume in mixing-water during fabrication; sun-cured blocks need none after. For a small one-room shell (~2.5m × 3m × 2m walls, ~15m³ of wall envelope, accounting for stone footing taking lower 0.5m and the rock-wall anchoring two faces), she's looking at **~600L total mixing water over the build**, spread across roughly 25 mixing-days. That's ~24L/day on mixing days. Her 8L bag means **3 round trips to spring per mixing day**. Plus drinking, cooking, washing.

**This is the desert constraint locking in for real**: she will spend ~3 hours a day on water-haul, not building, on every adobe-mixing day.

**Body state end of Day 2**: water 70%, hunger 75%, tiredness 65%. She drinks 3L over the day, sweats it back. Eats two flatbreads + dried meat strip. Sleeps grade A in the cool dry night.

**Need surfaced**:

### Beat 03 — Day 3, the lime question

She wants to know if she has lime access. Mortared stone is enormously stronger than dry-stack against any seismic or flood-undermine event. But lime requires:

1. **Limestone or other calcium carbonate source.** Walks ~600m up the side drainage to a strata transition she noted yesterday. **§14.13 saliency**: she sees pale layers in the upper sandstone face — possibly carbonate cemented (sandstone with calcite matrix), possibly true limestone. She breaks a fist-sized chunk with the mason hammer. **Vinegar test from her testing kit**: light fizz. Carbonate-bearing, but mixed with quartz sand — calcite content maybe 20–30%. **Yield as lime: marginal.**

2. **Fuel for a burn.** A lime burn requires ~900°C sustained for ~6–8 hours per batch. Mesquite is a good hot fuel but burns fast; she'd need maybe 60–80kg of dry mesquite for a single batch yielding ~3–5kg quicklime. **Mesquite available on-site: maybe 200kg standing dead within haul range, total.** Burning a third of her construction firewood reserve on one lime batch is a steep trade.

3. **Gypsum alternative.** She scouts the upper ledges. Selene finds **gypsum nodules** in a marl layer ~50m east of the build site. Gypsum plaster ("plaster of Paris" class) burns much cooler — ~150°C, a few hours, single mesquite-coal fire would do it. Yield is softer than lime and **gypsum plaster is water-soluble** — bad outdoors, but fine for interior finish on walls already protected by adobe + roof overhang.

**Decision tree on mortar**:
- **No mortar (dry-stone footing only, adobe above)** — simplest. Footing stones load on stones; adobe block weight clamps the joint. Adequate for low-wall stability in non-seismic, low-flood ground. **Risk**: if a flash flood undermines a footing stone, the dry-stack relies on friction; one moved stone propagates.
- **Mud mortar between stones** — clay + sand + water as a poor-man's mortar. Holds in dry climate, fails when wet. **Risk**: erosion in the lower courses where any rain-runoff hits.
- **Gypsum plaster as interior parge** — accept exterior dry-stone or mud-mortar, parge the interior wall with gypsum. Improves draft-sealing and internal weatherproof. **Feasible.**
- **Lime mortar** — best, but the fuel + carbonate-content + burn risk says no for a single-builder 45-day window.

She picks **dry-stone footing + mud-mortar between footing stones + adobe block upper + gypsum interior parge**. Roof TBD.

**§1 No Dominant Strategy check** — the hybrid path is the right answer here. A novice picking adobe-only would crumble at the footing under any wash event. A novice picking dry-stone-only would freeze at night through wall drafts. A novice picking cliff-face dugout would discover the friable sandstone collapse-zone the hard way. **All three single-strategy paths cost something a hybrid avoids.**

**Need surfaced**:
- N49 — Mortar chemistry as a deferred-but-real subsystem: lime vs gypsum vs mud vs none. Builder mastery here is real; novices have nothing to read with.

### Beat 06 — Days 9–11, adobe block fabrication begins

- Sand: 60%

Day 10: pours 16 blocks (4 batches). Water: 11L mix + 3L drink. **3 spring trips.**

### Beat 08 — Day 12 evening, the flood arrives

**16:30**: she hears it before she sees it. Low rumble, getting louder. She's been listening for half an hour. **Not thunder — water.**

A ~1m wall of brown water enters her stretch of wash at ~17:00, debris-laden — mesquite branches, gravel, a small uprooted yucca, a dead snake. The wash is a moving floor of mud. She watches from the high bench.

The wave depth at her build site: **about 80cm** — well over her uncured-block height (8cm) had she left them on the gravel slope, **over her stone-footing height (50cm) but not over the first adobe course (which is dry-stack at this stage, not yet mortared by the cure-pending blocks)**.

The flood holds for ~40 minutes at peak, then drops fast — ~20cm an hour. By 19:30 the channel is a slick of wet mud + standing pools, not flowing.

She descends at dusk to inspect. **Damage assessment**:
- Stone footing course: held. Mud-mortar joints somewhat eroded on the wash-facing side, but stones haven't moved. **Grade B status; needs re-pointing.**
- Lower adobe course (only 2 courses tall, ~16cm): **completely wet, partially eroded on the upwash face**. The blocks she'd laid yesterday — not yet fully bonded — have softened. Two of them have slumped. **Grade D status on that section; needs partial rebuild.**
- Curing rack on slope: empty (she moved it).
- Bedroll/tools: dry (high bench).
- Food cache: dry (niche).
- Her water bag: she'd left a 4L bag at the workspace — gone, washed downstream.

**Net loss**: ~6 hours of rebuild work on the adobe lower course. ~4L of water-bag capacity (annoying — needs to ration the remaining 8L bag tighter for spring trips). No catastrophic loss.

**§14.13 saliency on the post-flood site**: she reads the debris line on the rock wall — **new debris line ~95cm above floor, 15cm higher than the OLD debris line she'd spotted on Day 1.** This was a moderately bigger flood than the historical average she'd designed for. She updates her mental model: the upper bench (where she moved blocks) is the actual safe zone, not just above the old debris line.

**Decision**: she abandons any thought of building lower than the new debris line in mud-mortar. The first 1m of the wall (originally 0.5m stone + 0.5m of mortar-transition + adobe) becomes **all-stone, dry-laid with mud-pointed joints only above the new debris line.** First course of adobe block now starts at 1m elevation. **Roof span increases by 0.5m as a result.**

**This is the design's commitment to mechanical consequence**: the flood happened because the watershed and the cell-track aligned, not because the run "needed drama." The rebuild cost is real, the layout change is real, the lesson sticks because she observed the new debris line.

**Needs surfaced**:

- N53 — Post-flood debris-line update as in-fiction saliency: the same wall reads differently after a flood event. The novice didn't see the line at all; the expert reads the new one immediately.

### Beat 11 — Days 25–28, the roof decision

This is the hardest design problem of the build. **In a wood-scarce biome, how does Selene close the roof?**

**Decision tree on roof**:

- **Domed earth roof** (adobe arch + earth fill on top, structural masonry only). Possible if the span is short and the curve is right. **Span here: ~3m.** A 3m adobe dome at her wall thickness (40cm) and the available block strength is **at the marginal end of her Mason Expert confidence**. The mortar would need to be stronger than her mud-mortar — gypsum or lime. **Risk: collapse during construction or first wet season. Catastrophic if she's inside.**
- **Mesquite-joist flat roof** (small mesquite logs as joists, brush + adobe layer on top). Requires ~12 mesquite logs of ~3.5m × 8cm. **Mesquite that long and straight is rare in this drainage.** She'd have to scout 2–3 days and might find 8 of the 12. **Partial-completion risk.**
- **Juniper-joist** — junipers tend straighter than mesquite but the dead-standing ones she scouted Day 1 are ~400m upslope. Carrying them down is ~30 min/log round trip. ~12 logs × 30 min = 6 hours of haul time over 2 days. Feasible.
- **Hybrid: stone-corbel inset on two sides + reduced-span juniper joists.** Reduces the span the joists must cross. **Mason Expert reads this as best.**

She commits to the **corbel + juniper-joist hybrid**.

Day 25: hikes upslope and inspects the dead-standing juniper grove. **§14.13 saliency** fires on juniper-trunk quality at her domain edge — straight grain, sound core, lateral checks. She marks **9 viable trunks**; her span-target after corbeling reduces from 3m to ~2.2m. She cuts and hauls 4 trunks that day. **Tool wear on the small hatchet is significant** — she's stropping the blade every two trees.

Day 26: hauls 5 more trunks. Tool wear is real: her hatchet is now grade B from grade A start. Her mason hammer has a chip on the striking face from a stone she'd misread on Day 17 — she's been working around it.

Day 27: lays the stone corbel-courses on the wall-tops, projecting ~40cm inward on the two open-face sides. **§14.13 saliency** reads exact stone-orientation for corbel load-bearing. Mud-mortared (low load — gravity holds the corbel by its inset). 8 corbel stones per side, ~30cm × 30cm × 20cm sandstone.

Day 28: places the 9 juniper joists across the corbel courses. **§22 injury risk** — one joist slips off the corbel while she's positioning it, falls toward her. She gets her hatchet up as a deflection. Joist glances off, hits her forearm — **bruise, no break, no laceration**. She rests the arm 20 minutes, continues. **No infection cascade**, but the injury is honestly logged.

**Body state end of Day 28**: water 60%, hunger 65%, tiredness 80%, mild forearm bruise.

## Days 29–35 — roof skin + hearth

### Beat 12 — Days 29–32, roof closure

Days 29–30: lays a brush mat across the joists (mesquite scrub + ocotillo stems + agave-fiber lash points). The mat doesn't need to be load-bearing — just to keep the adobe roof-skin from falling through the joist gaps while it dries.

Days 31–32: lays the **adobe roof-skin** — flat, ~10cm thick, mixed with extra straw + more sand than wall-blocks (for lighter weight + crack-tolerance under thermal cycling). Water cost: ~40L for the full roof. Two days of pour, troweled flat with the broad face of the mason hammer. **Slight outward slope** for runoff toward the wash-facing side.

**Roof curing**: ~5 days in fall sun. Daily check for cracks. Selene **plasters small cracks daily** with a slurry — this is a Mason-baseline maintenance discipline, novice mason would let cracks open and find herself with leaks at the first rain.

Day 32 evening: shell is **structurally complete** but uncured. She continues sleeping on the high bench under tarp another week while the roof finishes.

### Beat 13 — Days 33–35, hearth + smoke routing

**The hearth is the load-bearing decision for habitability.** Without it: cold nights only, no cooking. With it: warmth + cooking but fire-safety risk in earth structure + smoke-routing problem.

**Mason Expert** reads the constraints:
- Hearth must be **on the rock-wall side** (the rock-wall anchor is the safe heat sink; adobe wall on the other side could over-dry and crack from radiant heat).
- **Smoke routing**: she could simply leave a smoke-hole in the roof above the hearth (primitive but works; soot stains the ceiling but doesn't structurally fail). Or build a stone-stack chimney up the rock face. She picks **smoke-hole + stone-rim deflector** — simpler, no chimney engineering, fine for mesquite-coal fires.
- **Fire-pit base**: dry-stack stones in the floor, embedded in the gravel; insulated underneath by a layer of sand. No mortar near the fire (would crack from thermal cycling without lime).

## Architecture stress-test

- ✅ **§1 No Dominant Strategy**: hybrid construction (stone footing + adobe block + corbel + juniper joist + gypsum parge) was the right answer; any single-strategy path (full adobe, dry-stone only, full dugout) had concrete costs that hybrid avoided. The flood proved it — pure-adobe lower wall would've been a worse rebuild; pure-stone roofless walls would've taken winter through them. **No path dominated; every path traded.**
- ✅ **§2 Mechanical Behavior**: the flash flood emerged from real watershed + cell-track inputs, not narrative pacing. Selene's pre-flood discipline was the right call because the sky-signs were real; her saliency-tier on weather (Practiced, not Expert) compensated honestly with construction-discipline. The new debris line is a permanent in-fiction trace she can read later.
- ✅ **§14 Skill Architecture (debuff-removal + saliency)**:
    - **Mason-dominant** saliency fired hard on stone, soil, mortar, joint quality, corbel geometry. Two-domain saliency (Mason + Forager-secondary) rendered simultaneously without overload.
    - **Practiced weather-reading** read the storm correctly enough to act, not tightly enough to forecast — and the design held: the gap was real, mitigation was construction-discipline.
    - **Quality grading (§14.3)**: mesquite-chaff-substituted blocks were grade B; she placed them where grade B sufficed. Continuous-degradation failure mode, no catastrophic-roll required.
- ✅ **§14.13 Saliency**: tested across at least 8 domains (soil, stone, mortar joints, curing blocks, debris-line, weather signs, plant edibility, joist-quality). No floating labels, no glows. Subtle visual weight only. The flood updated her saliency model in-fiction.
- ✅ **§3 Desert biome material list under fall conditions**: mesquite (fuel + chaff fiber), juniper (joist lumber, scarce), ocotillo (brush, scarce), sage (none used), agave (cordage fiber, light water source), prickly-pear (food, water), yucca (food, soap), clay deposits (sample E was the find), sandstone (stone + carbonate-bearing for marginal lime), basalt (footing + corbel), gypsum (plaster, hearth-adjacent fire safety). **All in honest scarcity profile** — nothing magicked into convenience.

- ✅ **§15 combat-adjacent (construction injury)**: joist-fall on Day 28 produced a real bruise without cascade. **Builder injuries are different from Hunter injuries** — tool slip, dropped material, abrasion, repetitive wear; not predator combat. Spec must cover both.
- ❗ **N51 — Flash-flood pre-warning saliency tier**: Mason-Expert ≠ weather-Expert. The Builder must read with what she has. The honest gap is real and the design holds it (no skill-cross-pollination cheat).
