## Days 1–4 — first camp, fire-by-friction as the opening test

### Beat 01 — Day 1, choosing the first rest site

Pell walks 4 hours from trailhead into mixed pine/birch/maple. She has no destination. Her loop, sketched in her notebook, is a rough triangle — three drainages, ~12 km perimeter, 30 days. She picks her first rest by reading deadfall density, water proximity, and wind exposure.

**§14.13 saliency at Survivalist Expert** fires distinctively for shelter-relevant cues — she catches, in one sweep:
- A leaning deadfall pine with a clear lee side (lean-to anchor)
- A stand of basswood (inner-bark tinder source)
- A patch of fluffed cattail down at a marsh ~80m off (premium fire-fluff)
- A spring seep into the small creek (water-source quality signal)

A baseline novice would see "forest." Pell sees four assets, ranked.

**Decision**: lean-to against the deadfall pine. ~2 hours to build, lasts the 2–3 days she'll spend in this drainage.

**Systems invoked**: §14.13 saliency (Survivalist domain) · §13.4 fuel/wood typology · §14 quality grading

### Beat 02 — Day 1 afternoon, the lean-to

Pell's lean-to is not a Builder's lean-to. A Builder would notch ridge poles, square the angles, plan a 7-day weather envelope. Pell's is **expedient** — a single 4m ridge pole lashed to the deadfall and angled to ground, four ribs across, then thatched with whatever shedding material lies within 50m:

- Roof thatch: dead pine boughs (waterproofing) + fern frond layer + leaf mound on top
- Sleeping side: lined with cut dry boughs, ~10cm deep — insulation from ground heat-loss
- Rain pitch: ~45° — sheds water but burns ridge-pole timber faster than a steeper pitch

Builder would do this at grade A, lasting weeks. Pell at Survivalist Expert turns out a **grade B** lean-to in 90 minutes — predictable: drips slightly in heavy rain, will need re-thatching on day 3, won't survive a real storm.

**This is the §14 cross-skill structural rule in action — Survivalist's shelter is everywhere-good (every biome, every condition) but **not best-in-class** in any single condition. Builder beats her in volume + durability; Forester beats her in materials selection; Hunter beats her in concealment. Pell's edge is **she can improvise it in 90 minutes from whatever's in 50m radius**.

### Beat 03 — Day 1 evening, fire-by-ferro (the warm-up)

Pell builds her first fire with the ferro rod. She wants to save the bowdrill for fire-by-friction practice — she has 30 days and intends to drill at least three friction fires before extract.

Ferro technique at Expert: she scrapes 3-4 sparks into a pre-built tinder nest (birchbark scrolls + dry fluffed grass + finer cedar shavings). **Ignition first strike.** A novice's tinder nest is loose and dies; Pell's is sized and layered. The fire takes; she builds it up to coal-bed, cooks half a packed ration over coals, banks the rest.

Notes she writes: *"Lean-to lee side is right. Roof drip-line is wrong by 15cm — adjust tomorrow. Cattail down at 80m is worth a return trip."*

### Beat 04 — Day 2-3, the bowdrill practice

Day 2 morning Pell pulls her bowdrill kit. The kit is **pre-tested at home** — she knows the spindle and fireboard pair already produce embers; the bow tension is set. **This matters mechanically**: the variable that kills most bowdrill attempts in the wild is kit-pair mismatch (wrong wood pairing, spindle too soft, fireboard too hard). Pre-testing eliminates that variable.

She still has to:
1. Cut a fresh notch in the fireboard (40-second cut, knife — quality A, knife is sharp)
2. Form a hand-socket from antler or stone for the spindle top
3. Build the tinder bundle that will catch the ember

Bowdrill, attempt 1: spindle slips out of bow loop at ~30 seconds. **Failure mode: bow tension on the loop was wrong**. She adjusts, re-strings.

Attempt 2: **ember!** Forms in the V-notch around the 50-second mark. She tilts the fireboard, drops the ember into her tinder bundle (fluffed inner bark of dead basswood, breathed on gently, then folded into outer layer of dry fern), and blows.

The ember-to-flame transfer is the moment that kills most bowdrill attempts even after a good ember. The tinder has to be **dry, fluffed, layered fine-to-coarse**. Pell's Expert tier means her tinder bundle is correct without consulting reference — the muscle memory is there.

Flame at ~70 seconds total. **Fire by friction, first attempt successful.**

Day 3 she does it twice more — both at <60 seconds. Mastery is real; her ember-bundle and breath-control are clean. She notes: *"Spindle wear half mm/run. Fireboard at 4 notches; good for 8-10 more uses. Replacement spindle stock: any of the small dead birch under the lean-to."*

**Systems invoked**: §14 mastery + signature ability (Master Survivalist's fire-by-friction is a master-only active skill per §14.5) · §14.4 mastery diminishing returns (her novelty is in successive notch sites and conditions, not the action itself)

### Beat 05 — Day 4, leaving Camp 1

Pell breaks camp Day 4 morning. She doesn't dismantle the lean-to — it stays for the next traveler, or for her own potential return. She caches nothing (mobile-fieldcraft principle: carry-or-lose).

**Camp 1 inventory loss/gain**:
- Loss: ration consumed (~3.5 of 4 days food)
- Gain: 1 cached tinder bundle (extra-dry birchbark scrolls) wrapped in oiled cloth; ~6 cattail-down rolls; 1 replacement spindle (cut from birch on Day 3); 1 grouse caught in cordage snare on Day 3 (~400 cal)
- Repair: re-served the cordage bowstring on the bowdrill bow (5-min re-wrap with paracord inner strands — grade A, no degradation)

Walks 2.5 km east toward the second drainage. Pace: ~3 km/hr at her loaded weight. Reaches new ground by noon.

## Days 5–9 — Camp 2, debris hut, cross-skill repair as the central story

### Beat 06 — Day 5, the debris hut

Camp 2 site selection prioritizes a flatter sleep grade than Camp 1 — Pell wants to test a **debris hut**, which is a fundamentally different shelter shape from a lean-to.

A debris hut is a **body-volume shelter**: a ridge pole supported at one end (notched into a tree fork or set on a forked stick), the other end on the ground; ribs angled down from the ridge; then mounded with **leaf debris** until the mound is at least 1m thick on top. Inside is a tunnel just barely larger than the occupant. The insulation is the mound, not the structure.

**Specific volume-to-body-heat math** (the design principle the debris hut surfaces):
- Body output: ~80W at rest, ~150W active
- Insulation layer (1m of leaves): R-value ~30 (vs sleeping bag R-2 + wool R-1)
- The mound traps heat; the body warms a 1.5m³ inside-volume to ambient + 8°C in calm conditions
- **Failure mode**: if leaf mound is too thin (<60cm anywhere), that section conducts cold straight through. Grade-C debris hut has a cold spot; the sleeper wakes shivering at 03:00.

Pell builds the debris hut over 3 hours. Quality grade A — she knows the volume rule (no wasted space inside; tunnel sized to her body) and the mound rule (1m+ everywhere). She tests the inside; her body warms the volume in ~20 minutes; she sleeps grade-A on Night 5.

**§14.13 saliency at Survivalist Expert**: she catches leaf-mound density variations as a visible quality cue. Her eye treats a thin spot as visually weak — saliency-as-pre-attention, surfacing the failure mode before it becomes one. This is what **field-tested intuition** looks like: not magic vision, but the trained eye picking out the thin spot in the mound the way a Hunter's eye picks out the off-stance bear or a Forager's eye picks the toxic lookalike.

**Flag for §14 spec**: field-tested intuition as Master Survivalist signature ability is plausible — "pre-attention to bad fuel, bad shelter site, bad fire conditions before attempting." This is consistent with §14.5 master-only signature abilities. Mechanically: the saliency shader fires not just on entities (plant, animal, track) but on **structural quality cues** (mound density, fuel wetness, fire-site wind exposure). Worth adding to the §14.13 saliency catalog.

### Beat 07 — Day 6, the first cross-skill repair (knife handle)

The knife handle scales — wood slabs pinned through the tang — were already worn at trip start. Day 6 the pin works partially loose during a heavy carving session (Pell is preparing pot-hangers for tonight's fire). The handle wobbles in her grip.

**Pell's repair options** (this is the §14 cross-skill repair surface):

1. **Repin and re-glue** — would need glue she doesn't carry. Skip.
2. **Lash with paracord inner strands** — wrap the handle scales tight to the tang. Holds well; loses some grip feel.
3. **Re-pin with a brass-rod stand-in from the multi-tool** — she has a small brass rod (the pivot pin from a worn-out section of her multi-tool). Could drive it through the loose pin's hole.
4. **Carve a new handle from green ash** — overkill; takes 2 hours; current scales still mostly intact.

She chooses **option 2 + 3**: drives the brass rod through, taps it flat with a stone (peening), then over-wraps with paracord lashing for redundancy. **20-minute repair.**

**Quality grading per §14**: Pell's knife-handle repair is **Survivalist grade B**. A Builder doing the same repair would do **grade A** (better grip, cleaner peening, the handle would feel original). A Forester would do **grade B+** (better wood-on-wood fit if a carving was involved, less polished metalwork). Pell's repair holds for the remaining 24 days **and is observably uglier than original** — the paracord wrap is visible, the peened rod stands proud by 0.5mm. The function is uncompromised; the aesthetic and grip-feel are.

**The §14 cross-skill repair principle, restated**:
- Survivalist repairs **everything**. Knife handle, axe head, boot seam, snare wire, bowstring, fishing rod tip, pot lid, tarp tear, sleeping bag zip.
- Survivalist's repair quality is **always one tier below the domain specialist**:
  - Builder repairs construction items at A; Survivalist at B
  - Forester repairs bow at A; Survivalist at B
  - Forager repairs herbal poultice at A; Survivalist at B (her remedy will work but at reduced potency)
  - Cook repairs preserved food at A; Survivalist at B (she'll re-smoke but the salt-ratio will be approximate)
- **This is the generalist tier — paid for in non-mastery on any single domain.** Pell is master of no single repair but competent at all of them. This is the **explicit design value** of the Survivalist node and worth preserving.

Pell writes in her notebook: *"Knife handle: grade B repair, paracord-and-brass, will hold to extract. Replace scales when home."*

### Beat 08 — Day 7, water purification chain test

Camp 2 is ~120m from a creek. The creek is **clear and running fast** — Survivalist Practiced water-reading flags it as "low-risk, drink direct with light filter."

But Pell wants to test the full chain. She's writing a notebook entry on what she calls "the four-stage water," and Day 7 is her chance:

**Stage 1 — Cartridge filter** (pump-style, replaceable). She pumps 1L of creek water through her filter, into the small pot. **Time cost: 3 minutes.** Removes large particulate, most bacteria, most protozoa. Doesn't remove viruses (small enough to slip through the 0.2-micron membrane). For most field water in a clean forest creek, this is sufficient.

**Stage 2 — Boil** (3-min rolling). She brings the 1L pot to rolling boil on her existing fire. **Fuel cost: ~150g of dry deadfall.** Kills viruses, bacteria, protozoa — anything biological. Doesn't remove chemicals or sediment.

**Mechanic to surface**: **fuel cost of boiling is real**. Pell on a mobile fieldcraft run who boils every liter is burning ~150g of fuel × 3 liters/day × 30 days = **13.5 kg of dry fuel just for water.** That's gathering time + carry cost (she doesn't carry fuel between camps; she gathers at each camp). On a deeper drainage with thinner deadfall, this becomes the constraint.

**Stage 3 — Chemical (deferred)**. Pell does not carry iodine on this trip — she questions whether iodine is game-canonical or not. **Flag for design**: chemical purification (iodine, chlorine dioxide) is a real backup but adds a discrete consumable. Decision worth making: yes or no. (No design impact from this run; flagging.)

**Stage 4 — Improvised charcoal+sand+gravel filter** (educational, not for stage-1 use). She builds one on the bank using a hollowed birch-bark cone, layered:
- Top: coarse pebbles (settles large debris)
- Middle: fine sand (filters particulate)
- Bottom: charred wood from yesterday's fire (carbon — absorbs some chemicals + improves taste)

Water poured in top, drips out bottom. **Doesn't replace boiling** (charcoal+sand+gravel doesn't kill biological); does improve taste + sediment of cloudy creek water she might draw in a worse stage. **Quality grade B** — a Forester would do this with hollowed cedar (better grain shape) at grade A.

Pell stores 1L treated in her pot, 1L in her canteen, drinks ~500ml at midday.

### Beat 09 — Day 8, the boot-seam repair

A boot seam at the heel starts to separate. Wet ground over Days 5–7 has saturated the wax weatherproofing; the thread itself is fraying.

**Pell's options**:
1. **Re-stitch with sinew** — she has no sinew but can improvise from deer leg-tendon **if** she can field-process one. None handy.
2. **Re-stitch with paracord inner strands** — she has the cord; strand is fine enough for boot-seam re-stitching. Awl required — multi-tool has one.
3. **Patch with leather scrap** — she has none; could harvest from glove leather but that compromises gloves.
4. **Hot-wax re-seal** — she doesn't carry boot wax; could substitute with pine pitch + crushed charcoal (improvised waterproof goo).

She does **2 + 4 in combination**. Re-stitches the worn 4cm of seam with paracord inner strand (awl pierces, paracord follows; ~25 minutes), then heats pine pitch on a flat stone next to the fire, mixes in charcoal dust, paints the new stitching + the original seam liberally. **Quality grade B repair** — a Cobbler (not a Mother Nature node, but a real specialist) would do this at grade A with proper sinew + beeswax. Holds for the trip.

**Cross-skill principle surfaced again**: paracord inner strands are the universal field-repair thread. Pell's 15m of paracord has now contributed strand to: knife-handle lashing (Day 6), boot-seam re-stitch (Day 8), bowdrill bow re-serve (Day 4). Each contribution is ~0.3m of strand. After 24 more days she will have used roughly 6m of the original 15m for repairs. Mobile fieldcraft is paracord-economy.

### Beat 10 — Day 9, leaving Camp 2 + the snare/fishing pivot

Pell has now spent 5 days in drainage 2. She breaks camp Day 9 morning. Food state: 1 day of starting rations remaining + 1 grouse smoked from Day 8 (~600 cal worth) + ~200 cal of foraged late-summer roots (Forager Practiced — dug Day 7).

She's been **net-positive** on calories despite mobile pacing — fieldcraft on cool clear days is energy-efficient, not energy-spendthrift. The drawdown will accelerate when weather turns or terrain steepens.

Walks 2 km south to a third drainage with a small pond. Carries: full gear minus 1 spent water-cartridge (she ran the spare in on Day 7 just to confirm both work — notes the spare is now her primary; she has no second spare).

## Days 10–14 — Camp 3, fishing, the saw failure, gear improvisation

### Beat 11 — Day 10, A-frame tarp + pond fishing

Camp 3 is on the south bank of a small pond, ~30m from water, 15m back from the bank under mixed maple/birch canopy. Wind is from the west; Pell's first decision is **tarp configuration**.

**Tarp configurations Pell knows** (Survivalist Expert in tarp craft):

| Config | When | Shed-rain | Wind-block | Setup time |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A-frame** | even rain, moderate wind | excellent | good (both ends open) | 8 min |
| **Lean-to** | one-sided wind, asymmetric rain | good | excellent (one side fully blocked) | 6 min |
| **Plow-point** | strong wind, drift snow, single anchor | moderate | excellent (cone shape) | 10 min |
| **Diamond** | overhead canopy only, hammock-style | poor | poor | 5 min |

Wind from west + 60% rain probability for Day 11 = **A-frame, ridge running E-W, lower side facing west**. She sets it in 9 minutes. Bedroll inside; pack on the dry-side high end.

### Beat 12 — Day 11-12, fishing the pond

Days 11–12 Pell shifts to **fishing as primary calorie source**. She has line, 6 hooks, a bobber. Bait is improvised from beetles dug out of a rotting log (Forager Practiced).

Fishing is **§14.13 saliency at modest tier** for her — she's Practiced, not Expert. A Forager-Hunter or Trapper-Fisher would see fish-shadows in the pond as visually salient against weed cover; Pell sees them as faint. She catches a small bass on Day 11 (~600 cal); two small bluegills on Day 12 (~250 cal each).

**Fuel economy in mobile fieldcraft** — Day 12 she has to make a new fire (Camp 3 fire is fresh — no banked coals from previous camp). She chooses **ferro rod over bowdrill** for this lighting (faster, less tinder cost). Pell's notebook entry: *"Each move = new fire start. Bowdrill skill staying sharp via daily practice but ferro is the working tool. Bowdrill is the **resilience floor** — what I do when ferro is wet or lost."*

This is the design principle: **fire-by-friction is the resilience floor**, not the daily routine. Daily fire is ferro or matches (efficient). Bowdrill is what's there when efficient methods fail. Worth surfacing in the §14 Survivalist node design: the master fire skill is a **fallback signature**, not a daily action.

### Beat 13 — Day 13, the multi-tool saw breaks

Pell uses the multi-tool's small saw blade to cut a 3cm-diameter birch sapling for a pot-hanger pole. The saw blade has been Used by previous trips; it snaps mid-cut. **Multi-tool now functional minus saw.**

**Repair options**:
1. **Carry the broken tool, work around** — knife handles 90% of saw-work for small cuts anyway. Skip dedicated repair.
2. **Re-pin a replacement blade** — she has no replacement blade; would need to home.
3. **Improvise a saw** from notched bone or improvised stone-flake-edge — high effort, low return; the knife covers cutting needs.

She chooses **option 1**. Tightens the broken saw stub into the multi-tool body (it doesn't extend now but doesn't rattle), files down the broken edge with the multi-tool's small file (preventing further damage), and notes in the book: *"Multi-tool saw broken. Knife covers downstream cuts. Pliers, scissors, file, awl, screwdriver all still functional. Replacement at home."*

**Cross-skill principle**: Pell repairs the **functionality**, not the part. The multi-tool's saw is broken; her workflow can route around it; she repairs the **workflow** rather than the tool. This is a deeper expression of fieldcraft than tool-by-tool repair. It's the signature mode: **don't ship-fix the broken thing if the broken thing isn't needed**. Repair-vs-route is a real design decision.

### Beat 14 — Day 14, gear improvisation — the pot-lid

She wants a lid for her cookpot — she's losing heat (and water to evaporation) on the open boil. She has no lid.

**Improvisation**:
- Selects a thin slab of slate from the creek bank (1cm thick, 8cm across)
- Test-fits across the pot mouth — slate is slightly larger than pot opening
- Files (multi-tool file) the slate edges to size — 12 minutes
- Tests with boil — slate holds, doesn't crack at thermal shock (slow heating; she set it on the rim before boil started)
- **Result**: pot now boils 1L water in 6 minutes instead of 9 — **33% fuel savings.**

**Material**: pot-lid from creek pebble (slate slab). **Quality grade**: A for function (full lid coverage, no leaks); aesthetically rough. This is the kind of gear improvisation that defines fieldcraft as a node — Pell didn't carry a lid because she didn't have one to bring; she made one from the streambed.

**Other gear improvisations Pell has made or could make** (cataloguing for design reference):

- Snare from boot lace (when paracord runs low)
- Fishing weight from creek pebble (drilled with multi-tool awl, threaded on line)
- Pot lid from flat rock (Day 14, just done)
- Pitch-sealed bark cup for water carry (boil-resistant if she lines with pitch)
- Bone needle for sewing (if she field-processes a small game leg-bone)
- Knife scabbard from birch bark (rolled, tied)
- Sleeping pad from packed pine boughs + cattail down stuffing
- Cord from twisted basswood inner bark (when paracord exhausted)
- Char cloth from old cotton scraps (cooked in tin over fire; tinder catalyst)

## Days 15–19 — Camp 4, the wet days, fire-under-rain

### Beat 15 — Day 15, the move + the rain front

Pell leaves Camp 3 Day 15 morning. Weather has shifted — sky overcast since Day 14 evening; barometric pressure dropping (she has no barometer but reads the sky and wind shift). **Rain probability HIGH for next 2-3 days.**

Walks 1.5 km west into a sheltered hollow under heavier conifer canopy — the **rain-aware shelter strategy**. Conifer canopy intercepts ~40% of light rain. Hollow protects from wind.

She arrives Camp 4 just as the first drops fall.

### Beat 16 — Day 15-17, the rain (fire-by-spark under wet conditions)

Days 15–17 are wet — light rain Day 15 evening, heavy rain through Day 16, drizzle Day 17. Total accumulation: ~25mm.

**Pell's fire strategy under wet conditions**:

1. **Tarp configuration**: Plow-point — wind has shifted NW; this configuration single-anchors well and sheds best
2. **Wood selection**: she does NOT use ground-deadfall (saturated). She harvests **standing dead** — small dead branches still attached to trees, which dry by airflow even in rain. **§14.13 saliency** flags standing-dead vs ground-fall — her eye catches the lichen pattern that signals seasoned wood
3. **Tinder under wet conditions**: char cloth (carried in waterproof pouch) + fluffed inner-bark from a freshly-split standing-dead branch (the inside is dry) + fatwood shavings
4. **Fire-by-spark under wet conditions**: ferro rod, **not** bowdrill. Bowdrill is impossible in heavy rain — wet spindle, wet fireboard, wet ember bundle = no ember. Ferro + dry tinder pouch is the right tool.

**The first wet-fire on Day 15 evening takes Pell 8 minutes** — much longer than her dry-fire of 90 seconds. The skill differential is real but the conditions degrade output regardless. Per §14 quality grading: **time-cost extends under hostile conditions** — same Expert skill, different execution time.

**Fuel economy under rain**: she burns more fuel for the same heat, because heat dissipates faster in a wet environment and the wet outer wood she scavenges burns less hot. Day 16 she gathers ~4kg of standing-dead vs her usual 2kg for a 24-hour fire-tend cycle.

**Body state Days 15-17**: warmth 65-70% (lower than usual — wet ground, intermittent drips on her bedroll), hunger 60% (foraging reduced in rain — Pell doesn't risk slippery rocks for fishing access; eats from stored grouse + roots), tiredness 60% (poor-grade sleep under intermittent drip).

### Beat 17 — Day 18, the bowdrill-in-rain attempt (failure as data)

Day 18 morning Pell deliberately attempts a bowdrill fire **for the practice value** — she wants to know what failure looks like under marginal conditions.

She protects her fireboard and spindle under tarp during the rain; both are dry. The bow string is dry. Her ember bundle (assembled earlier under tarp) is dry.

**The variable that beats her**: ambient humidity. The forming ember in the V-notch needs to keep its heat as it grows. Ambient humidity (~95% post-rain) wicks heat from the ember faster than dry conditions. She gets an ember twice — both die in the transfer to bundle. **Failure mode: ember-to-flame transfer fails in high ambient humidity.**

She notebook-logs: *"Bowdrill failure in 95%RH ambient. Ember forms, dies in transfer. Lesson: bowdrill is dry-condition method even with dry tinder if air itself is saturated. Plan for ferro + dry tinder pouch in wet weather."*

This is the kind of empirical learning the design wants — failure as data, not as punishment. **Pell did not need this fire** (she had ferro-fire already lit nearby). She tried bowdrill for the lesson. The result is in the notebook; future Pell or future player of Survivalist will know that bowdrill has a humidity ceiling.

### Beat 18 — Day 19, the saliency-under-deprivation moment

Day 19 is cold-and-damp. Pell has been wet-cold for 4 days. Her body is in mild deprivation: cumulative warmth deficit, slight calorie shortfall (she's eating but at 80% of needs), sleep at grade C for three nights.

She's walking a small ridge above Camp 4, looking for replacement standing-dead fuel. **She sees:**

A bear — black, mid-size — at ~120m, browsing fall berries in a thicket below.

**The Survivalist's predator-reading saliency is NOT Hunter-tier**. Pell catches the bear (it's not invisible — black bear on yellow leaves is contrasty enough) but she doesn't read its **state** the way a Hunter would. Hunter Expert would see: ears, posture, browsing-vs-hunting, recent-feed-vs-fresh-pursuit. Pell sees: bear, distance, direction.

**Saliency-under-deprivation moment**: Pell is hungry, cold, tired. Her saliency on her **own** domain (shelter sites, fuel sources, water sources) is **slightly enhanced** — she's hyperattuned to anything that solves her immediate problem (any patch of standing-dead jumps out; any leeward hollow registers strongly). Her saliency on **adjacent domains** (predator behavior reading) is **flattened** — the bear is visible but not legible.

**Flag for §14.13 spec — saliency under stress** (the open gap the user flagged):

The current §14.13 spec defines saliency as **skill-modulated** (skill differential → visual saliency differential). It does NOT spec what happens when **body state** modulates saliency. From this run plus Run 02 (Ari's degraded saliency under cold + caloric debt) and Run 03 (Tova's degraded saliency under fever) the pattern is:

- **Hunger / cold / tired** → saliency narrows to **immediate-survival domain**, sharpens within that domain, flattens in adjacent domains
- **Hypothermic** → general saliency drops; things the character would normally see fade
- **Fevered / sick** → saliency dampens generally; perception of camp surroundings degrades

**Concrete proposal**: §14.13 saliency multiplier =
```
salience = base_skill_modulator
         × need_state_modulator(domain, current_body_state)
         × ambient_modulator(weather, time-of-day)
```

Where `need_state_modulator`:
- For domain relevant to the deficit (cold → fire/shelter; hungry → food/calories): **boosted +20%**
- For unrelated domains (cold → bird-id, predator-state-reading): **damped -15-30%**
- Under severe deprivation (stage-2 hypothermia, sepsis, starvation stage 2): **all domains damp**, immediate-survival domain holds longest

This is a coherent extension of §14.13. **Worth folding into the spec** as a tier-2 modulator. Pell's run surfaces it cleanly; Run 02 (Ari) and Run 03 (Tova) confirm the degradation direction; Run 04 (Sam) didn't surface deprivation, so didn't test it.

Pell backs off the ridge without engaging the bear, returns to camp, banks the fire, eats. Her saliency-narrowing helped her stay safe (she didn't approach to investigate the bear) but it also flattened her predator-reading at the moment she most needed it. Honest cost.

### Beat 19 — Day 19 evening, the bear of the meadow (no — different individual)

The bear was clearly **not Camp Stalker behavior** (§13.2 Camp Stalker pathway requires accumulated visits and dependence; this is a one-time observation). Just a wild black bear in a normal fall-berry pattern. Pell logs the observation but takes no defensive action beyond camp-discipline (food cache hung at 4m on a separate branch ~30m downwind).

**Day 19 sleep**: grade B (improvement; rain has stopped, fire reliable, fuel stockpiled).

## Days 20–25 — Camp 5, the snow surprise, snow-shelter

### Beat 20 — Day 20, the move + first frost

Pell breaks Camp 4 Day 20. Walks 2 km north into slightly higher elevation. Reads weather: **temperature dropping**. Day 19's clearing has been followed by a cold front; ambient is now -1°C overnight, 6°C daytime. This is fall transitioning faster than typical.

Camp 5 site: a flat shelf under conifer canopy, ~20m from a spring seep. Sets up a **lean-to with reinforced thatch** — Camp 5 is her cold-fall configuration. She invests an extra hour on the thatch and adds a low rock-pile heat-reflector behind the fire (reflects radiant heat back into the lean-to).

### Beat 21 — Day 22, the snow surprise

Day 22 morning Pell wakes to **3 cm of fresh snow** on her tarp roof. Fall-to-winter transition has arrived early.

**Implications**:
- Ground deadfall now buried — fuel gathering requires kicking snow off, then drying the wood. Cost goes up.
- Water access at spring seep is still liquid (running water resists freezing for now) but the small pond she fished at Camp 3 is likely partially frozen by now.
- Tracking conditions excellent — every animal that crosses her area now leaves prints.
- Cold tolerance is becoming the dominant body-state pressure.

She has **8 days remaining**. She can extract on schedule, or stay if she's confident. She decides to stay through Day 30 — the cold transition is part of what she came to learn.

### Beat 22 — Day 23, snow-shelter experiments

Day 23 Pell experiments with snow-shelter forms — not because she needs one (her lean-to with reflector is performing) but because the design canon needs the comparative data.

**Snow shelter options Pell can build**:

1. **Tree-pit** — dig the snow away from the base of a heavy-conifer tree; the tree's lower branches form a natural roof; the pit floor (often clear of snow due to canopy interception) becomes the sleep platform. Takes 20 minutes. R-value ~10. **Best when**: dense snow but limited time + simple need. Pell builds a sample one on Day 23 just to test — it works; she'd use it as emergency shelter.

2. **Snow trench** — dig a body-length trench 0.5m deep into the snow, roof with branches + canvas + 30cm snow on top. 1 hour. R-value ~15. **Best when**: open snowfield, no trees nearby, single occupant.

3. **Simple quinzhee** (snow-pile-and-hollow shelter) — pile snow 2m high, let it settle 4 hours, dig a chamber inside. 2-3 hours plus settling time. R-value ~20. **Best when**: deep cold extended stay, sufficient snow depth, time available. Pell's current snow is too thin for a full quinzhee.

She doesn't build a full quinzhee — there's not enough snow. She builds the tree-pit + a small snow-trench as practice and notebooks the volumes: *"Quinzhee needs 1m+ depth available. Tree-pit works in any snow at any conifer base. Trench is for treeless open snow — desert/tundra crossover."*

**Cross-reference**: Run 02 (Ari) used a snow-pit at the plane crash. Run 12 (Pell) uses a tree-pit in a forest. Same principle (snow as insulation, body-volume shelter) different execution (open drift vs conifer base).

### Beat 23 — Day 24-25, mobile pattern slowing

Days 24-25 Pell shifts pattern. The 1-3 km moves she did Days 1-19 are slower in snow. She decides Camp 5 will be her last shifted-position; she'll stay 6 days here, Days 25-30, extracting Day 30 by hiking out from this camp.

This is a decision-logic point: **mobile pattern slowed by terrain**. The 1-3 km mobile pace works in dry forest; in 3cm wet snow on a slope, the same 2 km move costs 2× the calories. Mobility's cost-curve isn't linear with terrain.

**§1 No Dominant Strategy fires here** — mobile fieldcraft is its own valid playstyle (avoids predator-claim radius, doesn't accumulate food-cache scent), but it has its costs (no crafted items beyond 50m radius of current camp; every camp is a fresh fire start; weather closes the option). A fixed-base Builder camp would have a deeper food cache, more substantial shelter, more crafted gear — but is locatable, scent-accumulates, attracts Camp Stalkers (§13.2).

Pell on Day 25 is doing what a mobile player does when weather closes: she stops moving and becomes semi-fixed. Note: she did NOT plan this; she **discovered** it via state. This is the design's commitment to emergent decisions.

## Days 26-30 — Camp 5 hold, repair backlog, extract

### Beat 24 — Day 26, repair backlog

By Day 26 Pell has accumulated a small repair backlog she'd been deferring:

1. **Tarp grommet**: one of the tarp corner grommets tore through Day 23 (from snow weight + a yank when she repositioned). She re-grommets by **whip-stitching a small loop of paracord through the existing hole + a fresh reinforcement patch cut from her bandana** (she sacrifices the bandana — a tradeoff: lose face-warmth source for shelter integrity). **20 min, grade B repair.**

2. **Bowdrill bow string** has frayed near the spindle-contact point — she replaces it with a fresh paracord inner-strand twist. **8 min, grade A repair.**

3. **Boot lace** — left boot lace has stretched and stays untied. She **re-tips the lace** by dipping each end in melted pine pitch + char dust mix (the same compound from Day 8 boot seam). Tips harden into reinforced aglets. **5 min, grade A.**

4. **Knife edge**: knife edge has dulled noticeably from 25 days of work. She strops on **sandstone slab from the spring-seep area** (she scouted this stone Day 21 — saliency-flagged: sandstone has grit). Then finishes on her **leather belt** (improvised leather strop). 15 minutes. Edge restored to shaving sharp. **Grade A — knife maintenance is fully within Survivalist domain.**

**Cross-skill repair summary** by end of Day 26:
- Knife handle (Day 6) — grade B, paracord + brass
- Boot seam (Day 8) — grade B, paracord re-stitch + pitch seal
- Multi-tool saw (Day 13) — grade N/A (routed around; functionality preserved without part replacement)
- Pot lid (Day 14) — grade A function, slate from creek (gear improvisation, not strictly repair)
- Tarp grommet (Day 26) — grade B, paracord whip-stitch + bandana patch
- Bowdrill bow string (Day 26) — grade A, paracord inner-strand
- Boot lace tips (Day 26) — grade A, pitch+char aglet
- Knife edge (Day 26) — grade A, sandstone + leather strop

**Repair-vs-replace question**: when does Pell repair vs replace? Pell's notebook entry: *"Repair when (a) replacement materials harder to source than repair materials, (b) replacement takes longer than repair, (c) original retains majority of its function. Replace when (a) repair material exhausted, (b) repair would be grade C or below, (c) original has cascading damage."*

This is a **decision-logic principle worth elevating to design**: repair-vs-replace is governed by material economy, time economy, and quality threshold. Surface this in §14 cross-skill repair spec.

### Beat 25 — Day 27, the knife-vs-new debate

Day 27 Pell sketches in her notebook: *"What would it take to make a NEW knife from scratch in the wild?"* — this is the **repair-vs-replace at the extreme** question.

A new knife in the wild requires:
- Steel stock (Pell has none — she'd be reduced to a chipped-stone knife edge)
- Forge / heat source for tempering (her fire is too low-heat for proper tempering; would yield soft brittle blade)
- Hammer + anvil (none — could improvise from rounded stones, but precision suffers)
- Handle material (abundant; not a constraint)

**Realistic field-built knife** (no steel stock, just stone): a chipped flint or obsidian flake hafted onto an antler or wood handle. Sharp enough to skin a rabbit; useless for chopping; loses edge after a few cuts. **Grade D** "knife" relative to her current full-tang steel.

**Conclusion**: Pell repairs her existing knife (Day 26 edge strop) rather than make a new one. **Replacement is reserved for total loss** — the original is in service throughout the trip. This is the realistic discipline of mobile fieldcraft: take care of the one tool you have; don't pretend you can field-fabricate a replacement.

### Beat 26 — Day 28-29, observation days

Days 28-29 Pell is in **observation mode**. Camp 5 is performing; her repairs are complete; food is stable (foraged + 1 grouse Day 28); weather is cold but clear.

She uses these days to **log compendium-style observations** — fall-to-winter transition signs, deadfall behavior under snow, water-source freeze-line at this elevation, which conifer species hold most needles (good winter fuel signal). This is the **non-active fieldcraft skill**: the deliberate noting + memory-building that turns 30 days of mobile travel into mappable territorial familiarity.

Pell's **mobile pattern goal — building progressive familiarity with the territory** — is achieved through this. By Day 29 she has a usable mental map of three drainages, four creek beds, two ponds, one ridge-line, and approximate animal-sign distribution. If she walked back through this territory in 6 months, she'd know it.

### Beat 27 — Day 30, extract

Pell breaks camp Day 30 morning. Hike out is 9 km back to trailhead, downslope mostly, with a known route. She covers it in 5 hours.

**Body state at extract**:
- Warmth: 80% (good morning, fire-warmed before departure)
- Hunger: 55% (good calorie balance over the run)
- Thirst: 90% (water-treated to capacity)
- Tiredness: 50% (last 6 days were rest-balanced)
- No injury, no infection, no significant deprivation
- Cumulative weight loss: ~2 kg over 30 days (modest — she was eating well enough)

She walks out alive, on schedule, with notebook full of observations.

## Architecture Stress-Test (Run 12)

- ✅ **§1 No Dominant Strategy**: mobile fieldcraft vs fixed-base camp held under pressure. Pell's mobility paid in: no big game (knife-only, no rifle = no apex hunts), constant fire-restart cost, paracord-economy on repairs. Fixed-base would have given her crafted items, bigger food cache, but locatable scent + Camp Stalker risk + cabin-fever-like saturation. Both viable; different costs.
- ✅ **§2 Mechanical Behavior**: every event from real inputs. The rain front, the snow, the bear sighting, the multi-tool saw break, the bowdrill ember loss — all from real conditions, no scripted drama.
- ✅ **§14 cross-skill repair tier**: Pell repairs everything but at one tier below domain specialist. This is the explicit design principle confirmed. **The Survivalist as generalist tier** is paid for in non-mastery on any single domain — and that cost is meaningful (the knife-handle grade-B repair was observably uglier than original; she'd want a Builder to redo it at home).
- ✅ **§14 above-baseline mastery**: Pell's signature ability hypothesis — "field-tested intuition" — surfaced organically. She caught thin spots in leaf mounds, low-quality fuel, bad shelter sites before attempting. Saliency-as-pre-attention. Worth folding into §14.5 Master Survivalist signature ability list.
- ✅ **§14.13 saliency under deprivation**: surfaced as a real modulator. When Pell was cold/wet/tired (Day 19), her saliency narrowed to immediate-survival domain and flattened on adjacent domains (predator reading). **Flagged as §14.13 spec gap**: need_state_modulator should multiply skill_modulator. Both Run 02 (Ari) and Run 03 (Tova) confirm the degradation direction; Run 12 surfaces the modulation cleanly without death.
- ✅ **§15 hit-zone combat — not stressed this run**. Pell carried no rifle, no bow; encountered the bear at non-engagement distance; the absence of a stress on §15 is its own data — knife-and-fieldcraft is a valid no-combat run shape (the Forager game-completion-path constraint per §15.6 generalizes here to a Survivalist who chooses not to combat).
- ✅ **§13.4 Wet Wood**: confirmed extensively. Standing-dead vs ground-fall wood differential under rain. Fuel economy under wet conditions ~2× dry. Pell's foraging strategy (standing-dead preferred) is the right response.
- ✅ **Fuel economy under mobility**: Pell's daily fire is ferro, not bowdrill (efficient method as daily routine, friction as resilience floor). Boil water cost = ~150g fuel/L; mobile pattern = new fire each camp = ~1kg tinder/camp setup. Multiplied across 5 camps + 30 days, fuel economy is a real constraint, and would be the survival-limiting variable if drainage thinned.

## What did NOT end Pell — mechanical reading

A mobile run has different death risks than a fixed-base run. The specific risks for Pell were:

1. **Falls in unfamiliar terrain** — every move into a new drainage means walking on un-mapped ground. Pell mitigated this by moving in clear weather, moving short distances, and stopping when terrain shifted (Day 25 decision to stay through Day 30).
2. **Equipment failure deep in territory** — the multi-tool saw broke at Camp 3 (Day 13), far from extract. Pell's response was workflow re-routing, not crisis. If her knife had broken, the run would have been in real trouble.
3. **Encounter exposure** — Day 19 bear at 120m. Lower-impact than Run 01's cougar pounce (Pell was visible, distant, with cover; bear was not in defensive state). Pell's mitigation: back off without engagement.
4. **Hypothermia from compounding wet days** (Days 15-17) — closest Pell came to a Run-02-style cascade. Mitigated by: she stayed put, ran ferro fires daily, slept in well-pitched A-frame, maintained calorie intake even at reduced level.
5. **Septic injury** (Run-03 risk) — Pell did not cut herself badly. Her knife discipline was professional. She did not fell trees over 4cm diameter (the Run-03 axe-slip scenario was Builder felling 20cm timber; Pell's knife-and-fieldcraft scale stays small).

**What this surfaces about mobile fieldcraft**: it is a **lower-risk-ceiling, lower-reward-ceiling** strategy than fixed-base. Pell doesn't take big game, doesn't build defensible structures, doesn't accumulate stored food beyond a few days. In exchange she doesn't accumulate scent radius, doesn't become locatable, doesn't depend on a single shelter that could fail. **Both strategies have legitimate cost profiles per §1 No Dominant Strategy. The genre supports both.**
