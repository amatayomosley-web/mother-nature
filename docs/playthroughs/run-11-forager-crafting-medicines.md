## Week 1 — botanical survey + the umbel near-miss

### Beat 01 — Day 1, late morning, ecotone reconnaissance

Bo sets the tarp on a flat dry rise at the hardwood-meadow seam — high enough to dry harvested plants in the airflow, close enough to the bog for the wet-loving species, close enough to the meadow for the sun-lovers. **§14.13 saliency** fires immediately and constantly: she walks 200m and her eye catches eleven distinct medicinal species the same walk would yield maybe two for a Novice.

Standing at the meadow edge:
- **Yarrow** — flat white flower-heads on tall stems, fern-cut leaves at the base. Saliency: vivid. She doesn't think "yarrow"; she just sees the plant.
- **Plantain (broadleaf)** — low rosette of ribbed oval leaves at the trail edge. Saliency: vivid.
- **St. John's-wort equivalent** — yellow five-petaled flower, leaves with translucent dots when held to light. Saliency: vivid.
- **Mullein** — silvery woolly leaves in a basal rosette, central flower-spike forming. Saliency: vivid.

At the bog edge:
- **Sphagnum moss** — vast carpets. Useful as a wound dressing absorbent.
- **Cattail** — standing in shallows. Starchy rhizomes (food), pollen (food), young shoots.
- **Skullcap-equivalent** — small blue flowers on the bog margin. Mild sedative infusion.

Hardwood transition:
- **Willow** (a young white willow, ~3m, multi-stem at creek-side) — bark analgesic.
- **Elderberry** — clusters of small dark berries beginning to ripen. Cooked = mild fever-aid; raw = toxic.
- **Comfrey** — large rough leaves at the bog-hardwood seam. **Folkloric bone-knit; modern data says hepatotoxic.** (See §14 multi-axis grading note in Day 14 below.)

She does not harvest today. Survey only. **Notebook entry — "Day 1, ecotone map":** sketched. The notebook is Bo's diegetic compendium-shadow — her player would later see compendium entries promote from Practiced to Expert as she actually harvests, prepares, and uses each plant.

**Systems invoked**: §14.13 saliency (Botanist domain) · §14.6 hybrid perception model · ecotone diversity · §8 compendium tier inheritance

**Gaps surfaced**:

### Beat 02 — Day 2, the parsnip-or-hemlock moment

Mid-morning, west edge of the meadow, Bo finds a stand of **white-flowered umbels** — flat-topped clusters of tiny white flowers on hollow stalks, ~1.2m tall. Three plants growing together. **At Novice plant-ID, all four members of the umbel family (carrot family, Apiaceae) look essentially identical at flowering stage: white umbel, divided leaves, hollow stem. Wild parsnip = food. Cow parsnip = food (with skin care). Angelica = medicinal. Poison hemlock = lethal — Socrates-grade.**

**§14.13 saliency at Expert Botanist** does NOT add a label. It surfaces:
- **Stem character** — Bo's eye catches that two of the three plants have stems with fine downward-pointing hairs; the third has a smooth, purple-blotched stem. The purple-blotched smooth stem is the killer.
- **Leaf-base** — the two safe plants have leaves attaching with a sheath that wraps the stem; the hemlock's leaf-base is more cleanly bracted.
- **Scent halo** — Bo crushes a leaf-edge between thumb and finger of each. Two smell green-anise-carroty. The third smells **mousy / fetid** — the signature alkaloid scent of hemlock. **There is no floating label.** The diegetic cue is the scent shader (an in-world audio-or-text micro-prompt: *"a faint mousy reek"*).

The same three plants to a Novice forager would render as three identical white umbels. The Novice would see "wild carrot, probably edible." A Novice harvesting and eating the purple-blotched one is dead in 2-6 hours.

Bo harvests the two safe plants (cow parsnip young leaves for greens, a few seed heads for later) and **does not touch the hemlock**. She marks its location in her notebook — she may want to come back for the seed (hemlock seed is the right form for the documented sedative use, with the famously narrow margin between sedation and death, §8.3 example).

**Decision point Bo never had**: at no point did the game ask her "are you sure?" There is no confirmation dialog. The saliency cues + the scent prompt + the leaf/stem/base cues are what she has. If she'd been a Novice, she'd have had **less perceptual material** to work with, not a warning popup.

**This is the central botanist-meets-realism probe.** Look-alike risk is the highest-stakes information asymmetry in MN. Expert Botanist saliency is not "the same world plus labels" — it's "the same world plus more of the same visual cues a real expert reads."

**Systems invoked**: §14.13 saliency (look-alike domain) · §1 No Dominant Strategy (Botanist's signature edge is the look-alike read, not a power bonus) · §8.3 specialist-only knowledge unlocks (hemlock-as-sedative is **Expert-only** — a Novice has no idea this use exists, even with a populated compendium)

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N53 — Scent-as-saliency channel (audio mutter or short on-screen text — sparse, ~1/30-60s per §14.13 mutter spec)
- N54 — Per-plant-part stages of identifiability (hemlock is hardest to ID in vegetative stage before flowering; easier mid-flowering; easiest by mature seed-set)
- N55 — Hemlock seed as Expert-Botanist sedative recipe — closes the §8.3 wolfsbane analogue with a forest-biome plant

### Beat 03 — Days 3-5, the survey continues + first harvests

Day 3-5: Bo walks expanding rings around camp. Catalogues. First harvests, all in-window:

- **Yarrow** (flowers + leaves at peak open, dawn after dew evaporates) — ~200g fresh into mesh bag, hung under tarp eave to start drying
- **Plantain** (leaves before the plant flowers; she catches a stand still pre-flower) — ~150g fresh, intended for poultice work where fresh is essential
- **Mullein leaves** (first-year basal rosette — second-year flower-spike is too dry now) — ~80g fresh, for drying then smoke / fumigation
- **St. John's-wort** (flowering tops, midsummer peak is mostly past but this stand is late) — ~120g fresh, for infused oil and tincture

She does **not** harvest:
- **Bark** — wrong season; willow bark harvest window is spring rise of sap. The bark she'd cut now is dry, low in active glycosides, and damages the tree without yield. **Bo's notebook records: "willow bark — return next April."** This is the harvest-timing axis of §14 multi-axis grading firing in the moment.
- **Roots of mullein, burdock, comfrey** — wrong season; root harvest is fall after the plant has pulled sugars and alkaloids down for winter. **Notebook: "roots — late October."**
- **Elderberry** — not yet fully ripe; another 10-14 days

**Off-window cost — §14.3 multi-axis grading on gathered plants**: if Bo had harvested willow bark today, the resulting decoction would grade C — weak analgesic effect, short shelf-life, more waste of material per dose. The "same" item, gathered off-window, **produces an observably worse decoction** (lower concentration on her dropper count, shorter relief duration, no UI label saying "C-grade").

### Beat 04 — Day 6, the drying rack (cross-craft into Forester)

Bo builds her first drying rack. **Forester sub-craft — plant-source woodcraft.** Materials: six straight birch saplings (~2cm diameter, 1.5m long), birch bark strips for lashing, found stones for ground-anchor.

Layout: A-frame, two uprights joined at the top with a horizontal beam, parallel slats lashed perpendicular, ~30cm spacing. Shaded position under the hardwood edge (direct sun bleaches volatiles), upwind of fire (smoke would taint flavor and contaminate non-smoked drying), elevated 60cm off the ground (rodents).

Construction time: **3 hours, Practiced grade B.** A Master Forester would do this in 90 minutes at grade A — better lashings, longer-lived, slats spaced for optimal airflow. Bo's grade B rack works fine for the season but will need re-lashing in week 3.

She loads it: yarrow on slat 1, plantain on slat 2 (she'll use plantain fresh first, dry the remainder), mullein on slat 3.

**Drying timeline at this humidity**: yarrow ~5 days to brittle-dry, mullein ~7 days (woolly leaves hold moisture), plantain ~3 days. **The rack is the first piece of her medicine cabinet's infrastructure.**

**Systems invoked**: §14 cross-craft (Botanist primary, Forester secondary) · §14.3 quality grading on a structure · plant-volatile preservation as a real shelf-life axis

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N58 — Drying rack as a built structure (recipe block — see Design Inventory below)
- N59 — Shade-vs-sun-vs-smoke positioning as ingredient-quality determinant

### Beat 05 — Day 7, the first tincture starts (echinacea-equivalent)

Bo identifies a stand of **purple coneflower-equivalent** at the meadow's drier edge — narrow purple petals, prominent central cone, basal rosette of lance leaves. **Echinacea-class — folkloric immune-stimulant.** Bo's Expert tier flags this plant as: *"reputed immune support; in MN's realist model, mild effect at best; useful as a hedge before known exposure (cold, traveler contact), not a cure for active infection."*

**This is the realist commitment surfacing in the compendium-tier text.** The Expert-tier entry doesn't say "echinacea cures sickness" — it says what it actually does in the design's model: a small hedge, not a fix.

Harvest: top third of the flowering plants + the leaves. **NOT the roots** — root harvest for tincture is autumn, after first frost. She'll come back.

**Tincture recipe (started Day 7):**
1. Chopped flowering tops + leaves, packed loosely into a clean glass jar (she has one she brought, 250ml)
2. Cover entirely with the ethanol from her vial (~150ml committed; this is a real resource cost — ethanol won't be replenishable in-biome without a still she doesn't have)
3. Seal with beeswax-dipped cloth lid
4. Store in dark cool place (under the tarp, in the bedroll bag against earth contact)
5. **Macerate 6 weeks minimum.** Bo will not strain this before Day 49 — which means it will be ready *after* her 30-day arc ends. She's building this for her player's next character or for trade.

**The 6-week wait is the design point.** Tincture is the LONG-shelf, slow-prep medicine. Decoction is fast-prep, short-shelf. **§1 No Dominant Strategy in folk medicine: tincture costs ethanol + time; decoction costs nothing but burns out in a week.** Both are right at different moments.

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N60 — Tincture maceration as a multi-week passive process (in-world time, not real-time)
- N61 — Ethanol as a finite trade-good resource (Bo cannot manufacture more without a still — design call deferred on whether stills are in v1)

## Week 2 — harvest at peak + initial preparations + the traveler

### Beat 06 — Days 8-10, fresh-plant preparations (poultice + infusion)

By Day 8 her plantain harvest is ready for first use as **fresh poultice base** (poultice is the only preparation that demands fresh-not-dry). She crushes a handful of plantain leaves in the mortar (deliberately retains the pestle for **plant-only** — she did not bring it into food prep), adds a few drops of clean water from her filter, presses the mash into a fold of fine-mesh cloth.

She has no wound yet. She makes one poultice as a test prep — applies it briefly to a mosquito bite on her own forearm. **Felt effect**: the itch noticeably eases over 10-15 minutes. **MN's mechanical model**: plantain poultice removes the mild-itch and minor-inflammation debuffs at a small magnitude for several hours. **Concentration**: standard. **Side-effect probability**: low. **Shelf-life**: hours — poultice is single-use.

She also prepares her first **infusion** — chamomile-equivalent flowers from a small stand she found in the meadow. Hot water (not boiling — she lets the pot drop off boil before pouring), 5g of dried flowers, 10-minute steep, strain. Drinks one cup. **Effect**: mild relaxation, fractional improvement in stamina recovery for the next sleep cycle. Modest. Diegetic. No glow.

**§14 multi-axis grading on Bo's chamomile infusion:**
- **Concentration**: standard (Expert Botanist, dose-correct, fresh hot-not-boiling water)
- **Effect duration**: ~4-6 hours
- **Side-effect probability**: very low — chamomile is one of the safest folk preparations
- **Shelf life**: infusion is single-batch, drink within hours

**A Master Botanist's chamomile infusion vs Bo's** — would be roughly the same. Chamomile has a low ceiling. Where Bo's Expert tier matters is in **harder** preparations (tincture maceration timing, salve emulsion stability, look-alike-discriminating harvests) — not in tea.

This is honest: the mastery delta is not uniform across all preparations. A Master's edge is biggest where the preparation has high failure modes.

### Beat 07 — Day 11, the salve

She begins her **comfrey/calendula-class salve.** She does NOT use comfrey for this one. **Reason**: per the design's realist commitment, comfrey contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids — folklore says bone-knit; modern data says hepatotoxic on extended internal use, and even external use has measurable absorption. **In MN's model, a Novice-tier player using comfrey extensively will surface liver-function damage as a slow body-state debuff over weeks.** Bo's Expert-tier compendium entry on comfrey reads: *"folkloric bone-knit and bruise; toxic; surface scrape applications brief and tolerated; deep-wound or chronic use produces hepatic effect. Modern medicine has set this aside; folk use persists."*

She uses **St. John's-wort infused oil + dried yarrow powder + beeswax** instead — for general scrape-and-bruise. (She'll revisit comfrey for a single-use specific application in week 4.)

**Salve recipe:**
1. St. John's-wort infused oil (she started this on Day 5 — flowering tops covered in oil, sun-warmed two weeks; she'll have to compromise and use the partly-infused batch since 2 weeks isn't quite done)
2. Mortar-ground dried yarrow flower (~10g)
3. Beeswax — 25g
4. Gentle heat in dedicated tincture-pot (NOT food-pot), wax melts, plant matter steeped, strained through fine cloth
5. Poured into a small wax-paper cup, cooled
6. Sealed with beeswax-dip rim

**Yield**: one cup, ~40g salve. **Shelf-life**: 6-9 months in cool dark storage. **Use**: scrapes, minor abrasions, sore muscles.

**§14 multi-axis grading on salve:**
- **Concentration**: standard (some volatiles lost to the rushed oil-infusion)
- **Effect duration**: ~3-5 hours per application
- **Side-effect probability**: low (no comfrey)
- **Shelf life**: long

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N62 — Infused oil as a 2-3 week passive process (parallels tincture maceration, but with oil not ethanol)
- N63 — Salve emulsion stability as a §14 grade-A vs grade-C output difference (grade-C salve separates, beeswax pools at top)

### Beat 08 — Day 13, the traveler (intervention point lab)

Mid-day, an unscheduled event. A solo traveler emerges from the woods to the south — a man, late 40s, exhausted, carrying a heavy pack. He calls out from 30m. Bo answers calmly. He approaches.

**He has a deep blister-pop on the side of his ankle from boot-friction, 3 days old, walked through.** It is **red, hot to touch, and the redness extends a thumb-length up his ankle.** Local infection, early stage. Without intervention, this is the **stage-1 → stage-2 → systemic infection** pathway that killed Tova in Run 03.

**He is mechanically at the wound-stage** of the §14.12 cascade-death-chain for sepsis. Bo has been building her pantry for this exact moment.

**Intervention sequence — what Bo does:**

1. **Clean** the wound with boiled-and-cooled water, then with a strong yarrow infusion as wash (mild antibacterial + hemostatic on any re-bleed)
2. **Plantain poultice** as drawing agent. Fresh plantain, mortar-mashed, pressed onto the wound, held with cloth wrap. **Drawing function** = the poultice draws lymph and pus outward, reducing local bacterial load. **Folk-medicine effect in MN's model: delays infection progression by an estimated 2-4 days.** Does NOT cure. Buys time.
3. **Yarrow tincture** (she has a partial bottle from a prior season she brought along — 6-week-aged) — internally, ~15 drops in water, twice a day, for systemic mild antibacterial/immune effect. Again: **delays, does not cure.**
4. **Willow bark decoction** (her stock is dried bark from last spring, brought in pack) — analgesic + mild anti-inflammatory. He drinks one strong cup. Pain noticeably eases over 90 minutes.
5. **Honey, if available** — she has none in pack; in a forest playthrough with bee-tree access this would be the most effective single agent. She notes the gap.

The traveler stays at her camp 4 days. She changes the plantain poultice four times a day. By Day 17, the redness has **receded** to the immediate wound margin. His leg is improving. **MN's mechanical model: drawing-poultice + clean-wound-discipline + good rest + adequate calories delayed the cascade long enough that his own immune system carried the load.**

**§14.12 cascade intervention point firing as designed:**

| Cascade stage | Folk-medicine intervention | Mechanical effect in MN | Carry-the-day? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wound (clean, no infection yet) | clean wash + dry dressing | Removes local debuff. Prevents stage 1. | Yes, if caught early |
| Stage 1 — local infection (redness, warmth, ~3cm) | Plantain poultice + yarrow wash + rest | Delays progression 2-4 days. Buys immune-system time. | Often yes, if patient rests + eats |
| Stage 2 — spreading infection (lymphatic streak, mild fever) | Yarrow tincture internal + plantain poultice + willow analgesic | Delays progression 1-2 days. Slows but does not stop. | Sometimes — if catch + rest + calories align |
| Stage 3 — systemic infection (high fever, chills, confusion) | Same as stage 2, doubled doses | **Marginal effect.** Folk medicine cannot reverse systemic bacterial load. | Almost never |
| Stage 4 — septic shock | None of the above help. | **Zero effect.** Modern antibiotics-or-die. | No |

**This table is the realist commitment in spec form.** Folk medicine has intervention windows. Outside the windows, the cascade closes. Tova in Run 03 hit the cascade with willow-only at stage 2 — too late, too narrow. Bo intervened at stage 1 with full pantry — bought the win.

**The traveler leaves Day 17, ankle visibly healing.** Compendium entry for Bo: *"Stage-1 ankle infection — plantain poultice + yarrow wash + rest cycle held the cascade. Effective when caught early. No certainty of effect if caught late."*

**§14.12 cascade intervention point — Field Note candidate**: this is the kind of mid-arc moment that, if Bo had failed and the traveler had died, would unlock a "Treating infection in the field" field note for her account.

**Systems invoked**: §14.12 cascade-death-chain (intervention side) · §15 hit-zone (wound-as-entry-point for cascade) · §8.3 specialist crafting (poultice is Forager-only craft — a Hunter could read the compendium entry but couldn't make a grade-A poultice from raw plant)

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N64 — Stage-by-stage cascade intervention table (this table needs to live in `docs/chains/medicine-cascade-intervention.md` — see Cross-references)
- N65 — Folk-medicine effect grading is **delay magnitude**, not cure probability. Critical for design honesty.

## Week 3 — advanced preparations (tinctures need weeks) + preservation pantry

### Beat 09 — Days 18-19, the smoking-shed micro-build

Bo has 4kg of trout the traveler gifted her on departure (caught on his way out the day after he left). She needs to preserve it. **Smokehouse** = preservation pantry's main meat method in MN.

She doesn't have a full smokehouse. She improvises a **smoke-tent**:

1. Three sapling tripod over a small smoldering fire-pit (green alder + applewood scraps for cool aromatic smoke)
2. Tarp draped over the tripod, lower hem weighted with stones, top vent left open
3. Fish split butterfly, salt-rubbed (she has ~200g salt from kit), hung on horizontal sticks inside the tent
4. Smoke held cool — flame kept down, smoldering not flaming, 12-15°C above ambient at the fish level
5. **18 hours of tending**, fire fed in small batches, tarp re-positioned with wind shifts

**Output**: dry-smoked fish, shelf-life ~3 weeks in cool dry storage, ~5 weeks if wax-paper-wrapped.

**Where salt comes from in forest biome**: the kit ration. Bo will not find significant salt in the hardwood-bog-meadow region without going to a coastal margin or a salt-lick (deer-attractive mineral seep, rare). **Design point**: salt is the constraint that forces preservation to lean on **smoke + drying** rather than salt-cure in forest. Tundra and coastal scenarios would shift this balance.

**§14 cross-craft**: Forester (tarp structure) + Botanist (knowing alder = clean smoke, pine = resin-tainted, oak = strong) + preservation chain knowledge.

### Beat 10 — Days 20-22, the fermentation crock + the root cellar

Bo digs a **shallow root-cellar pit** ~80cm deep into the cooler shaded north-side soil behind a rock outcrop. Lined with birch bark and a layer of dry moss. Lid: a flat slab of fallen log cut to fit. **Temperature stays 8-12°C** through summer days — significantly cooler than ambient 22-25°C. This is her storage for her tincture jar, her oil-infusion jar, her dried-herb sealed wax-paper packets, and her one fermenting cabbage-equivalent crock.

**Fermentation**: she finds a stand of wild edibles — young dandelion roots and a stand of wild leek-equivalent. She doesn't have cabbage proper, but she **lacto-ferments** what she has:
1. Chopped dandelion root + wild leek bulbs (~600g combined)
2. Salt-rubbed (~3% by weight, ~20g)
3. Packed tight into her single ceramic crock (she brought the crock — 1L, sealed with a wax-paper lid weighted by stone)
4. Brine rises naturally over 24 hours from salt + plant water
5. Bubbles ferment for 7-10 days at root-cellar temperature
6. **Output**: tangy fermented vegetable, vitamin-preserved through winter if needed, gut-supportive

**§14 multi-axis on ferment**: concentration N/A (it's food), shelf-life ~3 months in cool storage, side-effect probability low (Bo's expert salt-ratio prevents off-fermentation), grade A.

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N66 — Root cellar as a built structure (parallel to drying rack — see Design Inventory)
- N67 — Lacto-fermentation as a passive multi-day chain
- N68 — Salt-as-bottleneck-resource in forest biome (drives preservation toward smoke + drying)

### Beat 11 — Days 23-25, the smoke / fumigation prep + the sleep tea

**Smoke / fumigation chain** — dried mugwort-equivalent bundled with dried sage-equivalent into a tight wand, tied with cordage. **Use**: thrown on low embers at night, smoke clears insects from the tent and (folkloric) eases sleep. **MN's model**: mosquito-density debuff removed for ~2 hours after fumigation; mild sleep-quality bonus (~5-10% recovery improvement) if dried mugwort is high-quality.

She also blends a **dried sleep-tea**: skullcap (from the bog margin, dried under tarp eave) + chamomile + a pinch of dried mint. Stores in a wax-paper-sealed packet inside the root cellar. Infusion: 5g in hot water, 7-minute steep, 30 minutes before bedroll.

**Effect in MN**: ~5-15% sleep-quality grade improvement; conditional on adequate warmth, food, shelter (the same conditions §4.3 already gates sleep on). The tea is a multiplier on a successful sleep, not a substitute for shelter.

**§1 No Dominant Strategy check**: the sleep tea doesn't replace a tarp or a fire. It's an at-the-margin improvement. A cold, exposed, hungry player drinking sleep tea still sleeps badly. The tea matters when the rest of the equation is already in line.

### Beat 12 — Days 26-27, the cattail starch

Calorie-dense yields. Bo has been carrying 30 days of food but eaten through ~18 days by Day 27 (she ate hard during the traveler's stay, gave him provisions on departure). She needs to top up on **starches** before the trip home.

She harvests **cattail rhizomes** from the bog shallow — 4kg fresh weight after ~3 hours work (wet, muddy, hard on a back). **Preparation**:
1. Wash + scrape outer skin
2. Crush in mortar, mix with water, let settle
3. Decant cloudy starch-water; let starch settle out
4. Dry the settled starch into cakes on the drying rack
5. **Yield**: ~600g of dry starch flour from 4kg fresh rhizome — about 2200 calories of carrier-grade carbohydrate

She also gathers a smaller bag of **wild parsnip seed-heads** (the safe one from Day 2 — by now she's confirmed the stand multiple times and the seed is mature) for over-winter sowing back home + a few flavor uses.

**Identification risk note**: parsnip seed and hemlock seed are also distinguishable but again only by **scent + texture + pattern**. Bo at Expert reads it. A Novice at this stage of the season would have a coin-flip on which seed is which. **Death by misidentification at the seed stage is the third-highest cause of historical foraging fatality** — MN should reflect this honestly.

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N69 — Cattail starch extraction chain (multi-step preparation)
- N70 — Seed-stage look-alike discrimination (distinct from flowering-stage)

## Architecture Stress-Test (Run 11 full)

### §1 No Dominant Strategy

- **Tincture vs decoction held under pressure.** Tincture needs 6 weeks + ethanol; decoction needs 30 minutes + bark. Bo built both. A player who *only* tinctured would be screwed mid-arc when an acute pain need arose; a player who *only* decocted would have nothing for trade or long-shelf storage. The two preparations occupy different lanes.
- **Botanist's edge is the look-alike read, not raw power.** Bo's Expert tier didn't make her tinctures 10x more potent than a Novice's. It made her **not dead from hemlock on Day 2**. That's the right shape of mastery delta — information-asymmetry, not damage-asymmetry.
- **Folk medicine is not a power-creep weapon.** A Master Botanist with full pantry can intervene at stage 1 of an infection cascade. They cannot reverse stage 3 sepsis. **The realist line held.** No magic potions. ✅

### §2 Mechanical Behavior

- The traveler's wound at Day 13 — not scripted; it was a possible-state in the world (other traveler with a friction-blister-on-an-old-boot)
- Bo's intervention's *success* was mechanical: stage-1 catch + plantain drawing + rest + calories aligned. **It would have failed** at stage 2-3 by the same mechanical logic that lets it succeed at stage 1. The system doesn't know whether Bo's player wants the traveler to live; it knows what the cascade math says given inputs.
- The Day 2 hemlock — not a designer-arranged "teaching moment." The plant was in the meadow because the meadow has the conditions hemlock likes. Bo found it because she was surveying. Her decision was hers; the consequence would have been mechanical. ✅

### §14 Skill Architecture

- **Multi-axis grading on medicines fired** (concentration + duration + side-effect + shelf-life × Master vs Novice). Bo's Expert produced grade-A on harder preparations (salve emulsion, tincture maceration timing, look-alike harvests), grade-A on simple ones too (yarrow drying), grade-B on the rushed oil-infusion + the improvised smoke-tent.
- **Cross-craft into Forester** worked — drying rack, smoke-tent, root cellar all used plant-source woodcraft on top of Botanist primary. No node-purity dogma.
- **Mastery delta is non-uniform** — Bo's edge over a Novice is huge on look-alike-discrimination and salve emulsion, small on chamomile tea. **This is the right asymmetry profile** — mastery matters most where preparation has high failure modes. ✅

### §14.12 Cascade Intervention Points

- The traveler's wound played the cascade-intervention table forward and forward only — confirming that **folk medicine in MN is a delay-the-cascade tool, not a cure-the-cascade tool.**
- Bo intervened at stage 1 → cascade arrested
- Tova in Run 03 intervened at stage 2 with willow alone → cascade slowed briefly, closed
- A hypothetical stage-4 sepsis intervention → **no folk remedy helps; this is the realist commitment in load-bearing form**
- This now needs to land as a chains doc: `docs/chains/medicine-cascade-intervention.md` — Cross-references below. ✅

### §14.13 Saliency

- Plant-ID saliency at three tiers fired cleanly across yarrow, plantain, hemlock, comfrey, the echinacea-class, the chamomile-class, mullein, cattail
- The Day 2 look-alike moment is **the central saliency stress test**: same three plants, no labels, no glow — just stem character, leaf-base, scent halo. Expert reads it. Novice doesn't. **No UI bailout.**
- Scent-as-saliency-channel surfaced as a new channel (N53) — the auditory-or-micro-text equivalent of visual saliency, for plant ID. ✅

### §15 Combat (medicine intervenes in injury cascades)

- The traveler's blister → infection pathway is **the §15 wound-mode entry into §14.12 cascade-death-chain.** Bo's pantry intervened at the right stage to stop the cascade. **The pantry is the §14.12 counterplay surface for §15 wound-mode injuries.** Without the pantry, the wound proceeds along the same curve that killed Tova. ✅

### Diegetic UI

- Notebook is diegetic — Bo writes by hand; pages get water-damp; her notes are HER notes, not the system's compendium back-room
- No floating plant names. No labels above hemlock. **The scent prompt is the closest thing to a diegetic cue, and it reads as a character-mutter not a UI element** (N53)
- Recipes are not menus — they are sequences Bo performs with materials in her hands. The pot, the cloth, the wax-paper, the dropper. ✅

### Realist commitment — where MN must hold the line

This run **specifically pressure-tested** the realist commitment against the magic-potion temptation:

| Folk remedy | Tempting fantasy effect | MN's honest model |
|---|---|---|
| Echinacea-class | "Cures colds" | Mild hedge before known exposure; small magnitude; no effect on active infection |
| Comfrey | "Knits bone, heals deep wounds" | Surface scrape brief use OK; deep or prolonged use → hepatic damage debuff over weeks |
| Willow bark | "Aspirin replacement" | Analgesic + mild anti-inflammatory; delays pain progression; does not cure source |
| Yarrow | "Stops all bleeding" | Hemostatic powder slows bleeding measurably; deep arterial bleed still kills you |
| Plantain poultice | "Antibacterial wonder" | Drawing function delays stage-1 infection 2-4 days; immune system carries the load; not a cure |
| Honey | "Universal antibacterial" | Genuine mild antibacterial; specific honeys (manuka-equivalent) better; still not antibiotics |
| Hemlock seed (specialist-only) | "Sedative with magical wake" | Real sedative; narrow margin between sedation and death; killed Socrates |
| "Magical herbal cure for sepsis" | Does not exist in MN | **The line is here. No folk herb cures stage-3 sepsis. Players will die of infection. That's the game.** |

The dominant strategy here would be a folk-herb-as-antibiotic shortcut. MN must refuse it. Folk medicine in MN is **delay magnitude on a cascade**, not a magnitude-of-cure. ✅

## Design Inventory

### Medicines (recipe blocks)

**Decoction (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: dried root or bark (~10g), water (~500ml), pot
- Process: cold start, bring to simmer, hold 20-30 minutes, strain
- Output: 1-2 doses, drink within 24 hours, refrigerate-equivalent in cool storage
- Shelf-life: hours to a day
- Example: Willow bark decoction (analgesic)

**Infusion (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: dried leaves or flowers (~5g), water (~250ml), pot, lid
- Process: water to just-off-boil, add herb, cover, steep 5-15 minutes, strain
- Output: 1 cup, drink fresh
- Shelf-life: same day
- Example: Chamomile infusion (relaxant)

**Tincture (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: chopped plant (~50g), ethanol ~40% (~150ml), glass jar, wax seal
- Process: pack jar, cover with ethanol, seal, store dark + cool, **macerate 6 weeks minimum**, strain through cloth, bottle in dropper-vial
- Output: ~120ml concentrated tincture, ~15 drops per dose
- Shelf-life: 5+ years sealed
- Example: Echinacea-class tincture (immune hedge); yarrow tincture (internal antibacterial)
- **Cost**: ethanol is a finite trade-resource in forest biome

**Poultice (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: fresh plant (~30g), mortar + pestle, water (drops), cloth
- Process: mortar-mash plant with a few drops water, press mash into cloth fold, apply to wound or sting
- Output: single-use, replace every 2-6 hours
- Shelf-life: hours — fresh-only
- Example: Plantain poultice (drawing) · Yarrow poultice (hemostatic)

**Salve (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: infused oil (~50ml, pre-made 2-3 weeks earlier), dried herb powder (~10g), beeswax (~25g), pot, cloth strainer, wax-paper cup
- Process: gentle heat oil + herb in dedicated pot, add wax until melted, strain, pour into cup, cool, wax-seal rim
- Output: ~40g salve
- Shelf-life: 6-9 months cool dark
- Example: St. John's-wort + yarrow salve (scrape · bruise)

**Smoke / fumigation (general recipe block)**
- Inputs: dried herb (~30g), cordage
- Process: bundle herb into tight wand, tie with cordage, dry-store 1-2 weeks before use
- Use: place on low embers, ~2 hours of smoke per wand
- Shelf-life: 12+ months
- Example: Mugwort + sage wand (insect deterrent · sleep-aid)

### Preservation methods (recipe blocks)

**Drying rack** (built structure — see Forester cross-craft below)
- Use: hang fresh leaves, flowers, roots; ambient airflow + shade; 3-10 days to brittle-dry
- Quality factor: shade vs sun (sun bleaches volatiles), upwind of fire (smoke contaminates), elevated (rodents)

**Smoke-tent / smokehouse** (built structure)
- Use: split-and-salt-rubbed fish or meat; cool smoke 12-18 hours; output dry-smoked, shelf-life 3-5 weeks
- Quality factor: smoke source (alder/applewood clean; pine resinous; oak strong); temperature held cool not hot; tent ventilation

**Salt cure** (process)
- Use: meat or fish, salt-pack ~10-15% by weight, 2-3 days cure, then dry-store
- Forest-biome constraint: salt is from kit ration or trade — no in-biome salt source without coast or deer-lick mineral seep
- Shelf-life: months if salt-cure complete

**Fermentation (lacto)** (process)
- Use: chopped vegetable + salt (~3%), pack into crock, brine rises, ferment 7-10 days at 8-15°C, then cool-store
- Shelf-life: 3-6 months
- Example: Lacto-fermented dandelion root + wild leek

**Wax-seal preservation** (process)
- Use: dried herb in wax-paper packet, beeswax-dip the rim, store cool + dark
- Shelf-life: 9-18 months for most dried herbs; 5+ years for tinctures in wax-sealed glass

### Built structures (Forester cross-craft)

**Drying rack** (recipe block)
- Materials: 6 birch saplings ~2cm × 1.5m, birch bark strips for lashing, ground stones
- Construction: A-frame, two uprights + cross-beam, parallel slats, lashed with bark strips, 30cm slat spacing
- Time: 90 min (Master Forester) / 3 hours (Practiced Forester, Bo) / 6 hours (Novice — Novice lashings slip, slats fall)
- Quality factor: lashing tightness, slat spacing (too tight = no airflow), placement (shade not sun, upwind of fire)
- Shelf-life: 1-2 seasons; re-lashing needed at week 6-8

**Root cellar pit** (recipe block)
- Materials: dig 80cm pit, line with birch bark, top with dry moss, lid of flat log slab
- Site: cool shaded north-side soil, behind rock outcrop ideal (thermal mass)
- Time: 2-3 hours digging
- Temperature: 8-12°C through summer (vs 22-25°C ambient); ~2-5°C through winter (below freezing in deep tundra)
- Use: stores tinctures, oil-infusions, fermenting crocks, dried-herb packets, root vegetables

**Smoke-tent** (improvised — full smokehouse is a Builder-tier structure)
- Materials: 3 saplings tripod, tarp, ground stones, smolder-pit
- Construction: tripod over pit, tarp drape, hem-weight, vent
- Quality factor: tarp tightness, vent control, fuel choice
- Time: 60 min setup + 12-18 hour tending session
- Output: dry-smoked fish or meat at grade B (full smokehouse Builder-grade A)

### Materials

- **Beeswax** (200g start) — sealing salves, sealing wax-paper packets, sealing tincture lids. Renewable only via bee-tree access in-biome.
- **Ethanol** (250ml start) — tincture solvent. Non-renewable without still. Used ~150ml across run. **~100ml left** — Bo's player will need to trade or find a still for next tincture run.
- **Salt** (kit ration) — preservation. Forest-biome constraint: very limited.
- **Wax-paper sheets** (20 start) — wrapping dried herbs. Renewable via beeswax + linen/paper craft chain (deferred).
- **Mesh bags, fine cloth, dropper** — non-consumable.
- **Mortar & pestle, pot** — dedicated to plant work (never food). Tool-wear: low.

### Tool-wear

- Knife: edge dulls after ~150 plant cuttings — needs stropping at week 2 and week 4 (Practiced edge-care, no whetstone needed for plant cuts).
- Pot: low wear, but **stained dark by plant volatiles** — visibly marked as plant-only.
- Mortar & pestle: zero wear at this scale.
- Dropper: **single point of failure**. Bo handles carefully. Breaking it would force her to use a quill or carved-bone substitute at lower dose precision.

## Big takeaways from Run 11

1. **Folk medicine is delay-the-cascade, not cure-the-cascade.** This is the load-bearing realist commitment. The Day 13 intervention worked because stage-1 is *delayable*; stage-3 would not have been. MN must hold this line or it becomes a fantasy-potion game.

2. **The Botanist's edge is information-asymmetry, not damage-asymmetry.** Bo's Expert tier did not 10x her tinctures. It kept her from eating hemlock on Day 2 and let her produce grade-A salve from a 2-week-rushed infused oil that would have separated for a Novice. **This is the right shape of mastery.**

3. **No labels, no glows, no UI bailouts on look-alikes.** §14.13 saliency carried the Day 2 moment on stem-character + leaf-base + scent + saliency-shader alone. The scent prompt (N53) emerged as a new diegetic channel the design owes spec for.

4. **The cascade intervention table is bidirectional.** §14.12 powers both Tova's death (Run 03, no intervention) and Bo's success (this run, full pantry intervention). One mechanism, two outcomes. **This is the architecture working.**

5. **Forager-only arcs work.** §15.6's "Forager must complete an arc without firing a weapon" was proof-by-construction here: 30 days, no kill, no combat, full deliverable. The constraint is honest and the design supports it.

6. **Cross-craft is real and necessary.** Bo is Forager primary + Forester secondary + Survivalist baseline. The drying rack, smoke-tent, and root cellar are not Forager skills — they're Forester. Without cross-craft, Botanist can identify and prepare but cannot store. **The medicine cabinet IS a cross-craft artifact.**

7. **Where MN must hold the line, and where playtest will pressure-test it:**
   - "Why can't I just brew a sepsis cure?" — because folk medicine doesn't do that. The compendium entry says so. The Tova run proves it.
   - "Why doesn't comfrey heal my deep wound?" — because the toxicology is real. Bo's Expert entry surfaces it.
   - "Why is my echinacea tincture not curing my fever?" — because echinacea is a hedge, not a cure. The Expert entry says so in advance.
   - Every one of these is a moment where the design must refuse to bend toward the fantasy. The compendium tier text + the multi-axis grading + the cascade-intervention table are the three load-bearing mechanisms that hold the line.

8. **The pantry is meaningfully tradeable in MP.** Bo's stocked medicine cabinet — yarrow powder, plantain dried, salve, willow decoction kit, sleep tea — is exactly the kind of specialist-only output §8.3 says the Forager produces and other characters can use but cannot make. **The traveler-treatment moment on Day 13 is the prototype for MP Forager-as-medic value exchange.**
