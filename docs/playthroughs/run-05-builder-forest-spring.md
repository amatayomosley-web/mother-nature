# Run 05 — Builder, Forest, Late Spring, 60-Day Cabin

Date of run: 2026-05-10

Run author: William + Claude (design exercise)

## Setup

- **Character**: "Maren" (placeholder name) — Builder primary (Carpenter + Mason nodes); Forager + Survivalist baseline
- **Background**: Mid-30s, ex-trail-crew, mason apprentice background (~3 seasons hauling stone and setting dry-laid retaining walls). Carpenter and Mason both Practiced moving toward Expert; not Master in either.
- **Biome**: Forest, mixed conifer-deciduous; mid-elevation
- **Season**: Late spring (early-May equivalent) — leaf-out underway, snowmelt complete in lowland, mud everywhere, nights still hitting 2–4°C, days 12–18°C
- **Scenario**: 60-day deliberate build. Established edge-of-clearing camp at the conifer-deciduous boundary, ~15m from a year-round stream. Maren walked in over 2 days with a horse-cart cache she's already off-loaded (no horse on site).
- **Time at start**: Mid-morning Day 1

### Starting kit

- Wool layered clothing, oilskin coat, leather boots, brimmed hat, work gloves

No bow. No firearm. Maren's defensive options are diegetic: axe at reach, perimeter discipline, scent management, and the fact that her stationary camp will become a known-quantity to local wildlife — for better and worse.

### Compendium grants at start

- Expert: shelter-construction, foundation-work (stone), notch types, log selection, tool-craft, food-cache structural design
- Practiced: cold-management, fire-craft, knot-craft, basic carpentry-as-craft (joinery, not just framing), mortar/lime-equivalent improvisation, edible spring plants, basic weather signs

### Skill node

Builder primary — Carpenter (felling, hewing, notch-craft, joinery, roof-craft) and Mason (stone selection, dry-stack, drainage, hearth-craft). Forager + Survivalist at baseline (debuffs removed but no above-baseline bonuses).

### Goal

Build a permanent single-room cabin with stone foundation over 60 days. Sequence: clear, fell, mill on site, raise, roof, chink, hearth, door. Survive the build period on stored rations supplemented by foraging. Return in autumn for occupation; cabin must be defensible against winter weather and incidental predator pressure.

## Phase 01 — Site selection and clear (Days 1–4)

### Beat 01 — Day 1, reading the site

Maren walks the clearing edge. The cache she pre-staged sits under tarp ~80m back in the trees. She has roughly a 40×40m area to choose within; outside it the slope steepens north and the ground turns wet south.

**§14.13 saliency at Builder-Expert level** fires on signals a Hunter or Forager would not weight the same way:

- **Drainage** — south-facing slight slope (3–5%) is the sweet spot; pad below the slope-break would pool snowmelt. She sees the slope-break as a discontinuity in vegetation: shrubs change at the contour where water sits.
- **Exposure** — wind history is read in tree-lean. The conifers along the north edge of the clearing have all leaned south over decades — prevailing winds from N/NW. She wants the cabin sited so its long wall takes that wind on its narrow gable, not its broad face.
- **Frost line** — the south-facing aspect gets first sun; frost lifts here weeks earlier than on the north side. Foundation will move less in seasonal heave.
- **Root systems** — three big conifers ring the candidate pad. Their roots will run under whatever she builds. She picks a spot 4m back from the nearest dripline, off the root spread.
- **Deadfall risk** — looks up. One leaning fir 20m east has a clear weight bias and the soil at its base is heaved; it's a candidate for the next windstorm. She moves her pad another 8m further from it.
- **Stream proximity** — 15m for water access is good, but the cabin needs to be above the rare-event flood line. She walks the stream upstream 100m, finds high-water debris caught in low branches at ~+60cm. She sets the pad +1.5m above current stream level. Comfortable margin.
- **Hearth-stone source** — she scans for surface stone within 200m. Finds a small scree fan upstream where the bank cut exposes glacial cobble. Stones from ~20cm to ~60cm. Enough for foundation + hearth without quarrying.

A Hunter would have read the same clearing as "good sightlines into the meadow + downwind from the deer trail." Same world; different cues elevated.

**Pad chosen**: 4×5m footprint (slightly larger than final cabin) at the south-of-center of the clearing, 4m off root-zone, 8m clear of leaning-fir, 18m from stream, +1.5m above flood line.

### Beat 02 — Days 1–2, clearing and stone-picking

Day 1 afternoon: clears the pad of shrub and small understory with hatchet + grub-pick improvised from axe-back. Drag-piles material to a burn site downwind, 30m off. **Pad surface graded by eye** — Builder-Practiced grading via the sand cone (she pours sand on the levelled surface; the shape it takes shows her the high spots).

Day 2: stone-picking. Walks the scree fan with a stoneboat (improvised from drag-poles + tarp) and moves ~30 candidate foundation stones to the pad. Selects on three axes:

- **Bedding face** — at least one flat-ish face per stone, to bear load downward without rocking
- **Frost spall risk** — stones with visible bedding planes or hairline cracks rejected; they'll split when water freezes inside
- **Size sort** — biggest stones for corners, mid-stones for sill courses, small stones for shimming and chinking gaps

Builder-Expert mason intuition reads which stones will sit and which will roll. A novice mason would pick by appearance and discover the rocking-stone problem only when laying.

**Tool-wear note**: stoneboat ropes show fraying after a single day of moving 30 stones. She moves to short-section heavy cordage doubled. Real construction-day wear, the kind that means tools and materials need their own budget.

### Beat 03 — Days 3–4, foundation trench

Maren digs a perimeter trench 40cm wide, ~50cm deep around the cabin footprint. Late spring soil is workable — frost is out down here, even though high country still has it. Below the topsoil, the soil grades to coarser gravel and small cobble at ~30cm. **Builder-Expert reading**: this is good news; well-draining sub-base, no clay lens to trap water.

She digs 5–6 hours each day. **Calorie burn high.** Day-3 rations check: 6 days remaining of carried food. Day 1's forage circuit yielded a handful of fiddleheads and one wild onion patch — supplemental, not a meal. She'll need to expand foraging starting Day 5.

Trench bottom packed flat. Base course of biggest flat-bottomed stones laid in, levelled by eye and the cord-and-plumb. **Builder-Expert dry-stack technique** — each stone selected to mate with the stone below it; smaller wedge stones used to bind, not to fill gaps. The principle: a stone that rocks gets re-set, never shimmed-into-place.

**End Phase 01 (end Day 4):**
- Pad cleared, graded
- ~30 foundation stones moved on site
- Perimeter trench dug
- Base course of foundation set, levelled
- Body grade A, rations 6 days, no incidents
- Saliency observation: bird activity high; small rodents using cleared pad as transit; no predator sign within the 100m perimeter

## Phase 02 — Foundation and stone (Days 5–11)

### Beat 04 — Days 5–7, the mortar decision

Maren faces the first major architecture decision: dry-stone or mortared foundation.

**Dry-stone** is what Builder-Expert mason intuition trains. Stones selected, set, and stabilized purely through gravity and fit. Strengths: no curing time, no material dependency, no failure-by-mortar-cracking, self-healing under minor frost heave (stones can re-settle), repairable forever. Weaknesses: requires Expert skill (which she has), slower per-course than mortared, gaps allow air infiltration to the floor of the cabin.

**Mortared** would need lime mortar. Lime comes from burning limestone or shell, both of which she has not located on site. Cement is not a wilderness material. Clay is available — there's a clay seam in the streambank ~80m up — but clay-only mortar fails in freeze-thaw cycles.

**She picks dry-stone for the foundation proper**, plus clay-and-grass daub later for chinking the log walls above. The foundation will be load-bearing dry-laid stone; the cabin walls will have clay daub for air-seal but not for structure.

This is a **§1 No Dominant Strategy** call: lime mortar would be strictly superior IF she could source it. The cost (not on site, would take a 4–6 day round trip to limestone country, no certainty of finding burnable seams) outweighs the benefit on a 60-day budget. The dry-stone path is right because of *her specific situation*, not because dry-stone is universally better.

**Gap surfaced — G50: Lime / mortar sourcing chain.** What's the actual mechanic for finding burnable limestone? Is there a regional-survey skill that lets the player know "no limestone in this drainage" without walking it? Or is it discovery-only? Current spec doesn't say.

### Beat 05 — Days 5–9, stone course-by-course

She lays foundation across Days 5–9. The 4×5m footprint has 18m of perimeter; she's targeting four courses of stone above the buried base course, finishing at ~80cm above grade. At Builder-Expert dry-stack rate she's averaging ~2.5m of finished course per day. Math: 18m × 4 courses = 72m of finished course; ~29 days at her rate if she did nothing else.

She reduces course count to three courses above grade (60cm finished height above grade) — enough to lift the sill logs above splash zone and snow-pack line. **Saved time: ~10 days. Cost: cabin floor is closer to ground; she'll need to be more careful about ground moisture in the chink-line at the base log.**

**This is where above-baseline mastery delta becomes felt.** Per §14.8 mastery is capped 15–30%. Maren's dry-stack work is grade-A — stones sit, won't rock, gaps are properly bound. A novice mason would produce grade-D foundation: stones rocking, gaps fudged with mud, courses out of plumb. The novice's cabin foundation would heave its first winter and crack the sill-log notches. Mastery delta is observable here as *durability* and *speed*, not magic — the master finishes in ~10 days what a novice would take ~20 days at, and the master's foundation lasts decades while the novice's lasts seasons.

### Beat 06 — Day 8, the bear track

Mid-morning of Day 8, Maren walks to the stream to refill water and sees a bear track in the soft mud of the bank. **Forager-baseline tracking would have seen "a print"; Builder doesn't have predator-reading bonuses.** She squats over it, reads what she can:

- Print is roughly 18cm long, 14cm wide — adult, mid-size, likely black bear (not grizzly-sized)
- Track is sharp-edged in the mud — fresh, probably overnight
- Direction: bear was crossing the stream upstream-to-downstream, moving along its bank

She can read it as "bear present" but cannot read it as "this is the female bear with this year's cubs in this drainage" the way an Expert Hunter would.

Her response is purely Builder-instinct:

- Moves the food cache from her current ground-stash (a tarp-covered pile under a tree) to a hung cache 4m up an adjacent fir, weighted with the cord-and-plumb to keep raccoons off as well
- Burns kitchen scraps daily rather than letting them accumulate
- Identifies a "panic tree" — the nearest climbable fir with branches starting at 3m and continuing up — for emergency tree-escape

She logs the bear in her compendium as "bear sign, this drainage, late spring." **Compendium tier: Novice → Practiced** if she observes the bear directly in a future encounter.

**§14.13 saliency tested honestly here**: she sees the track because tracks-in-mud are graphically obvious; she does not see the bear's *travel pattern* across the area because her predator-reading saliency is baseline. A Hunter on Day 8 would have spotted the rub-stone on a tree 30m off where the bear marks territory; Maren walks past it without noticing.

**Gap surfaced — G51: Saliency-blindness as observable absence.** When a player walks past a thing their skill doesn't surface, the player needs to *not* feel they missed something via UI cue. The track they saw was visible; the rub-stone they didn't see was rendered exactly the same as bark. Honest. But verifying this in playtest matters — over-rendered baseline = no skill differential; under-rendered Expert = the world feels empty for everyone.

### Beat 07 — Days 9–11, foundation complete

Days 9–11: closes the foundation. Last course set Day 11 mid-day. Sill plates — flat-faced stones around the top course — bedded with a thin layer of moss for vibration damping (a real mason technique). The pad inside the foundation footprint is back-filled with the dug-out gravel, graded with the sand cone for slight outward slope, packed by walking on it.

**Foundation done. Grade A. Built honest to her Expert mason skill. Time: 11 days inclusive of pad-clearing.**

**Body state end of Day 11:**
- Warmth, hunger, thirst all healthy; rations 4 days remaining
- Tiredness 60% — physical labor cumulative; she's been working 7-hour days
- Hands rough but no injury
- Saliency check: she's reading the build site fluently now; the stones, the courses, the gravity-loads — all coming naturally

## Phase 03 — Log harvest and milling (Days 12–24)

### Beat 08 — Day 12, log selection criteria

Maren has held off felling until foundation was done — she doesn't want logs lying around for two weeks accumulating moisture and inviting bark beetles. Now she shifts.

**Forest inventory she did during Phase 1–2 routine walks**: roughly 60 candidate trees within 200m, of which ~20 are at the size she wants.

**Builder-Expert log selection criteria** firing automatically:

- **Species** — wants conifer for cabin logs (lower resin-rot rate, straighter trunks, easier to draw the bark). Specifically: she picks fir over pine because fir is less prone to checking (longitudinal cracks) during drying. The deciduous trees on site (birch, maple) she leaves alone — birch she'll harvest for kindling and small handles later.
- **Age** — wants ~40–60 year old trees: old enough for solid heartwood, young enough that the tree wasn't already rotting from the inside. Saliency reads age from bark texture, branch spacing, and trunk taper.
- **Taper** — minimum taper. She rejects the dramatically-tapering trees and the very-young trees with too-pronounced butt-swell. She wants logs that are nearly cylindrical for ~6m of usable length.
- **Defects** — visible spiral grain (twisted bark) is rejected; the log will warp as it dries. Frost-cracks visible as long longitudinal scars are rejected. Conks (shelf fungi) or any visible heartrot indicators reject the tree.
- **Lean direction** — felling decision factors. She wants trees that lean toward an open lay path; trees leaning into other trees mean wedged falls (and per Run 03's axe-slip, that's *the* injury risk).

**§14.13 saliency** at her tier picks ~12 trees as "yes-cut, lay-direction clear." She marks them with a knife-cut at chest height.

A novice Builder would have picked trees by "looks straight," accepted spiral-grain trees, and discovered the warp during the raise. Practiced or Expert mason+carpenter sees the spiral grain in the bark *before* felling. Mastery delta is fewer wasted trees, faster project, fewer surprises.

### Beat 09 — Days 12–18, felling

Maren fells two trees per day at her current skill — felling, limbing, bucking to 5m length, dragging to the build pad with rope-and-skid. Two trees/day is sustainable; trying for three runs her into fatigue-error risk.

**Day 14, a real risk**: a tree she felled hangs up partway down in the canopy of an adjacent maple. **A "widow-maker" — the hung tree can release unpredictably.** Run 03's lesson (Tova) is fresh — Builder injuries are project-ending.

She doesn't try to free the hung tree by climbing or pulling. Instead she fells the maple it's leaning on, controlled, away from her position. Both trees come down. Loses ~90 minutes; gains ~1.5 trees of usable fir + the maple (good for hewing planks later or burning).

**Builder-Expert response**: her competence isn't "no mistakes," it's "good response to mistakes that arise." The novice would have either tried to wrestle the hung tree free (high injury risk) or abandoned it and lost the harvest entirely.

**Day 15: rain.** Spring storm — 8 hours of steady rain. She tarps the felled logs, retreats to camp, fits a hewn drawknife handle she's been wanting. Productive non-felling day.

**Day 18 end**: 14 felled trees stacked at the pad. Sufficient for cabin walls (~28 wall-log slots, two-courses-per-log, ~6m logs cut in half). She needs ~2–3 more for roof rafters and door/window framing.

### Beat 10 — Days 19–20, hewn vs round

**The hewn vs round decision.**

**Round logs** (left as-felled, debarked only): faster prep, all logs use available timber thickness, traditional saddle-notch joinery, gaps between logs must be chinked with daub/moss.

**Hewn logs** (squared on two or four sides with axe/adze/drawknife): much more prep time (~3 hours per log to two-side hew), wastes ~20% of log volume as chips, but produces flat-mating surfaces, tight log-to-log fit, minimal chinking needed, ~30% better thermal performance.

Maren splits the decision:
- **Wall logs (lower 4 courses)** — round, saddle-notch joinery, chinked with moss + clay daub. Time savings: ~12 days vs full-hewn.
- **Sill logs (the bottom-most course on stone foundation)** — hewn on the bottom face only, to mate with the foundation top. Critical for keeping water out of the wall. ~6 hours total hewing.
- **Roof rafters** — left round, debarked, with one notch at the wall plate.

**§14 mastery delta is the discriminator**: an Expert Builder makes this kind of compound trade-off naturally — using the right level of effort for each piece. A novice would either fully hew everything (60-day cabin becomes 90-day cabin) or fully round everything (cabin leaks all winter).

### Beat 11 — Days 21–24, notches

Notch choice per §14.5 master-only signature ability is a real differentiator. Maren is Expert but not Master.

**Notch type options:**

- **Saddle notch** — simplest, log-to-log curve fits a curved groove. Standard cabin technique. Maren-Expert can cut these in ~25 minutes per notch with axe and chisel. Quality grade A — tight fits, no light shows through.
- **Dovetail notch** — interlocking sloped surfaces that lock corner together against pull-apart. Stronger, more weather-shed. Slower: ~45 minutes per notch. Maren-Expert can cut these grade B-A. A novice could not cut dovetails at all — they'd produce gappy approximations that fail.
- **Square notch** — flat-bottomed; simpler than dovetail but weather-traps water. Maren passes on these; they're worse than saddle.
- **Scarf joint** — only used for splicing log lengths, not for corners. She has no need at her log sizes.

**Maren picks saddle notches for the main wall corners**, and a single hewn-and-pegged sill-corner detail (a master-craft technique she has Practiced but not Mastered — she'll get grade-B output, which is *fine* for a sill where the joint sits in stone). She accepts she's not building a Master-class cabin; she's building an Expert-class cabin. The distinction would matter at year 10; at year 1 it doesn't.

**§14.5 signature ability check**: "Master builder — multi-room layout, smoke-vented winter cabin, raised flood-tolerant platform" are gated behind Mastery. Maren is below that gate. Her cabin is a single-room, hearth-vented-by-improvised-hood, ground-pad — solidly built but not a master's signature work. That's honest to her skill tier.

By end of Day 24, all 28 wall-logs have their two notches cut, dry-fit tested against pairs to verify mating. ~7 logs needed minor re-cuts. **Quality grade A on saddle notches, grade B-A on the sill corners. No catastrophic mis-cuts (which would have wasted a log entirely).**

**Body state end of Day 24:**
- Cumulative fatigue 70%, sleeping ~7 hours/night with grade-B-A sleep
- Hands: blister-callus cycle complete; she's hand-toughened
- Rations: gone Day 22. Now on foraging only.

## Phase 04 — The food problem (Days 22–35, overlapping)

### Beat 12 — Day 22, rations end

Maren's 7 days of carried food ended Day 7. She extended via small foraging supplementation through Day 21 — fiddleheads, wild onion, the early shoots of stinging nettle (boiled), a single grouse she got with a stone-throw at close range. But she planned the build assuming foraging would extend her supply, not replace it.

**She is now hunting and foraging for ~2200 cal/day of physical labor — at Forager + Survivalist baseline, not Expert.**

**Spring food math** (per §4.6 — spring is the *hungry gap*):

- New plant growth: increasing but not yet abundant. Fiddleheads peak ~Day 10–25 then over. Cattail roots accessible at the marsh ~600m off. Spring chickweed and dandelion are everywhere — calorie-dense for leaves, but leaves are leaves.
- Fish run: trout starting; salmon-equivalents not yet. She's not a fisherwoman — she's Novice. Saliency at her tier sees "fish in water"; not "where fish hold in current behind rocks." She tries with a hand-line and the bone hook she carved from the grouse leg. Catches 1 small trout in 4 hours of effort.
- Snares: Builder Practiced trap-defenses, not Hunter trapping. She sets 4 snares on visible rabbit runs. Saliency reads the runs but not the *best snare positions*. Hit rate ~0.5 rabbits/day across the 4 snares.
- Bird eggs: nesting season. She finds two clutches in low brush; ~12 eggs total over 3 weeks of opportunistic checking.

**Calorie ledger Days 22–35:**
- Average daily intake from foraging + snares + eggs + occasional fish: ~1400–1700 cal/day
- Daily burn at heavy labor: ~2400–2800 cal/day
- **Daily deficit: ~700–1100 cal**

She is losing ~0.5–0.8 kg/week of body weight. **By Day 35 she's lost ~3 kg.** Not crisis yet, but cumulative.

### Beat 13 — Days 25–28, the trade question

**This is where multiplayer becomes a real design surface, not just an "interaction system."**

Day 26: Maren spots a passing party. Two travelers on the south trail through her clearing, 80m off — a man and a woman, walking a horse-drawn cart with hide bundles and what looks like smoked meat. They wave; she waves back.

She has a real economic decision per §15.6 (Forager game-completion path) and the broader multiplayer architecture (§9.3 jungle rules):

- She has cabin progress visible. Her foundation done, logs stacked, notches cut — anyone passing sees she has *stationary value*, which is both an asset and a vulnerability.
- She has *labor* and *raw materials* she could trade — hewn lumber, mason-quality stone selection, drawknife work.
- They have *food* — smoked meat, which is exactly what she needs.

She walks down. The travelers are Trappers en route to a regional rendezvous, 5 days further on. They're heavily food-stocked because they trapped through winter.

**Trade**: Maren exchanges ~6 hours of drawknife work fitting the trappers' axe-handles + a hewn piece of pine for their cart-bench seat + her stone-mason advice on a hearth they're planning to build at their winter camp + 4kg smoked deer meat and a small bag of dried beans.

Net result: ~9000 cal of food for ~8 hours of labor + advice. *This is what the No Dominant Strategy pillar produces in MP*: a Builder's *capability* is itself currency. She didn't need to hunt; she needed to *build for someone who could hunt*.

**Gap surfaced — G52: Trade interaction mechanics.** What's the actual UI for proposing trade? How does the player communicate "I'll do 6 hours of axe-handle work"? Per §13.1 Forest Signs the design wants templated communication; for multiplayer trade the design owes either gesture/menu or chat. Currently OPEN.

**Need surfaced — N46: Multiplayer trade as Builder's primary economy.** Most playthrough designs assume the player produces their own food. Builder primary node is the first archetype where *trading labor for food* is the actually-correct game-completion path. The design should explicitly support this; without it, Builder is fragile in MP and unviable in solo.

**Solo-play implication**: in solo, this trade beat couldn't happen. A solo Builder at Maren's skill, in spring forest, would face the deficit Maren did at Day 22 with no relief valve. The math says solo Builder needs *either* (a) significant pre-stocked food (~30 days at 2500 cal/day = ~30kg dense food in her carry-in, much more than 7 days she had), (b) a Builder+Forager hybrid skill (currently does not exist as a node), or (c) settling close enough to a settlement/NPC that food trade is possible.

This is real architectural feedback to surface.

### Beat 14 — Days 29–35, second trade + caloric stabilization

Day 32: a single solo passerby — a Hunter, by gear and gait. He's en route to spring fish-camp 2 days NE. He stops, asks about the build, asks if she's seen sign of a particular bear (she has — she shares the track location). He's amused she's building "permanent" rather than nomadic, and offers her two smoked salmon + a fresh hare he just took today, against nothing in particular. She gives him a sketched map of the stone-rich scree fan she found — useful for him later, useless to her once she's got her stones.

This is *Hunter-Forager-Builder reciprocity in action* and an example of jungle-rules-cooperative emergence. Not all multiplayer encounters are trades; some are gift-economies built on relationship/territory. The Hunter knows he'll pass back through here in autumn, and a Builder with a cabin is a *future friend* worth keeping warm to.

**Body state stabilization** through Days 29–35: with the trade meat and supplementary forage, deficit reduces to ~300–500 cal/day. Weight loss slows. By Day 35 she's down ~4 kg total — visible cheekbones, but functional.

## Phase 05 — The raise (Days 36–42)

### Beat 15 — Days 36–39, wall-raising

The cabin walls go up fast once notches are cut and dry-fit verified.

**Single-person raising technique** Maren uses: she ramps logs up the wall face with two parallel skid-poles, rolling each log up to position using a rope-and-pulley anchored to a tree overhead. This is real one-person log-cabin technique. She can put up ~6 logs/day at this pace.

Day 36: course 1 (sill course, hewn-bottom logs set on foundation, levelled, tarred-equivalent (she uses pine resin she collected during Phase 3 from felling-scars) at the contact zone).

Days 37–39: courses 2 through 6. Walls reach ~2.4m high. By end of Day 39 she's at wall-plate height — the topmost log course where roof rafters will rest.

Notch quality at the corners: every saddle notch sits within ~3mm of plumb. Mastery delta visible — a novice's corners would walk out 1–2cm by the time they reach wall-plate, requiring re-cuts mid-raise. Maren's plumb because she set up the cord-and-plumb on Day 36 and re-verified every two courses.

**Day 40**: storm. Wind 50 km/h, rain. She tarps the open-topped wall pen and waits. Loses a day to weather.

### Beat 16 — Days 41–42, roof rafters and the snow load problem

She has 7 round-log rafters cut from the last harvested trees. Pitched-roof decision time.

**Roof options** at her skill tier:

- **Gabled, pitched ~35°** — standard cabin roof. Sheds snow well, gives loft space inside, complex to frame as a solo build (rafters meet at a ridge beam; ridge beam needs supporting upright).
- **Shed-style, pitched ~20° one direction** — much simpler to frame solo. Sheds water but holds snow load worse — risk of collapse in heavy winter snow.
- **Sod / earth roof** — heavy thermal mass, very durable, very heavy (requires structural reinforcement she hasn't built for). She rejects this.

She picks **gabled at 35°**. Solo framing is the real challenge.

She frames gable-end uprights first — vertical posts at each gable end, notched into the wall plate at the bottom and reaching to the ridge height. Then sets a ridge beam between them (a single straight log she'd held in reserve). Then rafters pair-by-pair, notched at the wall plate and at the ridge.

**This is signature-ability adjacent territory.** Builder Master would do this with one of the signature-tier moves (multi-day pre-fit, raise it all in one push). Maren at Expert does it in 2 days, working slowly and verifying each rafter before securing.

**Quality grade A on framing geometry; quality grade B on individual rafter joints** (a few minor gaps where her rafter-to-wall-plate notch wasn't quite tight). The gaps she'll daub during chink phase.

End of Day 42: cabin shape complete. Walls + roof structure standing. No roof skin yet.

## Phase 06 — Roof skin, chinking, hearth (Days 43–52)

### Beat 17 — Days 43–46, roof skin

**Roof skin options:**

- **Split shingle** — split from straight-grained wood (cedar preferred, fir workable). Each shingle ~40cm × 15cm × 2cm. Roof needs ~400 shingles for 4×5m footprint at 35° pitch. Drawknife + froe (improvised from her wedge) to split. Time: ~3 days at her skill.
- **Sod** — strips cut from the meadow, laid grass-down on a waterproof underlayer (birchbark). Heavy. Maren doesn't have full structural margin for sod on a single-thickness rafter system.
- **Thatch** — bundled long grass or reeds. Available materials marginal at this site (no marsh-grass close). Not her primary choice.
- **Bark slabs** — large bark sheets harvested from felling-scrap, laid overlapping. Quick. Less durable than shingle (1–3 years vs 20+ years).

She picks **bark slab underlayer + split shingle overlay**. The bark goes on first as a moisture barrier; the shingles over for durability. ~3.5 days total.

Saliency reads which felled logs left the best bark sheets — bigger logs with intact bark, harvested by careful peel rather than torn-off chunks.

**Day 46 end**: roof is on. The cabin is now weather-tight on top. First proper night sleeping inside, on the ground pad, listening to rain on shingles.

### Beat 18 — Days 47–49, chinking

Chinking is the gap-filling between log courses. Materials: moss + clay daub.

**Moss source**: she collected ~3 large sacks of sphagnum moss during Phase 3 from a small bog 200m north. Sphagnum is excellent for chinking — naturally antimicrobial, swells when wet, compresses without breaking down for years.

**Clay daub**: clay from the streambank seam, mixed with grass cuttings, water, and a small amount of ash (the ash adds slight alkalinity that resists mold). Maren mixes batches as she goes. **Builder-Practiced daub-craft**: she gets the mix consistency right (stiff peanut-butter texture, not soupy, not crumbly).

Technique: moss packed into gap as backing; clay-daub layer over the moss inside and outside the wall. The clay outside gets weathered; she'll renew it annually.

**Quality grade A inside** (her workmanship indoors, all under shelter); **quality grade B outside** (sun and wind during her application caused some surface cracks before fully drying — minor, fixable).

**Gap surfaced — G53: Daub renewal as ongoing maintenance.** Cabin is not "done one and forever" — clay daub needs ~yearly re-application on weather-exposed faces. The game needs to model this so a long-lived character (or returning seasonal Builder) has actual cabin-maintenance tasks. Lost in current spec.

### Beat 19 — Days 50–52, hearth and smoke routing

The hearth is Maren's mason-craft signature.

**Decision: hearth placement, hearth construction, smoke routing.**

- **Hearth placement**: against the west wall (inside), ~50cm from the wall, on a stone-lined floor extension. Off the corner because a corner-hearth wastes radiant heat into one wall; off the center because center-hearth makes the room hard to lay out.
- **Hearth stone**: a single large slab from the scree fan, set on bedded smaller stones, with a low (~30cm) raised rim of mortared-with-clay-daub small stones around the fire footprint. The slab spreads heat to the floor.
- **Smoke routing — the chimney question.**

**Chimney options at her skill tier:**

- **Full chimney (stone stack from hearth through roof to outside)** — full §14.5 signature-ability territory. Requires lime mortar (she has none), advanced mason-craft (she's Expert but not Master). She rejects.
- **Smoke-hole (hole in roof above hearth)** — simplest. Lets rain in. Lets heat escape too quickly. She rejects.
- **Smoke-hood (cantilevered hood above hearth, funneling smoke up and out through a roof-penetrating clay/wood pipe)** — middle ground. She can build it. The hood is wattled (sticks + clay daub) and the smoke-pipe is a hollow log lined with clay daub.

She picks smoke-hood. Construction takes 2.5 days. Final: hood at ~2m above floor, pipe through roof at ~3m above floor, total stack height ~1.5m above roof ridge.

**Fire safety** — this is where Maren's Expert mason intuition matters. The hood's wood components are clay-coated; the pipe's interior is clay-lined; the roof penetration has a stone-flashing collar (a flat stone wrapped around the pipe at the roof line, sealed with clay to the surrounding shingles). She knows what burns and what doesn't. **Builder Expert craftsmanship: the hearth is safe to use on the first lighting; a novice would have built it with the wood near the pipe undefended and lit a roof fire within the first week.**

**§14 mastery delta on hearth-craft**: this is exactly the kind of build where the difference between Practiced and Expert is the difference between a hearth that works and a hearth that burns the cabin down. Master tier would add a true chimney with the lime-mortar-required full stack and dramatically better draft + heat retention. Expert + smoke-hood is "your cabin works"; Master + full chimney is "your cabin is a craftsman's cabin."

### Beat 20 — Day 52, first fire

Day 52 evening: Maren lights the first fire in the hearth.

The smoke draws up through the hood, into the clay-lined pipe, and out the roof. Small puffback into the room initially — Builder-Practiced reading: hood placement is slightly forward of optimal, can be adjusted with a baffle behind the fire. She slides a flat stone behind the fire as a baffle. Smoke draw improves.

She sits in the cabin she built. The fire is warm. The walls are tight. The rain outside doesn't reach her.

She has 8 days remaining of her 60-day budget.

## Phase 07 — Door and window finishing (Days 53–54)

### Beat 21 — Day 53, door

Door frame she'd left open in the wall during the raise — a 90cm × 180cm opening on the south wall, framed with hewn vertical logs (jambs) and a hewn header. She'd planned for this since Day 36.

**Door slab**: split-and-drawknifed planks from her hewn timber, edge-joined with hidden tongues, cross-braced with two horizontal battens. About 6 hours total.

**Hinge material**: she has no metal hinges. Options:

- **Wooden pivot-hinge** — door slab extends past the door opening on top and bottom as pegs; pegs ride in carved sockets in the header and threshold. Old technique. Drawback: the door swings on a single axis through the slab, which means the slab has to be ~30% thicker on the pivot side to bear the swing-stress. She accepts that.
- **Leather strap-hinge** — if she had spare leather. She has small amounts of hide left from preserved meat from the trade, but not enough.
- **Twisted-bark hinge** — bark-rope hinges. Real technique. Less durable but works for season.

She uses wooden pivot-hinges, top and bottom. Door swings inward (so blowing rain doesn't blow it open). Latch is a wooden bar that drops into a slot inside.

**Fit tolerance**: she gets a ~6mm gap around the door perimeter — tight enough to be weatherproof when shut, loose enough to swing freely with seasonal wood movement. Builder-Expert tolerance work. A novice would have built either a stuck-shut door (fit too tight) or a door that drafted air constantly (fit too loose).

### Beat 22 — Day 54, window

She decided early to build one south-facing window. Glass is not available. Options:

- **Greased rawhide pane** — semi-translucent, lets some light, lets some heat through. Has the strap of hide left over from the trade. Workable.
- **Wooden shutter only** — opaque when closed, full-light when open. No insulation when open in winter.
- **Open hole** — only summer-viable.

She builds **rawhide pane + exterior shutter**. The pane is permanent; the shutter swings closed at night and in winter. Best of both modes.

Window is ~50cm × 60cm. South wall. Above hearth (warms the pane). Light filters in.

By end of Day 54, the cabin is functionally complete.

## Architecture Stress-Test — What Held, What Cracked

### What held

- **§1 No Dominant Strategy** held in a different direction than Runs 01–04. The cabin-build path is NOT trivially superior to nomadic foraging — it pays off only across multiple seasons. Maren spent 54 days largely unable to hunt; in winter the cabin pays back its build-cost in not-dying-from-cold and storage-of-preserved-food. A nomadic Forager in the same 54 days could have built a much larger compendium and accumulated lighter caches but would face winter exposure. Different specific costs.
- **§2 Mechanical Behavior** held. The bear track on Day 8 was real-mechanical (a bear walked there), not narrative pacing. The trade encounters emerged from passersby with their own state, not from a Storyteller arranging "now you meet a trader."
- **§14 mastery delta** strongly observable. Foundation quality, notch precision, smoke-hood safety, door tolerance — every phase showed Expert vs novice differential as continuous degradation (a novice's cabin works worse and lasts shorter) rather than catastrophic failure.
- **§14.13 saliency** held across an entirely different domain than Runs 01–04. Builder-Expert reads sites, stones, log defects, notch lines, snow-load implications — none of which a Hunter sees. *The same world*. Validating that the saliency model works for non-combat archetypes.
- **§15 weapon cost matrix** held by negative example. Maren had no bow, no firearm. Her defensive posture was scent management, perimeter discipline, panic-tree identification. The trade-off was sharp: she's more vulnerable to predator surprise; she's less likely to advertise her position via gunshot or attract a Camp Stalker through repeated kills. Different costs.

### What cracked

- **The food cost of Builder primary in solo play is severe.** Maren only survived intact because of trade. Solo-only Builder, in spring forest, at her food carry-in, would have entered serious caloric debt by Day 30. This is real architectural feedback: Builder may be MP-viable but solo-fragile in current spec, *and that's likely intentional* per §1, but the game must surface this honestly during character selection ("Builder is best played in MP or after pre-stocked food at settlement").
- **Saliency-blindness as observable absence** (G51) is structurally honest but needs playtest. The world rendered the same for Maren as for Hunter Sam (Run 04), but Maren's eye landed on different pixels. The thing she missed (bear rub-stones) was actually rendered; she walked past it because her domain doesn't surface it. This is design-correct. But for new players the "what didn't I see" question matters — needs validation.
- **Trade UI is unspecified** (G52). Run 05 implies an MP economy that works; the design has not specified how players propose or close a trade.
- **Annual-maintenance modeling is absent** (G53). A real cabin needs daub renewal, shingle replacement, hearth-pipe cleanout. If the game models cabin durability as a static "is intact / is destroyed" state rather than a degrading state with maintenance opportunities, it loses depth and the Builder's specialist value-over-time.
- **Lime mortar sourcing chain** (G50) is unspecified. Limestone availability is a regional question; current spec doesn't say whether the player can discover it via survey or only by walking. Same shape as G11 (water-system spec) from Run 01.

### Time at each skill tier — discovered estimates

| Phase | Master | Expert (Maren) | Practiced | Novice |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Site selection + clear | 1–2 days | 2 days | 3–4 days | 5–7 days, likely wrong site |
| Foundation (dry-stone) | 5–7 days | 9 days | 14 days, grade C | does not complete viably; abandons or builds without foundation |
| Log harvest + mill | 8–10 days | 13 days | 18 days | 22+ days, many wasted trees |
| Raise (notches + walls) | 4 days | 7 days | 12 days, grade C corners | 18+ days, walks-out corners require re-cuts |

| **Total** | **24–28 days** | **42–43 days** (Maren ran 54 with food disruption) | **66–73 days** | **96+ days** — likely does not finish before winter |

**Implication for §1 No Dominant Strategy in 60-day window**: Novice Builder cannot complete a defensible winter cabin in 60 days. Practiced is on-the-edge. Expert finishes with margin. Master finishes with major margin and at higher quality. **This means the 60-day cabin path is gated behind skill tier**, which is intentional design — the Forager-or-Hunter path doesn't have this gate. Different paths, different prerequisites, no dominant strategy.

## Big takeaways from Run 05

1. **Builder primary is a fundamentally different game from Hunter or Survivalist.** Same world; different domain weights, different time-scales (54 days vs Run 01's 19 days), different threat profile (low predator engagement; high injury risk via tool use), different economy (labor-and-craft vs hunt-and-forage).
2. **The 60-day cabin path validates the §14 architecture's depth.** Mastery delta is observable as time + grade + durability across every sub-phase. Novice doesn't finish; Master finishes with margin; Expert (Maren) finishes with food disruption but completes the goal.
3. **Builder is MP-economy-coupled in a way other archetypes are not.** Solo Builder in spring forest faces caloric deficit Maren only survived because of trade. This is design-correct *if surfaced honestly to the player*. Architectural callback: §1 No Dominant Strategy means Builder is a path, not a worse Hunter — but the path's specific cost is food-economy-dependence.
4. **Saliency model holds for non-combat archetypes.** Validating across Runs 01–04 (hunting / survival domains) was important; this run validates the model for craft-domain skills (stone selection, log defects, frost line reading, drainage). Same shader principle works.
5. **The cabin is a multi-season investment.** Run 05 ends with Maren's cabin standing but unoccupied. The payback is across the following autumn-winter-spring cycle. This is what permanent shelter means — Builder's contribution is not "this run's survival" but "permanent infrastructure that benefits future runs and future players."
6. **No death this run.** Four runs in a row ended in mechanical death (cougar, hypothermia, sepsis, moose trample). This run's mechanics did not produce death because (a) Maren's skill in her domain is high; (b) she avoided combat entirely; (c) she traded for food when math demanded; (d) weather was non-extreme for her phase. **The simulation is not required to produce death; honest mechanics sometimes produce survival.** This is the genre's commitment to fairness from the other direction — death is earned, survival is also earned.
7. **The trade beats matter architecturally.** Two trade encounters (Days 26, 32) were the load-bearing events for survival. Mother Nature has historically been spec'd as a hostile-nature solo game with MP overlay; this run shows MP can be the *primary economy* for some archetypes. The design's commitment to "Builder is a real path" requires this to be supported, not optional.
8. **Tool wear is the long-arc resource constraint.** Maren's boots, cordage, sleeping bag down, drawknife edge all showed real wear over 54 days. Across multiple seasons these become repair-or-replace decisions. The current spec under-models gear durability for long-arc play.
