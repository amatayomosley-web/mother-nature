# Run 15 — Builder + Forager + Survivalist, Tundra, Arctic-Summer Greenhouse Exploration

Date of run: 2026-05-10
Status: 90-day exploratory simulation. **Farming is NOT currently committed to Mother Nature scope.** Third farming run after Run 13 (forest garden, Ren) and Run 14 (desert irrigation, Vasco). This run probes the tundra microclimate-buffer hypothesis: whether a greenhouse can give farming a foothold in a biome where unsheltered cultivation is impossible.

> **EXPLORATORY CAVEAT.** Building on Runs 13 + 14, this run does not re-derive what those runs already surfaced (no Cultivator node → backstory can't substitute; full pressure stack required to preserve §1; +10 system scope cost for the crop side). It tests three new things: (a) does a greenhouse-as-microclimate buffer change the calorie-equation enough to make tundra cultivation viable; (b) does *glass* as a salvaged-tier material open a new construction tier; (c) does 24hr daylight + permafrost soil produce growth dynamics distinct from forest or desert. F-codes continue from Run 13's F26; general gap codes continue from Run 14's N80.

## Phase 1 — Days 1-7, site, frame, the glass-rationing decision

### Beat 01 — Day 1, the layered saliency hit + the glass math

Karuk arrives on the south slope mid-morning of the arctic-summer onset. Ambient ~9°C, light NW wind, sun at ~30° elevation and climbing. He spends the first hour standing still. His three-domain saliency layers produce a read no single-node character would surface:

- **Survivalist-tundra-Expert** reads the **active-layer depth from vegetation cues** — the cottongrass on the lower slope tells him thaw is ~50cm there (saturated below); the lichen mat on the upper slope tells him thaw is ~30cm (drier, more frost-stable). **The float-foundation lesson from Run 06 is in his compendium**: do not dig below the active layer, build on top of it. He picks his greenhouse site at the upper-slope edge where the active layer is shallow and drainage is best.
- **Builder-Practiced (Mason flavor)** reads the **rock outcrop 80m east** as a free quarry (granitic, fracture-plane weathering, suitable for dry-stack foundation footing), and reads the **valley fold 30m E** as a natural windbreak that adds ~2°C to the local microclimate already.
- **Forager-tundra-Practiced** reads three things: a **cottongrass stand** (tubers harvestable later, ~250 cal/kg), a **dwarf willow thicket** (good for wattle weaving and small fuel), and an **arctic blueberry patch** ~40m down-slope (ripening Day 60-75; ~500-800 cal/day during the brief window).

The site selection completes in under two hours. **The triple-domain saliency is doing real work** — a Mason-only Builder (Joren in Run 06) would have read stone + drainage; Karuk reads stone + drainage + active-layer + forage stands + microclimate.

**Then the glass math hits.**

He lays the 5 panes on the ground and stares at them.

- Total glass area: ~0.6m²
- Greenhouse target: 2m × 3m = 6m² footprint, ~2m peak height
- Full-glass roof at the peak alone would need ~5-7m² of glass
- South-wall full glass would need ~5m²
- **He has 0.6m² of glass for what would conventionally need ~10m² of transparent surface.**

The glass is not enough to be the *envelope*. It can only be a *partial aperture*. **The rationing decision becomes the central design choice of the entire build.**

He sits with this for half a day. The options:

- **A. South-wall clerestory**: 5 panes set as a ~50cm × 250cm continuous south-wall window at upper-third of the south wall. Captures the low arctic sun (which never gets very high — max ~40° in midsummer at this latitude). Rest of the structure is opaque: sod walls + sod roof + small entrance. Microclimate: trapped heat via the clerestory band; rest of the envelope is insulator.
- **B. South-facing roof clerestory**: panes set into a south-sloped roof section, capturing higher overhead sun. Pro: more sun-hours/day during the high-sun midsummer weeks. Con: harder to seal against rain; structural risk if any pane fails (roof leak directly onto crops); glass on a slope is hailstorm-vulnerable.
- **C. South-facing wall pane + reflective surfaces**: 5 panes as a south wall section; line the opaque north interior with the heavy tarp + any reflective material to bounce light back. Lower glass-area but better-defended.

He chooses **A with elements of C**: south-wall clerestory band 50cm tall × ~250cm wide, set high on the south wall to catch the low arctic sun angle, with the back wall (north) lined with the heavy tarp shiny-side-in for light reflection. The remaining 5cm of pane width (panes are 30×40, the clerestory band is 250×50 → 5 panes × 40cm wide laid horizontal × 50cm pane height = he has to cut one pane down or accept ~200cm of clerestory + a small 30cm gable insert at the apex).

He has no glass-cutter. **He cannot cut glass.** He accepts the 200cm clerestory + 1 spare pane held in reserve for *replacement-if-one-breaks-during-install-or-storm*.

**This is a different kind of Day-1 finding from Runs 13 and 14.** Ren's day-1 was the labor cost of clearing sod. Vasco's day-1 was the water-budget math against an intermittent spring. Karuk's day-1 is *aperture-as-finite-resource* — he is not bottlenecked by labor or water; he is bottlenecked by a single physical material that, once spent, is spent.

**Gaps surfaced**:
- N81 — **Salvaged-tier construction materials (glass, salvaged wire, plastic pipe from Run 14, structural tarps) as a distinct tier above local-material construction.** Higher capability, lower availability, irreplaceable mid-run. Where does this sit in MN's material economy?
- N82 — **Glass-pane handling as a Glimpsed-tier technique** — Karuk has no skill node that covers glass-cutting, glass-stress-testing, glass-thermal-shock-management. Glass is in the kit, the skill to handle it well is not. *Parallel to Run 14's Glimpsed-agronomy gap on Vasco — the kit-vs-skill mismatch is becoming a recurring shape.*
- F27 — **Aperture rationing as a Builder-Cultivator hybrid design problem.** How much glass per m² of growing area is optimal? In real-world greenhouse engineering this is a known ratio; in MN it would need spec.

### Beat 02 — Days 2-5, the frame

Karuk strips a 2.4m × 3.4m pad of sod (oversize by 20cm in each axis for footing), tamping the gravel pad beneath in the float-foundation pattern from Run 06. **This is Joren's technique imported via Karuk's compendium** — Practiced grade, not Expert, but procedurally correct.

He fells 14 dwarf birch + willow stems (max ~5cm diameter, ~2m tall) over Day 2-3 for the frame. The wood is irregular — these are scrubby tundra trees, not forest lumber. **Carpenter at Practiced level on irregular wood is the same grade-B output Run 06 Joren had on driftwood rafters.** He notches and lashes the frame with cordage:

- 4 corner posts driven through the sod pad onto the gravel footing
- Wall plates at top tying corners together
- 4 rafters sloping from north peak (1.8m) to south eave (1.4m) — south-low to maximize the clerestory's sun-catch geometry
- A central south-wall stud where the clerestory pane band will sit

**The frame is grade B-C.** The wood is too thin for ideal spans; the lashings are right; the geometry is correct. A Carpenter-Expert in milled lumber would build this in 1.5 days. Karuk takes 4 days.

By end of Day 5: frame stands. Walls not yet filled; clerestory aperture not yet glazed.

**Gap surfaced**:
- N83 — **Dwarf-tundra-timber as structural class** — irregular, thin, short, brittle compared to forest lumber. Quality-grading and span-load specs for tundra-wood construction. Extends Run 06 N54 (driftwood economy) with a different limitation profile.

### Beat 03 — Days 6-7, sod walls + the glass install

Days 6-7: sod walls cut and laid. Karuk uses Run 06 Joren's two-layer technique — grass-side-down lower layer, grass-side-up upper layer — packed into the frame's wall cavities. North wall is double-thick sod (45cm); east and west walls are single-thick (25cm); south wall is single-thick sod *below* the clerestory band and the clerestory band itself above.

The glass install on Day 7 is the most technically demanding moment of the run so far. The clerestory band sits at ~1.3m off the floor, set into a frame channel he's carved into the upper south-wall stud + the wall plates. Each pane:

1. Bedded into a groove with hot tar from the fire pot
2. Held by wire ties through small holes in the frame
3. Sealed top and bottom with sealing wax pressed in while warm

**He is operating at Glimpsed tier on a critical-path procedure.** His hands are gentle. He breaks none of the 5 panes during install. **This is partly skill, partly luck** — a Master-Glasswright would have a signature ability that reduced this to procedural; Karuk has procedural reading only.

End Day 7: greenhouse shell stands. 2m × 3m × 1.8m peak. Sod walls, sod roof (single-layer, lighter than Joren's cabin because the structure is shorter-lived). South-wall clerestory band glazed with 5 panes, wax-and-tar sealed, wire-tied. Door is the heavy tarp grommeted to a willow frame, hinged on cordage.

**Body state end of Day 7**:
- Warmth 90% (mid-summer, fine)
- Hunger 60% (drawing on rations + early forage)
- Thirst 75% (stream-haul: 600m round trip; he has hauled 2L every other day to stash at camp)
- Tiredness 65% (heavy week)
- Mosquito tax: **moderate; rising daily** — the bloom is starting

**The greenhouse is built. Nothing has been planted. Karuk has used 7 of 90 days on shell construction alone — comparable to Ren's clearing arc, faster than Vasco's cistern.**

## Phase 2 — Days 8-18, soil, beds, planting

### Beat 04 — Days 8-12, the soil-import problem

Karuk turns to the interior. Tundra soil in the greenhouse footprint is the same thin organic mat over permafrost-cooled mineral that he's been digging through all week. **Permafrost-cooled soil is not just thin — it's cold.** Even with the greenhouse trapping daytime heat (he checks: interior at midday Day 8 reads ~6°C warmer than ambient at floor level, ~10°C warmer at clerestory height), the floor-level soil is still drawing cold from the active-layer ice below. **Plants in cold-soaked soil grow slowly even if the air is warm.**

His Survivalist-Expert reads this immediately. His Forager-Practiced has noted that the only plants growing fast on the slope are the ones rooted in raised tussocks of cottongrass or willow-root mats — *raised* substrate, decoupled from the permafrost. **The lesson is in his compendium**: raised beds aren't an aesthetic choice in tundra. They're a thermal necessity.

He builds two raised beds inside the greenhouse, ~30cm tall, log-framed (more dwarf birch stems) — bed 1 is 80cm × 280cm (south of the clerestory, full-sun), bed 2 is 60cm × 280cm (north, partial-shade). Total growing surface: ~3.9m².

Then the **soil-import problem**:

- The thin organic mat in the greenhouse footprint, scraped from the floor and accumulated from the 20cm margin he cleared, gives him maybe 30cm × 30cm × 100cm worth of soil. Far short of filling beds 30cm × 80cm × 280cm + 30cm × 60cm × 280cm = ~1.2m³ needed.
- **He has to import soil**. The candidate sources:
  - **Peat hummocks** 150m S — abundant peat, but peat is acidic (pH ~4.0-4.5) and nutrient-poor; not crop soil. Lesson from Ren's run F6 — soil pH matters even when MN doesn't model it.
  - **Forage refuse compost** — he has nothing aged; compost takes weeks (Ren F8). He can start a pile, but it won't be ready in time.
  - **Stream-bank silt** — meltwater stream has fine silt at the bend. 600m haul. Modest quantity per trip.
  - **Cottongrass tussock soil** — the raised tussocks are *already raised, already root-bound, already pre-decomposed by years of cycle.* He can cut tussock-tops and import them whole. **This is Forager-Practiced + Survivalist-tundra-Expert saliency firing** — the same kind of "the wash is already a channel" insight Vasco had in Run 14.

He commits to a mix: tussock-top blocks as the bulk fill (~70% of bed volume), peat layered thin between for water retention (15%), stream-bank silt as a mineral amendment (10%), wood ash from his fire as a pH-raiser (5%). Days 8-12 are the import grind:

- Day 8: cuts and hauls ~30 tussock-blocks from upper slope (each ~30 × 30 × 20cm, ~15kg saturated). 3 round trips/hour × 6 hours = ~18 blocks. Backpack haul, no sled.
- Day 9: another 30 blocks. Beds start to fill.
- Day 10: stream-silt haul. 6 trips × 600m round trip × ~10kg per haul = ~60kg silt landed.
- Day 11: peat-block harvest from S hummocks. 8 round trips × ~12kg each = ~95kg peat.
- Day 12: incorporation. He layers and mixes inside the beds, working the tussock-top organic mat to break up the root-bound surface, layering peat and silt, dusting wood ash on top, raking smooth with the hatchet-back.

**End Day 12**: beds filled, surface raked, ~30cm depth, mix grading at C-B because his agronomy at Novice can't tell him whether the pH amendment is enough. The tussock-top mass should be roughly neutral once broken up; the peat will pull pH down; the ash should counter the peat. He's guessing the proportions.

This is the **same shape as Ren's F6 amendment problem in forest soil**, played out in tundra. The substrate types are different (forest acidic loam vs tundra peat-cottongrass), the amendments are different (forest leaf mold + silt vs tundra tussock-top + silt + ash), but **the diagnostic gap is identical: Karuk has no Cultivator skill, so his amendment is procedurally-correct guesswork**.

**Gaps surfaced**:
- F28 — **Permafrost-cooled root-zone temperature as a per-bed property.** Distinct from air temperature. Raised beds decouple; floor-level beds stay cold. Crop growth rate must read soil temp, not air temp.
- F29 — **Tussock-top blocks as a tundra-native raised-bed fill material** — Forager-tundra-Practiced + Survivalist-Expert recipe. *Parallel to Vasco's caliche-mortar (Run 14 N68) — biome-specific Builder/Forager cross-domain technique.*
- F30 — **Soil-pH amendment by wood ash on acidic tundra substrate** — same amendment shape as Ren's forest run (F6), different magnitude profile. Cross-biome pattern: Cultivator would auto-pass; non-Cultivator does procedural guess.

### Beat 05 — Days 13-15, planting + the 24hr daylight question

Day 13: planting. Karuk lays out his seeds.

- Bed 1 (south, full-sun): kale (12 plants), cabbage (6 plants — heavy feeders, more space), turnip (12 plants), radish (a row of 18 for early thinning)
- Bed 2 (north, partial-shade): peas (a row of 16 — peas tolerate light shade and prefer cool roots), fava beans (a row of 10), mustard (8 plants as fast-cycle insurance), small grains — barley (a 60cm × 60cm test patch, ~30 seeds)

**Planting depth and spacing**: his Forager-tundra-Practiced cross-applies for some of this (he knows seed depth conventions; he knows what germination looks like). His agronomy at Novice produces sub-optimal spacing on the cabbages (too tight; he'll have to thin) and the peas (he plants the row too close to the south wall, where the clerestory's noon sun will be hottest and the peas may bolt early).

**The 24hr daylight question** surfaces here. Karuk knows from his Forager-tundra compendium that arctic plants have evolved to handle perpetual daylight in midsummer. His seeds are cold-hardy but not all arctic-adapted:

- **Brassicas (kale, cabbage, mustard, turnip, radish)**: tolerate 20+ hour daylight without bolting prematurely. Will grow *faster* under arctic conditions — possibly 30-40% accelerated cycle from southern-latitude norms.
- **Peas + fava beans**: legumes, generally prefer a dark period. Under 24hr daylight they may flower early without setting good pod, or bolt to flower-then-seed without producing pods worth eating.
- **Barley**: short-day grain; arctic land-races exist and tolerate 24hr daylight; his seed stock is a sub-arctic land-race noted on the packet — should be fine.

**This is the most novel mechanic this run is surfacing — biome × daylight × crop-cycle interaction.** Forest crops in 12-14 hr daylight grow on the cycles Ren observed. Desert crops in 12-13 hr daylight + heat stress grow on Vasco's cycles (or fail to grow inside the run window, in his case). **Tundra greenhouse crops in 22-24 hr daylight grow on a different cycle entirely** — faster for daylight-loving brassicas, problematic for legumes.

Karuk plants the peas anyway, knowing they may underperform. The fava beans go in too. **He is doing what he would do, which is plant the kit he has** — and the cycle-mismatch is exactly the kind of finding this run is supposed to surface.

By Day 15: all seeds in beds. Watered with 8L hauled from stream the prior evening + clerestory's small interior condensation collected from the north tarp wall (where humid air condenses against the cooler surface — a microclimate side-effect Karuk didn't anticipate and now logs).

**Gaps surfaced**:
- F31 — **Crop-cycle modifier for 24hr daylight** — per-species. Brassicas accelerate; legumes bolt; some grains tolerate; some fail.
- F32 — **Greenhouse interior microclimate**: temperature stratification (clerestory band hotter, floor cooler), humidity differential (north wall condenses), aperture-driven solar gain curve through the arctic-summer 80-min day. Distinct from outdoor microclimate; not modeled in MN currently.

### Beat 06 — Days 16-18, the first week of tending

Days 16-18: Karuk establishes the daily rhythm. **The mosquito tax from Run 06 N48 is in full effect** — he works the cool-block 02:00-08:00 and the evening 18:00-22:00, sheltering inside the greenhouse during peak mosquito hours (the bugs concentrate outside but are *fewer inside the greenhouse* — the warm trapped air discourages them; this is an unexpected positive Karuk logs).

Water rhythm: 6L/day hauled from stream (~1.5hr round trip with mosquito tax), 4L of which goes to plants, 2L to himself + cooking + minor cleaning. The clerestory condensation contributes maybe 200ml/day collected — meaningful but not load-bearing.

**Day 17 evening: first germination.** Radishes are up first (Day 4 from seed — fast). Brassicas show by Day 18. He logs the first visible growth. **His Cultivator-saliency would normally start firing here on seedling vigor reads** — but he doesn't have Cultivator. He reads them as "alive and green" and moves on.

**Body state end of Day 18**:
- Warmth 90%
- Hunger 55% (rations down to ~3 days; forage starting — early greens, cottongrass shoots ~150 cal/day available)
- Thirst 75%
- Tiredness 55%
- Mosquito tax: HIGH — losing ~30% of outdoor labor productivity

## Phase 3 — Days 19-50, growth, the bear visit, the storm scare

### Beat 07 — Days 19-30, growth acceleration + the daylight test

Days 19-30 are the growth-explosion phase. The 24hr daylight + greenhouse-trapped heat are working **better than Karuk expected on brassicas.** Kale that would take ~25-30 days to be harvestable as baby leaves in a temperate kitchen garden hits harvest size by Day 23-25 inside the greenhouse. The radish row is ready to thin on Day 22.

The **legume bed (peas + fava)** does what Karuk's compendium warned: peas flower by Day 22 — *before* they have built sufficient leaf-and-root mass to support pods. The flowers are visible but stalks are spindly. He pinches some back to force more leaf growth, but his Novice-Practiced output here is guesswork.

The **barley test patch** germinates strong by Day 16 and is putting on inches per day under the perpetual sun. By Day 30 it is 30cm tall.

**This is the design hypothesis confirming itself partially.** Brassicas in tundra greenhouse = accelerated cycle. The 90-day window starts to look genuinely sufficient for kale, radish, cabbage, mustard, turnip — possibly with succession harvests. **The greenhouse-as-microclimate-buffer IS expanding the growing window meaningfully** — Karuk's Survivalist-tundra-Expert tells him that outside, at this same site, with these same seeds, nothing in the brassica family would have reached harvest. The unsheltered tundra growing window is too short.

Specifically:
- Outside the greenhouse, the realistic mid-summer-only growing window: ~40-50 days between last spring frost and first autumn frost
- Inside the greenhouse: **likely 60-75 days** (the clerestory adds ~10-15 days frost-protection at each end; the trapped midday heat accelerates growth)
- **Net expansion: +20-30 days, or ~50-75% extension of the viable window**

**This is the central finding of Run 15 already, by Day 30.** The microclimate buffer works. It is not magic — it does not eliminate frost, it does not produce tropical conditions. But it shifts the season-edge by enough days to put cold-hardy crops within reach.

The forage parallel continues: cottongrass tubers, early arctic blueberry, lichen tea, a few rock-tripe — ~600-900 cal/day, declining as he depletes the immediate area.

### Beat 08 — Day 33, the caribou herd passes

Pre-dawn Day 33, Karuk wakes to a sound he has not heard at this camp: the muffled thunder of hooves on tundra. He pulls on layers and steps outside. A caribou herd, ~40-60 animals, is passing on the slope ~200m north of his greenhouse, moving E to W along a low-relief contour.

**§14.13 saliency at Survivalist-tundra-Expert** reads the herd:
- Body condition is good (post-calving, on the move to summer feeding grounds)
- Pace is steady, not panicked — no predator pressure on them today
- Wind is from the W; the herd is moving into the wind, which means they don't smell his camp from the lead
- **The herd will not raid the greenhouse**. Caribou eat lichen, sedge, leaves of dwarf willow. They are migrating, not foraging-out. They are not interested in his cabbages.

The herd passes in ~20 minutes. He logs it: the herd's route is ~200m N of the greenhouse, the date Day 33, herd size ~50. **If he had a bow and a Hunter skill node, this would be a year-defining moment** — caribou are *the* tundra calorie bank. He has neither. He stands at the door of the greenhouse and watches them go.

**This is §15.6 Forager game-completion path** in plain operation. Karuk does not have the combat tools to hunt caribou. His path to caloric stability is forage + greenhouse + cache. The caribou are not for him this run.

**Compendium auto-log**: "*caribou herd, ~50 animals, Day 33 pre-dawn, route 200m N of greenhouse, E-to-W, calm pace, good body condition.*" Apex herd movement now noted; if a Hunter character were on this slope next summer in the same window, the route is reproducible.

**Gap surfaced**:
- N84 — **Herd-route memory at Survivalist-Expert tier (vs Hunter-Expert tier)**. Karuk logs the route by date and direction; a Hunter would log individual identification of lead animals, calf counts, body-condition variance across the herd. The Survivalist sees the herd as a system; the Hunter sees individuals within it. Both are observable, both are useful, both are skill-gated. *Extends Run 06 N64 — non-Hunter apex logging fidelity — into herd-scale.*

### Beat 09 — Days 34-42, the bear visit + greenhouse-as-defense

Day 36 morning: brown bear scent on the wind. **Karuk's predator-reading at Practiced** picks it up — a slightly stronger musky note over the regular tundra smells. He doesn't see the bear. He bangs his cookpot once, hard, to broadcast camp presence. Goes about his day.

Day 38 pre-dawn: he wakes to a heavy *crash* somewhere downslope. Steps outside. A young brown bear, ~120kg, is 80m down the slope, having apparently come up from the stream and crossed his scent line. The bear is looking *at the greenhouse*.

**This is the moment the design hypothesis intersects with Run 13's bear visit.** Bear smells the greenhouse — through the sod walls, the brassicas are putting out a faint vegetable-edibility scent on the warm air rising from the clerestory. The bear is investigating.

Karuk's defensive options (echoing Run 06's Builder approach + Run 13's bear-at-the-garden):
- No bow, no rifle. Slingshot + knife + hatchet only.
- The greenhouse is the *food cache* in this run, mechanically. (Per Run 13's F24 finding.)
- Mason hammer signal-display from Run 06? He doesn't have a mason hammer — only a hatchet. But the cookpot works for the percussive signal.

He bangs the cookpot loud against a rock, shouts, holds up the hatchet to look larger. The bear startles, considers, **moves up the slope toward him by ~10m before stopping**. This bear is not running. Karuk's stomach drops.

He keeps banging. He moves laterally to give the bear a clear retreat path E (away from the greenhouse and the stream). After another 30 seconds, the bear breaks toward the E and disappears over the rise.

**Damage assessment**: zero damage. The bear never reached the greenhouse. The visit was probe-and-leave. **But the bear now has a behavioral memory of the location.** Apex individual flag (§5.5) fires: this bear will return.

Karuk spends Days 38-42 hardening the perimeter:
- Builds a clatter-line of cordage with hung pots and willow rattles around the greenhouse perimeter at ~5m radius
- Sets a small smoke fire pit on the upwind side to broadcast camp-presence scent at night
- Reinforces the greenhouse door — the tarp door is the weakest point; he builds a *small inner sod wall* in front of the door that the door folds against from inside, reducing the scent egress and making the door less obvious as an entry point to a curious bear

**The greenhouse-as-defense is asymmetric**:
- The walls are sod, which a bear could push through but probably wouldn't bother with given easier food elsewhere
- The clerestory glass is *the structural vulnerability* — a bear standing on its hind legs could reach the panes at ~1.3m height and bat at them with a paw
- The door is the most likely breach point

**This is the §13.2 Camp Stalker mechanic firing on a *greenhouse* instead of a food cache or garden**. The same shape Ren saw in forest, the same shape Vasco felt with the coyote at his cistern, the same shape Joren saw at his stone cache. **Gap surfaced**:

Pre-dawn Day 64. Karuk wakes to the clatter-line ringing — a single sharp ping, then silence. He grabs hatchet + cookpot. **Decision tree**:
- **A. Build a higher fence / actual barrier**: tundra has thin material — willow stems don't make a strong fence. Sod walls extended around the greenhouse perimeter would be a huge labor cost (days).
- **B. Increase active deterrence**: more smoke fires, fish-oil-rag stink-bombs (no fish, no oil), more aggressive cookpot signaling on patrol.
- **C. Move camp away from the bear's likely return path**: but the greenhouse can't move, it's the whole point.
- **D. Get a bow**: he can't, the kit is the kit.
- **E. Accept the risk + harvest aggressively + cache**: this is the path Ren took with the bear in Run 13.

This is **a meaningful refinement of the cache pattern from Run 06 N63**. There, the structure WAS the defense (stone cabin + stone food-cache). Here, the greenhouse is fragile in one specific axis (the clerestory), so the defense is *redundancy of the critical fragile component, stored separately*. Salvaged-tier materials get their own cache discipline.

**Gap surfaced**:
- N87 — **Bear habituation curve at camp-stalker memory level.** First visit is probe-and-leave; second visit (8 days later) is probe-and-pause; third visit (if it comes) is probe-and-test-the-barrier. The behavioral memory increment is per-visit; the player's deterrence has to escalate just to hold steady. **Extends §13.2 Camp Stalker into a per-encounter learning rate spec.**
- F35 — **Salvaged-material redundancy caching**: the precious-component-stored-separately pattern. Specific to higher-tier materials.

## What ended Karuk — n/a, run alive

Karuk survived Day 90. The plausible failure modes he dodged:

1. **The Day 64 second-bear visit** — the bear's slower retreat suggested habituation. A third visit, especially after the smoke fire had been blown out by the Day 74 storm, could have ended differently. Two broken panes would have crippled the greenhouse's microclimate value.
3. **The 24hr daylight bolt-out on legumes** — failed as expected. If Karuk had committed more bed area to legumes, his calorie return would have been worse. The species-mix luck was Forager-Practiced-good but not Cultivator-Expert-good; a Cultivator would have known to drop legumes entirely.

## Architecture Stress-Test results

### §1 No Dominant Strategy — VERDICT: HELD, with new conditional

§1 held in this run because:
- 7 days of pure construction before any planting (large pre-harvest labor debt)
- Glass-vulnerability requires recurring tarp-deploy storm-prep cost (continuous attention tax)
- Bear pressure escalating with each visit (predator pressure dimension)
- 24hr daylight breaks legume bed (~30% of growing surface in this run's plan was sub-optimal)
- Microclimate buffer is real but not magic (+10-15 days each end of window, not +30)
- Harvest is ~5-6 days of reserve calories for 90 days of work, with forage carrying most of his intake
- Salvaged-tier materials (the 5 glass panes) are biome-distribution-gated (where does glass come from? wreckage only — see Q3)

**But there is a new conditional surfacing**: **the microclimate buffer is *more impactful in tundra than the comparable infrastructure was in forest or desert*.** In forest, Ren's garden delivered 13% of intake at high labor cost. In desert, Vasco's project delivered 0% at high labor cost. In tundra, Karuk's greenhouse delivered 25% at comparable labor cost. **The microclimate buffer is doing real work that the unsheltered alternatives cannot do.**

This means: **tundra greenhouse farming is the *most efficient* of the three farming approaches surfaced so far**, by the calorie-per-labor-hour metric. **§1 demands that this not become dominant**. The pressures that keep it from dominating are: glass availability (irreplaceable, salvage-only), seasonal window (90 days is barely enough; 60 days would not be), Cultivator-skill-absence (legume failure, sub-optimal spacing), bear pressure escalating with success (Camp Stalker memory), storm vulnerability.

**The §1 verdict is**: HELD, but the dominance test is *narrower* in tundra than in forest or desert. Without the glass-rarity constraint specifically, tundra greenhouse farming could become the optimal play strategy for the biome. **Glass-as-salvage-only material is therefore load-bearing for §1 preservation.** A different design choice (glass craftable from local sand + fuel) would collapse §1 here.

### §2 Mechanical Behavior — VERDICT: HELD

Every event emerged from inputs:
- The bear came because the greenhouse trapped vegetable-scent on rising warm air through the clerestory
- The storm came because the weather grid produced a front
- The legumes flowered early because 24hr daylight hit their cycle clock
- The cabbages headed-out because brassicas accelerate under perpetual light + warm microclimate
- The clerestory condensation surfaced because warm humid interior air met the cooler N tarp wall
- The root cellar worked because the active layer is naturally refrigerated by the underlying permafrost

**No scripted moments. §2 holds.** The risk to §2 here: the greenhouse interior microclimate is currently *not* a modeled MN system. Implementing it would require per-structure interior atmospheric state coupled to the §3.3 weather grid + structure-thermal-properties + aperture-driven solar gain. Non-trivial but composable.

### §14 Skill Architecture — VERDICT: UNRESOLVED, same shape as Run 13

**Karuk had no Cultivator skill.** Same finding as Run 13 (Ren) and Run 14 (Vasco). His Builder + Forager + Survivalist Expert tier produced excellent output on the *infrastructure* side (greenhouse construction, site selection, microclimate read, root cellar) and **sub-optimal output on the *crop* side** (legume bed failure, spacing errors on cabbages, no pest diagnostic firing because he had no Cultivator-saliency).

The pattern across all three farming runs is now clear:
- **Builder/Forager/Survivalist character with farming-adjacent backstory can build the infrastructure** (Ren's wattle fence, Vasco's cistern + channel, Karuk's greenhouse + raised beds + root cellar).
- **The same character cannot do the crop side well** without a Cultivator/Agronomy/Botanist skill node.
- **The kit can include the materials** (Ren's seeds, Vasco's seeds, Karuk's seeds + glass + wire + wax) but **the kit cannot include the skill** — that's the §14 architecture saying backstory ≠ mechanical capability.

**The §14 question for farming is the same as it was after Runs 13 and 14**: either a Cultivator node is added, or farming is permanently played at Novice-Practiced grade on the crop side, with all the sub-optimality that implies.

### §14.13 Saliency — VERDICT: HELD with new sub-domain surfacing

Karuk's three-domain saliency layered correctly:
- Survivalist-tundra-Expert read active-layer depth, snowpack signs, frost-onset signs, autumn warning signs, herd-route reading, predator-scent reading
- Builder-Practiced read stone quarry, drainage, frame structure
- Forager-Practiced read tundra plant species, blueberry patch ripening, cottongrass tuber availability

**What did NOT fire**: Cultivator-saliency. Karuk could not visually distinguish vigorous vs leggy brassicas, could not catch early-stage pest signs (no pest pressure materialized this run, but if it had, he would have missed early indicators), could not read seedling-stage stress signs other than wilt.

**A new sub-domain that surfaced**: **Glasswright-saliency** — the absence of it cost Karuk. He could not read pane stress, could not predict thermal-shock failure points, did not know where to expect the first crack to appear after temperature swings. Glimpsed-tier reading on glass produced a blind spot in his risk model. **This may not need its own skill node**, but it does need to be in some other node's compendium (perhaps Builder-Expert or a Salvage-Wright sub-node).

### Permadeath compatibility — VERDICT: COMPATIBLE, AND SHARPER THAN PRIOR RUNS

If Karuk had died on Day 50 of the bear visit on Day 38 or 64, the greenhouse and all the standing crops would have been destroyed within weeks — bear would have broken into the greenhouse, harvest would be lost, glass panes would have been left where they fell and likely been broken by the next storm. **This is exactly the cost-of-death cascade that §6 permadeath promises.**

Specifically sharper than Run 13 because: the greenhouse + 5 glass panes is a **higher-value abandoned structure** than Ren's garden. A surviving multiplayer character who finds an intact tundra greenhouse on a south slope has discovered something that took 90 days to build with salvage-only materials. **This is a meaningful MP encounter shape** — abandoned greenhouses are *worth* exploring, salvaging, refitting.

Field Notes (§14.12) entry shape for "killed by bear at greenhouse" or "starved waiting for late-summer harvest" or "frozen overnight when sod-roof slumped" would all be earned.

## EXPLORATORY DESIGN QUESTIONS

The run was set up to test six questions. Each is answered against run evidence.

### Q1 — Does greenhouse-as-STRUCTURE + crops-as-SYSTEM cleanly split, or are they irreducibly coupled?

**Run evidence**: Karuk's Builder-Practiced built the greenhouse competently (sod walls, frame, clerestory install). His Forager + Survivalist read the site, microclimate, and forage well. **The structure side mapped cleanly to existing Builder + Survivalist + cross-domain compendium.** No new skill node was required for the *structure* — just new recipes (glass-bedding, tussock-top soil import, active-layer root cellar).

But the *interaction* of structure-and-crops produced findings that don't sit cleanly in either domain:
- Aperture rationing decisions (F27) require thinking about *how much sun* the crops need vs *how much glass* you have. That's a Cultivator-Builder hybrid problem.
- The microclimate buffer's value (+10-15 days each end of the window) depends on *which crop you're growing* — accelerated brassica cycle doesn't help if you planted only legumes.
- Greenhouse interior conditions (F32) affect *which species can be grown there* — high humidity could promote disease (not modeled this run but a future concern); 24hr light kills legumes.

**Answer leaning PARTIAL SPLIT**: the structure splits cleanly (Builder + tundra cross-domain Survivalist + new salvaged-tier recipes), but the *design* of the greenhouse depends on what's being grown. **A Cultivator skill node would carry the crop knowledge that informs greenhouse design choices.** Builder-only farming = build a generic greenhouse and hope; Builder + Cultivator co-design = optimize the aperture, microclimate target, bed depth, ventilation for specific crops.

### Q2 — Does the greenhouse-as-microclimate-buffer break §1 in a biome where unsheltered farming is impossible?

**Run evidence**: Karuk delivered 25% of his caloric intake from the greenhouse — *better than Ren's 13% in forest, dramatically better than Vasco's 0% in desert*. **The greenhouse is more efficient per labor hour than the comparison alternatives** because the microclimate buffer extends the otherwise-impossible window.

**But §1 holds because**:
- 7 days of construction before planting (large pre-harvest labor debt)
- Glass-tarp-storm-prep is recurring labor cost
- Bear visits escalate with each repeat (predator pressure)
- 24hr daylight kills ~30% of planted species (legumes)
- Single broken pane mid-season = major run setback
- Salvaged-tier glass is single-source (wreckage only)
- Stream depletion + autumn frost narrow the window further
- Solo character's calorie balance is still net-balanced-to-slightly-negative; forage + small-game carries 75%

**Answer leaning YES, §1 holds conditionally** — but the conditional is sharper than in forest farming. **The glass-as-salvage-only constraint is load-bearing.** If MN ever allows local-craft glass (sand + fuel kiln), tundra greenhouse farming becomes the dominant tundra strategy. The salvage-only design choice is therefore not a flavor decision — it is the §1 enforcement mechanism for the entire tundra greenhouse archetype.

### Q3 — What scope-creep does this run add over Runs 13 + 14?

**New scope items surfaced**:
- **Glass as material with full physics**: thermal shock, hail vulnerability, bedding requirements, salvage-only distribution rules
- **Salvaged-tier material economy**: where does salvage spawn? Per-biome wreckage distribution? Player-economy implications in MP?
- **Greenhouse as new structure archetype**: aperture spec, microclimate interior model, structural-vulnerability rules
- **24hr daylight crop-cycle modifier**: per-species, per-biome — needs spec
- **Permafrost-cooled root-zone temperature**: distinct from air temperature; affects growth rate
- **Active-layer root cellar**: seasonal storage with a freeze-out date
- **Bear habituation curve (N87)**: per-visit Camp Stalker memory increment

**Compared to Run 13's F1-F26 + Run 14's N65-N80**, this run adds 7 new general gaps + 10 new farming-specific gaps = **17 new spec items**. **Cumulative across all three farming runs: 26 (Run 13) + 16 (Run 14) + 17 (Run 15) = ~59 farming-related spec items**, before any system design pass.

**The scope-cost picture**: farming with tundra greenhouse extension is *not just farming + a greenhouse*. It is farming + greenhouse + salvaged-tier-economy + 24hr-daylight-crop-modifier + permafrost-soil-temp-model + microclimate-interior-atmospheric-model + bear-habituation-curve. Several of these (permafrost-soil-temp, 24hr-daylight) are *only* needed for tundra; they don't generalize to forest or desert. **The scope cost per biome is non-linear**: each new biome adds its own farming-blocker stack.

### Q4 — Does this open a "salvaged-tech tier" of construction?

**Run evidence**: yes, clearly. Glass + wire + sealing wax + tar functioned as a distinct material tier with these properties:
- **Higher capability** than local materials (transparency, structural ties, thermal insulation)
- **Lower availability** (single-source from wreckage)
- **Irreplaceable mid-run** (no local production)
- **Requires cache discipline** (redundancy storage; storm protection)
- **Skill at Glimpsed tier only** for most characters (no Glasswright node in §14)

**Answer leaning YES** — salvaged-tier as a material category is real, useful, and surfaces here in tundra precisely because the local material base is so thin that salvaged tech becomes load-bearing. **The design choice this opens**: do other biomes have their own salvaged-tier capabilities? (Desert: salvaged HDPE pipe in Run 14. Forest: ???). Per-biome wreckage tables would need design.

**Risk**: a salvaged-tier tier risks becoming a *progression mechanic* — accumulate salvage = unlock higher construction tier. That would violate §14's debuff-removal principle (which says progression is removing impairments, not gaining bonuses). **The fix**: salvaged-tier must be *capability that the player still has to operate at Glimpsed skill tier on*. Karuk's glass install was procedural-guess; his wax-and-tar seal could have failed; his luck held. The salvaged-tech-tier exists, but using it well requires skill the kit doesn't grant. This preserves §14.

### Q5 — How does tundra greenhouse farming compare to forest garden (Run 13) and desert irrigation (Run 14)?

**Per-run summary**:

| Metric | Forest (Ren) | Desert (Vasco) | Tundra (Karuk) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Days to first harvest | 36 | n/a (no harvest) | 50 |
| Total garden calories produced | ~19,500 | 0 | ~6,500 |
| % of total caloric intake | 13% | 0% | 25% |
| Pre-planting infrastructure days | ~14 (clear + amend) | ~18 (cistern + channel) | ~12 (greenhouse + beds + soil import) |
| Run total calories produced per pre-planting day | ~1,393 | 0 | ~542 |
| Run total calories produced per total day | ~217 | 0 | ~72 |
| Cultivator gap impact | high (Three Sisters timing, pest diagnosis) | catastrophic (no harvest, flooded out) | medium (legume bolt-out, spacing) |
| Microclimate buffer | none (open field) | partial (channel-shade, plot-shade rags) | substantial (greenhouse +10-15 days each window edge) |
| §1 violation risk | low (forage 87% efficient) | very low (project failed) | **moderate-low** (greenhouse is more efficient per labor day than forest garden) |
| Materials needed | local only | irrigation pipe (salvage) | glass + wire + wax + tar (salvage-heavy) |

**Viability ranking**:
1. **Tundra greenhouse — most viable per-labor-hour** when conditions hold (microclimate buffer works; cold-hardy crops accelerate; structure does double duty as preservation drying space). Most fragile to single-event failure (broken pane, bear breach).
2. **Forest garden — most viable per absolute calorie return** but largest open-air pressure exposure (deer, bear, drought, slugs, blight). Lowest skill-gate (Forager-Practiced covers a lot of the diagnosis at low grade).
3. **Desert irrigation — least viable in solo run window**. 100-day project in 60-day window means *no harvest possible*. Multiplayer might shift the calculus.

**What each suggests about MN's possible farming commitment**:
- **Tundra greenhouse** suggests farming-as-microclimate-engineering is a viable mechanic: the design hypothesis (buffer expands window) is confirmed mechanically. Salvaged-tech materials become a load-bearing element of biome-specific scope.
- **Forest garden** suggests farming-as-open-air-plot is the highest-output option in the gentle biome but exposes the largest pressure-surface for wildlife / weather / disease.
- **Desert irrigation** suggests that infrastructure-heavy biomes turn farming into a *multi-season* commitment that solo runs can't complete.

**The collective recommendation**: if MN commits to farming, **tundra greenhouse + forest garden are the two most plausible play-loop archetypes**; desert is for *infrastructure-as-survival* (water-only, per Run 14's recommendation). Each archetype has its own scope cost; the trifecta cost is the largest scope addition in the design.

### Q6 — Storm risk and glass-pane vulnerability — what's the recovery path?

**Run evidence**: Karuk dodged the hail event. The Day 74 pellet-rain came close. If actual hail had hit:
- One pane breaking mid-season: spare pane installed (Karuk has one in cache). ~3 hours labor to remove broken pane, re-bed wire, re-seal. Crop interior cools immediately during install; if storm continues, install in storm = wet glass + cold hands + high break-risk on the replacement.
- Two panes broken: no spare. Karuk would have to either patch the gap with tarp + cordage (microclimate buffer significantly reduced; brassicas may slow / stop) or abandon the clerestory band entirely.
- Three+ panes broken: greenhouse is no longer microclimate-buffered. Crops will fail at first frost. Karuk would be left with ~10 days of remaining unharvested vegetable that he must aggressively harvest immediately + abandon the structure as a true greenhouse (could still use it as a sod hut for shelter).

**Recovery path**: depends on glass redundancy + cache discipline. Karuk's pattern of storing the spare separately, then storing all panes interior on departure, is the recovery mechanism. **The mechanic this surfaces**: salvaged-tier components need not just *use* logic but *protection* and *redundancy* logic. F35 (salvaged-material redundancy caching) is the design pattern.

**Catastrophic failure mode**: a major storm with hail PLUS a bear visit mid-storm could destroy the greenhouse entirely. This would be a cascade event analogous to Run 14's flash flood — a single bad-weather event eating the run's infrastructure work. **The §2 mechanical compatibility holds** — the storm + bear are not scripted; they could converge mechanically — but the cost is large.

## What Run 15 delivered

1. **The greenhouse-as-microclimate-buffer hypothesis was tested and CONFIRMED, conditionally.** Microclimate buffer extends growing window by ~+10-15 days each end of the season. Cold-hardy brassicas accelerate under 24hr daylight + trapped warmth. Greenhouse delivered 25% of caloric intake — better than Ren's forest 13% and dramatically better than Vasco's desert 0%.
2. **Tundra greenhouse farming is the *most efficient* of the three farming archetypes surfaced**, by calorie-per-labor-hour. **This makes §1 enforcement *sharper* in tundra**: glass-as-salvage-only is the load-bearing dominance-preventer. The salvage constraint is therefore not flavor — it is mechanism.
3. **Salvaged-tier materials surfaced as a coherent material category** (glass + wire + sealing wax + tar). Implications: per-biome wreckage tables, cache discipline + redundancy logic, Glimpsed-tier skill spec for unfamiliar tech, salvage-as-material-not-as-progression.
4. **17 new gaps** (N81-N87 + F27-F36) surfaced — most importantly F27 (aperture rationing), F28 (permafrost root-zone temp), F31 (24hr-daylight crop modifier), F32 (greenhouse interior microclimate), N81 (salvaged-tier material economy), N87 (bear habituation curve).
5. **The Cultivator-skill-node absence cost Karuk** (legume bolt-out, spacing errors, no early pest diagnostic). **Same finding as Runs 13 and 14.** The §14 architecture is unanimous across three farming runs: either Cultivator gets added, or farming is permanently played at Novice-Practiced grade on the crop side.
6. **24hr daylight is a new biome-mechanic that surfaces here for the first time as a *farming* variable**, distinct from its already-modeled effect on sleep + work cadence. Brassicas accelerate; legumes bolt; per-species cycle modifier needed.
7. **Active-layer root cellar surfaced as a tundra-specific seasonal storage technique** — free refrigeration in summer, freeze-locked in winter. Small but real.
8. **Bear habituation curve (N87)** extends §13.2: per-visit memory increment with escalating boldness if the player doesn't escalate deterrence. Camp Stalker becomes a real per-encounter learning rate, not a single flag.
9. **Cross-biome farming comparison**: tundra greenhouse > forest garden > desert irrigation in viability per labor hour, with the caveat that *each opens a different shape of play*. Forest is the highest-output. Desert is the most-infrastructure. Tundra is the most-efficient-but-most-fragile.
10. **Run did not produce a death.** It did produce a clear scope-decision input: tundra greenhouse farming is *the* most plausible single-biome farming commitment for MN, *if* farming ever ships. The case for ship-tundra-greenhouse-only is the strongest single-biome case across the three runs.

**The decision on MN's farming commitment, after three exploratory runs, is sharpened to three real options**: ship-full-farming (large scope), ship-tundra-greenhouse-only (smaller scope, strongest viability), or defer-farming-but-ship-Earthworks + salvaged-tier + greenhouse-as-shelter-and-drying (lowest scope, captures diegetic feel without committing to crops). The choice is now well-informed by the comparative evidence; what remains is a scope-and-design-time judgment call.
